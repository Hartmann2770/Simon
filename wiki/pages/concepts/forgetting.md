## Definition

Forgetting is the decline over time in the ability to retrieve previously learned information. The forgetting curve — associated with Hermann Ebbinghaus's nineteenth-century self-experiments — describes the characteristic negatively decelerating function relating retention to time since learning: rapid loss early, slower loss later. Forgetting is not erasure of memory: modern accounts distinguish between *retrieval failure* (the memory exists but cannot currently be accessed) and *storage failure* (the memory has genuinely degraded).

## Key Principles

1. **Forgetting as retrieval failure, not erasure.** The New Theory of Disuse ([Bjork & Bjork, 1992](../sources/bjork-bjork-1992.md)) proposes that storage strength — how deeply a memory is encoded — only increases and never decreases. What we call forgetting is a decline in *retrieval strength* (current accessibility), not destruction of the memory trace. Memories that seem completely forgotten can often be relearned far more quickly than new material, indicating residual storage strength.

2. **The forgetting curve.** Ebbinghaus demonstrated that retention of nonsense syllables drops steeply in the first hours after learning, then more slowly. The specific parameters of the curve — onset rate, plateau — vary dramatically depending on the material, encoding conditions, prior knowledge, meaningfulness, and number of previous retrievals. Popular versions of "the forgetting curve" with specific percentage figures (e.g., "we forget 70% within 24 hours") are oversimplifications not supported by controlled research, as [Thalheimer (2010)](../sources/thalheimer-2010.md) documents.

3. **Forgetting creates conditions for maximal consolidation.** Paradoxically, some forgetting is a precondition for maximally effective relearning. When retrieval strength is low (i.e., when forgetting has partially occurred), a successful retrieval attempt produces the greatest gain in storage strength. This is the mechanistic basis for spacing effects and for the recommendation to space retrieval practice attempts over time.

4. **Factors modulating forgetting rate.** Forgetting is faster for: low-meaning or poorly-encoded material; material learned in a single session; material without regular retrieval practice; material learned in a single context. Forgetting is slower for: material retrieved many times; material spaced and interleaved; well-organised, meaningful, deeply processed knowledge.

5. **Applied forgetting curves.** Personalised scheduling systems like spaced repetition software use algorithmic models of individual forgetting curves (tracking performance and lapse rates per item) to schedule each review at the optimal time — before full forgetting occurs but late enough to maximise consolidation gain.

## Evidence

[Bjork & Bjork (1992)](../sources/bjork-bjork-1992.md) provide the theoretical framework explaining forgetting as retrieval-strength decline and showing why partial forgetting potentiates subsequent learning. [Thalheimer (2010)](../sources/thalheimer-2010.md) critically examines popular forgetting-curve claims in training contexts and urges practitioners to look at conditions rather than fixed percentages.

[Oermann et al. (2021)](../sources/oermann-et-al-2021.md) illustrate real-world forgetting dynamics in CPR skill retention over 12 months, demonstrating that training-interval length and sustainment schedule determine forgetting rate in applied settings. [Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) document that distributed practice is highly effective precisely because it counteracts forgetting at realistic time intervals.

## Applications

- Design review schedules that space sessions appropriately — not just once after initial learning.
- Use expanding-interval schedules (short early, longer later) for most material.
- Avoid "one-and-done" training events; build in spaced refreshers.
- When evaluating learning interventions, measure retention at realistic post-training delays (weeks or months), not only immediately after training.
- Critically evaluate vendor claims about forgetting curves that cite specific percentages without contextual caveats.
