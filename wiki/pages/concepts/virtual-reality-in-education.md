# Virtual Reality in Education

## Definition

Virtual reality (VR) in education refers to the use of computer-generated, three-dimensional environments — typically accessed through head-mounted displays (HMDs) or desktop interfaces — to support learning. VR creates a sense of presence in a simulated world, enabling learners to inhabit and interact with environments that may be dangerous, impossible, or impractical to access in the real world.

## Key Principles

1. **Immersion and presence**: VR's defining characteristic is the degree to which a learner feels "inside" the environment. Fully immersive HMD-based VR leverages vestibular and proprioceptive cues (head-tracking, body orientation) to create a stronger sense of spatial presence than desktop or flat-screen media.

2. **Engagement and affect**: VR consistently produces high learner engagement and positive emotional responses across studies. This affective benefit is robust even when cognitive learning outcomes (test scores) are not significantly improved over conventional instruction.

3. **Experiential learning and access**: VR enables learners to experience situations that are otherwise inaccessible: performing surgery, experiencing psychosis from the inside, practicing emergency response, navigating historical environments. This expands the range of achievable learning objectives.

4. **Memory encoding**: VR experiences are encoded as genuine episodic memories with high spatial and contextual detail, rather than as memories of watching media. Higher spatial presence correlates with more vivid and durable autobiographical recall.

5. **Technology maturation**: The field is in early stages. Most experimental studies prior to 2020 were small-scale and methodologically weak; standardised outcome measures and rigorous controlled trials are lacking.

## Evidence

**Engagement without guaranteed performance gains**: [Allcoat & von Mühlenen (2018)](../sources/allcoat-muhlenen-2018.md) found that VR learners showed equivalent knowledge test scores to video and textbook learners, but significantly higher positive emotion and engagement. This suggests VR's current advantage may be primarily motivational.

**Systematic review quality assessment**: [Jensen & Konradsen (2017)](../sources/jensen-konradsen-2017.md) reviewed 21 experimental studies of HMD-based VR in education and training, finding that study quality was below average on the MERSQI instrument — highlighting the need for more rigorous research.

**Memory advantages from immersion**: [Krokos et al. (2019)](../sources/krokos-plaisant-varshney-2019.md) demonstrated in a controlled experiment that HMD VR users recalled 8.8% more items in a virtual memory palace task than desktop users, with vestibular cues cited as a key mechanism.

**Autobiographical encoding**: [Schöne et al. (2019)](../sources/schone-wessels-gruber-2019.md) showed that VR experiences are encoded as genuine autobiographical memories, with spatial presence correlating with memory vividness.

**Spatial presence mediates retention**: [Cho](../sources/cho-spatial-presence-vr.md) found that in second-language vocabulary learning, higher spatial presence (achieved through HMD vs desktop) predicted better retention and motivation.

**Health professions simulation**: [Wan & Lam (2019)](../sources/wan-lam-2019.md) reviewed VR-based simulation for mental illness education, finding consistent improvements in empathy and knowledge. [Katz et al. (2020)](../sources/katz-et-al-2020.md) demonstrated the feasibility of self-directed VR for ACLS refresher training.

**Institutional adoption challenges**: [Pomerantz](../sources/pomerantz-xr-teaching-learning.md) documented that higher education institutions report high student enthusiasm for VR/XR, but face persistent barriers: lack of pedagogical frameworks, technical support, and scalability.

## Applications

- **Medical and clinical training**: VR is well-suited to health professions education where real patient exposure is limited, risky, or ethically constrained. First-person perspective simulations of illness states (psychosis, dementia) build empathy as well as knowledge.
- **Hazardous environment training**: Aviation, military, construction, and emergency response training benefit from VR's ability to simulate danger without risk.
- **Spatial and visualisation tasks**: VR is most effective for content with strong spatial components — anatomy, architecture, molecular structure, geography.
- **Language and cultural immersion**: Situated vocabulary learning in a VR environment that contextualises language in realistic settings.
- **Memory enhancement**: The memory palace technique is demonstrably more effective in immersive VR than on a flat screen, suggesting VR as a tool for high-demand memorisation tasks.
- **Asynchronous and scalable refresher training**: Self-directed VR scenarios allow learners to practice on demand, addressing scheduling and scaling constraints of traditional simulation.
