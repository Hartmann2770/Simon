## Definition

Learning design frameworks are structured approaches to the process of creating educational or training experiences. They differ from cognitive theories (like CLT) in that they address the *design process* rather than the cognitive mechanisms of learning — guiding decisions about content selection, activity sequencing, technology integration, assessment, and evaluation. Frameworks range from comprehensive curriculum-level models to practical toolkits for individual session or course design.

## Key Principles

### The 7Cs of Learning Design (Conole)

[Conole's 7Cs framework](../sources/conole-7cs-learning-design.md) provides a holistic curriculum design model for technology-enhanced learning:
- **Conceptualise** — define the overarching learning vision, pedagogical philosophy, and student profile
- **Capture** — identify and curate relevant resources (OER, media, tools)
- **Communicate** — plan learner-to-instructor and learner-to-content interactions
- **Collaborate** — design activities requiring peer interaction and social knowledge construction
- **Consider** — build in reflection and metacognitive activities
- **Combine** — map all elements into a coherent integrated design
- **Consolidate** — evaluate, iterate, and refine

The framework is deliberately holistic: it pushes designers to attend to social, reflective, and technological dimensions alongside cognitive content delivery.

### Performance-centred design (Shackleton-Jones)

[Shackleton-Jones (2019)](../sources/shackleton-jones-2019-how-people-learn.md) proposes that learning design should begin not with objectives or content but with a *performance gap analysis*: what does the person currently do, what should they do, and what is preventing the change? Only then is the question of training versus other interventions (job aids, process redesign, resources) addressed.

The framework distinguishes between **resources** (searchable, on-demand content) and **references** (interventions designed for specific performance contexts), arguing that most L&D conflates them. Affect and motivation are treated as design parameters, not afterthoughts.

### Evidence-informed design process (Neelen & Kirschner)

[Neelen & Kirschner (2020)](../sources/neelen-kirschner-2020-evidence-informed-learning-design.md) propose a three-question framework for L&D:
1. Is there a performance problem worth solving?
2. Can training solve it?
3. What does the evidence say about how to design that training?

This framework integrates performance consulting with learning science and positions the L&D professional as a decision-maker who can distinguish when training is and is not the right intervention.

### Competency-based curriculum frameworks

[Forneris et al. (2022)](../sources/forneris-et-al-2022-competency-based-nursing.md) illustrate competency-based education (CBE) as a curriculum design framework: outcomes are defined as observable competencies in authentic contexts, curriculum maps backwards from those competencies, and assessment is performance-based rather than time-based.

### AI-augmented design

[Hardman (2024)](../sources/hardman-2024-accelerating-excellence.md) documents emerging AI-assisted design workflows, noting that AI can accelerate lower-level design tasks but that evidence-based learning architecture decisions still require human expertise grounded in learning science.

## Evidence

Learning design frameworks are evaluated primarily through practitioner adoption, curriculum quality audits, and in some cases programme outcome data, rather than controlled experiments. The 7Cs framework has been applied in multiple higher education institutions. Competency-based frameworks have outcome data in nursing and medical education supporting improved practice readiness. Evidence-informed design frameworks are supported indirectly by the large body of learning science evidence they synthesise.

## Applications

- Use the 7Cs framework for technology-enhanced or blended learning course design, particularly in higher education contexts.
- Use the Shackleton-Jones performance-gap model as a pre-design diagnostic to determine whether training is the right intervention.
- Apply the Neelen & Kirschner three-question framework as a first-pass needs analysis before committing to a training programme.
- Use competency-based frameworks for professional and vocational curricula where practice transfer is the primary success criterion.
