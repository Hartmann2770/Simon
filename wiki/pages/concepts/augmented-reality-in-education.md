# Augmented Reality in Education

## Definition

Augmented reality (AR) in education refers to the overlay of computer-generated content — text, 3D models, animations, sound — onto a learner's real-world environment, perceived through a mobile device, tablet, or AR glasses. Unlike virtual reality, AR does not replace the real world but enriches it. Learners remain anchored in their physical surroundings while interacting with virtual information that is spatially situated within that environment.

## Key Principles

1. **Situated information**: AR's defining affordance is that digital content is presented in the physical location where it is most relevant. A chemistry student points a device at a molecular model and sees atomic bonding animations overlaid directly onto the physical model. A language learner sees vocabulary labels on real-world objects. This situatedness exploits context-dependent memory processes.

2. **Taxonomy of AR types**: AR technologies differ in how they anchor virtual content:
   - *Marker-based AR*: digital content triggered by a printed or physical marker (e.g., a QR code or image target)
   - *Markerless/location-based AR*: content triggered by GPS location or image recognition of the environment
   - *Projection-based AR*: digital content projected directly onto real surfaces

3. **Cognitive grounding**: [Carmichael et al.](../sources/carmichael-biddle-mould-ar-learning.md) identify four cognitive theories underpinning AR's educational value: mental models, dual coding, situated cognition, and spatial cognition. AR is particularly effective when it supports the construction of accurate mental models of spatial or structural relationships.

4. **Accessibility advantage over VR**: AR typically requires only a smartphone or tablet, making it significantly more accessible and deployable at scale than full VR setups. This lowers the barrier to classroom adoption.

5. **Engagement as a consistent finding**: Across reviews, AR consistently raises learner motivation and engagement. The question is whether this translates into durable learning gains — and the evidence is increasingly positive, particularly for STEM.

## Evidence

**Five directions for educational AR**: [Yuen et al. (2011)](../sources/yuen-yaoyuneyong-johnson-2011.md) provided the earliest influential taxonomy, identifying AR books, AR gaming, object modelling, skills training, and information management as the five major application directions in education.

**Systematic benefits review**: [Diegmann & Schmidt-Kraepelin](../sources/diegmann-schmidt-kraepelin-ar-benefits.md) synthesised 25 studies, finding consistent benefits for engagement, spatial understanding, and collaborative learning. Spatial understanding tasks showed the strongest effects.

**Meta-analytic confirmation**: [Garzón et al. (2019)](../sources/garzon-pavon-baldiris-2019.md) conducted a meta-analysis of 61 studies (2012–2018) and found a moderate-to-large overall positive effect of AR on learning outcomes. STEM and higher education showed the largest effects. Head-mounted AR produced larger effects than smartphone AR, suggesting that immersion level matters even within AR.

**Comprehensive review of AR types**: [Majeed & Ali (2020)](../sources/majeed-ali-2020.md) provided the most recent comprehensive review, confirming positive outcomes across disciplines while flagging device accessibility and teacher training as adoption barriers.

**Cognitive theory grounding**: [Carmichael et al.](../sources/carmichael-biddle-mould-ar-learning.md) provided principled criteria for when AR adds genuine learning value, preventing technology-for-novelty-sake adoption.

**Multimedia and situated learning**: [Santos et al.](../sources/santos-et-al-ar-vocabulary.md) demonstrated AR's effectiveness for situated vocabulary learning by treating AR as a form of multimedia embedded in authentic environments, resulting in better retention than flashcards.

## Applications

- **STEM education**: 3D molecular structures, anatomical overlays, geometric solids, and engineering diagrams made interactive and spatial.
- **Language learning**: Vocabulary labels on real-world objects for situated, context-rich word learning.
- **History and cultural heritage**: Historical reconstructions overlaid on present-day sites; museum exhibits with layered information.
- **Medical and technical training**: Procedural guidance overlaid on physical equipment or anatomical models.
- **AR books and print media**: Static textbook images brought to life with animations triggered by the printed page.
- **Collaborative learning**: Multi-user AR environments where groups interact with shared virtual content in the same physical space.
- **Field-based learning**: GPS-triggered content for geography, ecology, architecture, and urban studies fieldwork.
