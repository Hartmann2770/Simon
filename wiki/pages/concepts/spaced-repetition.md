## Definition

Spaced repetition (also called the spacing effect or distributed practice) is the finding that spreading learning or practice over multiple sessions separated by time intervals produces substantially better long-term retention than an equivalent amount of study concentrated in a single session (massed practice or "cramming"). It is one of the oldest and most robust findings in memory research, originally documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in the 1880s, and remains the empirical foundation for spaced repetition systems (SRS) like Anki and SuperMemo.

## Key Principles

1. **Spacing beats massing for long-term retention.** Cramming produces fast acquisition that decays rapidly. Spreading study over days or weeks produces slower initial gains but far more durable retention. This is the spacing effect paradox: what feels harder and less productive in the short term is more effective in the long term.

2. **The mechanism: retrieval strength and storage strength.** Bjork & Bjork's (1992) New Theory of Disuse explains spacing through two independent memory dimensions. *Retrieval strength* is how accessible a memory currently is (declines with time and disuse). *Storage strength* is how deeply encoded the memory is (only increases, never decreases). Retrieval is most beneficial — produces the greatest storage-strength gain — when retrieval strength is low (i.e., when some forgetting has occurred). Spacing ensures the learner retrieves memories when retrieval strength is declining, maximising the consolidation benefit of each retrieval attempt.

3. **Expanding intervals.** For most material, the optimal spacing schedule involves intervals that expand over time: short gaps first (hours to days), then progressively longer gaps (weeks, months) as the material becomes more stably encoded. This is the principle behind SM-2 and related SRS algorithms.

4. **Interaction with retrieval practice.** Spacing and retrieval practice are synergistic. Spaced retrieval practice — testing on material after a delay — produces effects substantially larger than either spacing or retrieval practice alone, because the delay lowers retrieval strength and makes successful recall more effortful and therefore more consolidating.

5. **Personalised scheduling.** The optimal interval varies by material difficulty, individual learning rate, and forgetting rate. [Oermann et al. (2021)](../sources/oermann-et-al-2021.md) showed that personalised, algorithm-prescribed intervals outperform fixed-interval refreshers for CPR skill retention at one year.

## Evidence

[Bjork & Bjork (1992)](../sources/bjork-bjork-1992.md) provide the theoretical framework (New Theory of Disuse) explaining why spacing works in terms of storage and retrieval strength dynamics. [Bjork (1994)](../sources/bjork-1994.md) situates spacing within the broader "desirable difficulties" framework, noting that spaced practice feels less productive but produces better outcomes.

[Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) gave distributed practice the second-highest "high utility" rating in their comprehensive review of learning strategies, alongside practice testing. [Karpicke & Roediger (2007)](../sources/karpicke-roediger-2007.md) demonstrated that the combination of retrieval practice and spaced repetition is especially powerful.

[Thalheimer (2010)](../sources/thalheimer-2010.md) critiques oversimplified forgetting-curve statistics commonly used to justify spaced training, but supports the principle that spaced review significantly reduces forgetting relative to single-session training. [Oermann et al. (2021)](../sources/oermann-et-al-2021.md) demonstrate the practical value of both spacing and personalised scheduling in a real-world healthcare training context.

## Applications

- Spaced repetition software (Anki, SuperMemo, Duolingo, RemNote) automates scheduling so learners review each item at the moment optimal for retention.
- Course design with quizzes and assignments spread over weeks rather than concentrated before exams.
- "Interleaved review" in textbooks and homework sets that include previously covered material.
- Professional continuing education: periodic skill refreshers scheduled at expanding intervals rather than annual one-off training.
- Memory palace systems can be combined with spaced repetition for high-volume factual material.
