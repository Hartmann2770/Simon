## Definition

**Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** is a macro-theory of human motivation developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan. It proposes that human beings have three innate psychological needs — **autonomy**, **competence**, and **relatedness** — and that the degree to which these needs are satisfied by a social environment determines the quality of motivation (how autonomous or controlled) and well-being (how flourishing or depleted) experienced in that environment.

## Key Principles

### The Three Basic Psychological Needs

**Autonomy** is the experience of volition — acting from genuine choice rather than external pressure or internal compulsion. Autonomy does not mean independence; one can be autonomous while following another person's guidance, provided one endorses the activity.

**Competence** is the experience of effectiveness — feeling capable of producing desired outcomes and avoiding undesired ones. Competence need satisfaction requires optimal challenge: too easy produces boredom; too difficult produces overwhelm. Both are need-thwarting.

**Relatedness** is the experience of meaningful connection to others — feeling cared for, belonging, and caring for others. In educational contexts, relatedness with teachers and peers supports motivation even for uninteresting tasks.

### The Motivation Continuum

SDT maps motivation onto a continuum of **relative autonomy** (also called **regulatory styles**):

| Regulatory Style | Type | Perceived Locus of Causality | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| External regulation | Extrinsic | External | Doing for reward/to avoid punishment |
| Introjection | Extrinsic | Somewhat external | Doing from ego pressure (shame, pride) |
| Identification | Extrinsic | Somewhat internal | Doing because personally valued |
| Integration | Extrinsic | Internal | Doing because consistent with identity |
| Intrinsic motivation | Intrinsic | Internal | Doing for inherent enjoyment/interest |

More autonomous regulation consistently predicts better learning, persistence, and well-being outcomes.

### Cognitive Evaluation Theory (sub-theory)

Explains how environmental factors affect intrinsic motivation: events that support perceived competence and autonomy (positive feedback, optimal challenge, meaningful rationale) enhance intrinsic motivation; events that undermine autonomy (contingent rewards, surveillance, threats, imposed goals) diminish it.

### Organismic Integration Theory (sub-theory)

Explains how extrinsic motivation is internalized: behaviors initially done for external reasons can be progressively internalized through identification and integration until they become self-determined. Internalization is facilitated by autonomy support and rationale-giving.

## Evidence

The empirical base for SDT spans 40+ years and thousands of studies:

- [Ryan and Deci (2000)](../sources/ryan-deci-2000-intrinsic-extrinsic.md): Core review article documenting the motivation continuum, the three needs, and their educational implications.
- [Eccles and Wigfield (2002)](../sources/eccles-wigfield-2002-motivational-beliefs.md): Expectancy-value theory — complementary framework showing that autonomy (through value identification and integration) and competence (through expectancy) jointly predict engagement.

## Applications

1. **Provide rationale**: telling learners *why* something is being taught (connecting to their goals and values) supports identification and internalization.
2. **Acknowledge negative affect**: when asking learners to do uninteresting but important tasks, acknowledging the difficulty ("this is tedious but important because...") is better than pretending it's interesting. Autonomy requires honesty.
3. **Support rather than control**: autonomy-supportive teachers who provide choice, minimize pressure, and understand learners' perspectives produce better long-term outcomes than controlling teachers, even when controlling teaching produces short-term compliance.
4. **Use informational feedback**: feedback framed as competence-relevant information ("here's how you can improve") supports the competence need; feedback framed as controlling evaluation ("you failed to meet the standard") thwarts autonomy.
5. **Build learning communities**: collaborative, trust-based learning environments satisfy the relatedness need and increase engagement even for content learners might not find intrinsically interesting.
