## Definition

Dual coding theory is a cognitive theory of mental representation developed by Allan Paivio, most fully in his 1971 book *Imagery and Verbal Processes* and the 1990 revised synthesis *Mental Representations: A Dual Coding Approach*. The theory holds that the human cognitive system contains two distinct but interconnected subsystems for representing and processing information: a **verbal system** specialised for language-based information and a **non-verbal (imaginal) system** specialised for mental images, spatial representations, and sensory-perceptual information.

The key educational implication is that material presented simultaneously through both channels — with verbal and visual information that are semantically connected — has the potential to form richer, more retrievable memory traces than material presented through one channel alone, because the learner can encode dual representations and use either or both to reconstruct the content later.

## Key Principles

### 1. Two independent but connected systems

Paivio proposed that verbal and imaginal systems are:

- **Functionally independent**: each can operate without activating the other. You can reason verbally without generating mental images; you can navigate spatially without verbal labelling.
- **Interconnected through referential links**: concepts represented verbally can activate their corresponding mental image (and vice versa) through associative connections Paivio called *referential connections*. A word like "bicycle" can activate both a verbal code and a vivid visual-spatial image.
- **Organised differently internally**: the verbal system is sequential and propositional (ideas linked in chains of logical or linguistic structure); the imaginal system is synchronous and analogical (preserving spatial relationships, perceptual properties, and whole-part structure simultaneously).

### 2. Three types of representational units

Paivio distinguished three levels of processing in dual coding:

- **Representational**: activating a verbal or imaginal code directly from sensory input (reading a word, seeing a picture).
- **Referential**: activating one system's code from the other — generating an image from a word, or a verbal label from an image.
- **Associative**: activating other codes within the same system — verbal associations, or connected images.

Memory and understanding are enhanced when all three levels are engaged: the learner not only encodes the incoming information but generates cross-system referential links and internal associative networks.

### 3. Concreteness and imageability

Paivio's most robust empirical finding was the **concreteness effect**: concrete, imageable words and concepts (chair, bicycle, ocean) are remembered significantly better than abstract, low-imagery words (justice, concept, likelihood). The explanation is that concrete concepts have strong imaginal codes available in addition to their verbal codes — they can be doubly encoded — while abstract concepts are predominantly verbally encoded and thus more likely to be forgotten or confused with similar abstract concepts.

This finding has direct instructional implications: using concrete examples, analogies, and visual representations of abstract concepts extends their encodability into the imaginal system, enriching memory.

### 4. Dual coding and the multimedia learning connection

Richard Mayer's [Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML)](multimedia-learning-principles.md) is explicitly built on dual coding theory. Mayer translates Paivio's framework into a model of multimedia learning with three stages:

1. **Selecting** relevant verbal and visual information from the presented material
2. **Organising** the selected information into coherent verbal and pictorial mental models
3. **Integrating** the verbal and pictorial models with each other and with prior knowledge

The CTML's architecture — two channels, each with limited capacity, both feeding into integrated long-term memory representations — is a direct translation of dual coding theory into instructional design guidance. The *multimedia principle* (learning better from words + pictures than from words alone) is dual coding theory's core prediction operationalised as an empirically testable instructional principle.

### 5. Relationship to cognitive load theory

[Cognitive load theory](cognitive-load-theory.md) and dual coding theory are complementary rather than competing frameworks, and they converge on similar instructional prescriptions through different theoretical routes:

- **The modality effect** in CLT (learning better from narration + graphics than from text + graphics) is explained by both frameworks: CLT explains it as splitting information across two processing channels (visual and auditory) to avoid overloading the single visual channel; dual coding explains it as engaging the verbal (auditory) system to process speech while the imaginal system processes graphics, allowing true dual encoding.
- **The split-attention effect** in CLT aligns with dual coding: physically separating related verbal and visual elements impedes the formation of referential connections between the verbal and imaginal codes.
- The redundancy effect in CLT (on-screen text + narration is worse than narration alone) is also compatible with dual coding: redundant verbal input overloads the verbal channel without adding imaginal encoding.

Despite their compatibility, the theories differ in scope: CLT is a theory of instructional design focused on managing cognitive load; dual coding theory is a broader theory of mental representation that encompasses perception, memory, and imagination beyond instructional contexts.

### 6. Dual coding in augmented reality

[Carmichael et al.](../sources/carmichael-biddle-mould-ar-learning.md) identify dual coding as one of the four cognitive theories underpinning the educational value of augmented reality. AR is particularly well-suited to dual coding because it overlays verbal labels, annotations, and explanations directly onto visual and spatial objects in the real-world environment. This co-location of verbal and imaginal content supports the formation of referential links: the learner simultaneously encodes a verbal code (the label or explanation) and an imaginal code (the spatial, visual representation of the object or process) with both anchored to the same physical location. [Augmented reality in education](augmented-reality-in-education.md) thus functions as an environmental instantiation of dual coding principles.

### 7. Dual coding and spatial memory

A particular strength of imaginal encoding is the retention of spatial and relational structure. Spatial memory — knowledge of where things are in relation to each other — is predominantly imaginal. This is why method-of-loci memory techniques (memory palaces) are effective: concrete, spatial, imaginal encoding is exploited for material that would otherwise be encoded only verbally. The superiority of spatial environments for memory, documented in virtual reality research ([Krokos et al., 2019](../sources/krokos-plaisant-varshney-2019.md)), reflects the power of imaginal/spatial encoding that dual coding theory predicts.

## Evidence

Paivio and colleagues accumulated extensive experimental evidence for dual coding through the 1970s–1990s. The concreteness effect — concrete words recalled better than abstract words — has been replicated thousands of times and is one of the most robust findings in memory research. Picture-word pairings are remembered better than word-word pairings, and pictures are remembered better than words alone — both predicted by dual coding's account of imaginal encoding advantages.

[Mayer & Moreno](../sources/mayer-moreno-cognitive-load-multimedia.md) provide experimental evidence directly relevant to the instructional design predictions of dual coding: the multimedia principle (pictures + narration > narration alone), the modality principle (narration + graphics > text + graphics), and the contiguity principle (spatially aligned text and graphics > separated) are all consistent with dual coding predictions about how verbal-imaginal referential links are formed.

[Carmichael et al.](../sources/carmichael-biddle-mould-ar-learning.md) apply dual coding to AR design, arguing that AR's co-location of verbal and visual information is theoretically advantageous on dual coding grounds — a prediction supported by the positive outcomes in the broader AR education meta-analyses ([Garzón et al., 2019](../sources/garzon-pavon-baldiris-2019.md)).

## Applications

- **Always pair verbal instruction with relevant visuals.** The multimedia principle is dual coding's most direct practical implication: material explained in words should be accompanied by diagrams, images, or spatial representations that encode the same content through the imaginal system.
- **Use spoken narration rather than on-screen text** when presenting with graphics, allowing the verbal channel (auditory) to process language while the imaginal channel (visual) processes images.
- **Align text labels with their visual referents.** Placing verbal information adjacent to the visual element it describes enables efficient formation of referential links between verbal and imaginal codes.
- **Use concrete examples for abstract concepts.** Abstract ideas that have weak imaginal codes can be enriched by concrete analogies, worked examples, and diagrams — each extends the concept into imaginal encoding.
- **Exploit spatial memory for difficult material.** Memory palace techniques, concept maps, and spatial diagrams exploit the imaginal system's strengths for sequential and relational material that is hard to retain in purely verbal form.
- **Design AR overlays to pair verbal and visual information.** AR's affordance for co-located verbal and visual content is directly aligned with dual coding principles — labels and annotations should appear on or immediately adjacent to the objects they describe, not in separate panels.
- **Pre-train with key visual representations.** Before presenting complex multimedia content, introduce learners to the visual representations (diagrams, models) they will encounter — this pre-builds the imaginal schemas that multimedia content will activate.
