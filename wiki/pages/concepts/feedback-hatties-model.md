## Definition

In Hattie and Timperley's model, feedback is information provided to a learner about their performance relative to a goal, with the function of reducing the gap between current and desired performance. Feedback is not a single entity but a family of interventions whose effects vary dramatically — from among the most powerful positive influences on learning to negative effects that impede it — depending on the level and type of feedback provided.

## Key Principles

### Three feedback questions

Effective feedback addresses three questions:
1. **Feed-up: Where am I going?** Goal clarity — the learner must understand what the target performance looks like.
2. **Feed-back: How am I going?** Current performance information — how does the learner's present work compare to the goal?
3. **Feed-forward: Where to next?** Next steps — what specifically should the learner do to close the gap?

All three components must be present for feedback to be fully effective. Most instructional feedback addresses only the second question and omits the first and third.

### Four levels of feedback

Feedback operates at four levels, with different effects on learning:

1. **Task level** — feedback on the correctness or quality of a specific piece of work ("Your answer is incorrect because..."). Effective for simple, well-defined tasks; less effective for developing higher-order thinking.
2. **Process level** — feedback on the strategies, approaches, or processes used to produce the work ("Try connecting the two concepts before drawing a conclusion"). Most effective for developing deep understanding and transfer.
3. **Self-regulation level** — feedback that develops the learner's capacity to monitor and adjust their own learning ("How would you check whether your answer makes sense?"). Supports autonomous learning and metacognition.
4. **Self level** — personal praise or criticism directed at the learner as a person ("You're so smart"). Minimal effect on learning outcomes; can be actively harmful by attributing performance to fixed ability rather than effort and strategy.

The highest-impact feedback is at the process and self-regulation levels. The most common type of teacher feedback — praise — is at the self level and is the least effective.

### Conditions for feedback effectiveness

Feedback is most powerful when:
- Goals are clear, specific, and challenging but achievable
- The gap between current and goal performance is moderate (not too small — no meaningful change — and not too large — overwhelms the learner)
- The learner has sufficient knowledge to interpret and act on the feedback
- Feedback is timely relative to the task
- The learning environment is perceived as safe enough for errors to be informative rather than threatening

## Evidence

[Hattie & Timperley (2007)](../sources/hattie-timperley-2007-power-of-feedback.md) synthesise over 500 meta-analyses and identify feedback as one of the most powerful influences on achievement — but also note that many feedback interventions have near-zero or negative effects. The variability is explained by the level (task vs. process vs. self) and by the conditions listed above.

[Hattie & Donoghue (2016)](../sources/hattie-donoghue-2016-learning-strategies.md) extend this by situating feedback within a phase-sensitive learning model: process-level feedback is most effective during deep-learning phases; during surface-learning phases, task-level corrective feedback may be more appropriate.

## Applications

- When giving feedback, address all three questions: what the goal is, how current performance compares, and what the learner should do next.
- Shift feedback from self-level praise to process-level commentary: instead of "Well done," say "You used an effective elimination strategy here — can you apply the same approach to the next problem?"
- Build self-regulation feedback by asking metacognitive questions: "How confident were you in that answer? How would you check it?"
- Design assessment tasks that generate feedback at the process level, not just binary correct/incorrect responses.
- In instructional design, programme formative check-ins that address the feed-forward question explicitly — not just "You scored 60%" but "Here are the three concepts to revisit before the next session."
