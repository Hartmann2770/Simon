# Simulation-Based Education

## Definition

Simulation-based education (SBE) refers to instructional approaches that use realistic replicas, scenarios, or models to allow learners to practice skills and make decisions in a controlled environment before doing so in the real world. SBE spans a wide range of fidelity: from low-fidelity paper cases and partial-task trainers to high-fidelity mannequins, standardised patients, and increasingly, virtual reality-based simulations. The core principle is that learning by doing — in a safe environment with feedback — produces more durable and transferable competencies than passive instruction.

## Key Principles

1. **Fidelity spectrum**: Simulation fidelity refers to how closely a simulation replicates the real-world environment and task. Higher fidelity is not always better — the appropriate fidelity level depends on the learning objectives. Early skill acquisition may benefit from lower fidelity (reduced complexity); expert refinement benefits from high-fidelity realistic scenarios.

2. **Deliberate practice and feedback**: SBE is most effective when it incorporates deliberate practice — targeted repetition with feedback on performance — rather than passive exposure. The ability to repeat scenarios, fail safely, and receive structured debriefing is a defining advantage over real-world clinical or operational exposure.

3. **VR as simulation platform**: Virtual reality adds unique capabilities to simulation: first-person perspective (enabling empathy simulations), unlimited repeatability without resource cost, remote and asynchronous access, and dynamic environmental manipulation. However, VR simulation differs from high-fidelity mannequin simulation in tactile and physical feedback.

4. **Transfer to real performance**: The ultimate goal of SBE is transfer — that skills practiced in simulation perform well in the real context. Transfer evidence is inconsistent and depends heavily on simulation design, debriefing quality, and the fidelity match between simulation and real task.

## Evidence

**VR for mental illness empathy**: [Wan & Lam (2019)](../sources/wan-lam-2019.md) reviewed VR-based simulation in mental health education, finding consistent gains in empathic attitudes and knowledge — outcomes unavailable through mannequin simulation, which cannot replicate first-person illness experience.

**ACLS VR refresher training**: [Katz et al. (2020)](../sources/katz-et-al-2020.md) demonstrated that a voice-driven VR ACLS scenario improved knowledge scores and was perceived as realistic and useful by anesthesia residents. The self-directed, asynchronous completion model addresses a key limitation of traditional simulation.

**XR in medical device training**: [Maddox](../sources/maddox-xr-medical-training.md) argues that XR-based simulation compresses training time and improves competency assessment outcomes for medical device familiarisation.

**HMD studies in training**: [Jensen & Konradsen (2017)](../sources/jensen-konradsen-2017.md) reviewed 21 studies using VR HMDs in education and training. Training domains (surgery, aviation, military) showed clearer skill-based outcomes than general academic domains, suggesting SBE applications may be where VR's evidence base is strongest.

**Mastery learning and spaced practice in healthcare simulation**: [Cheng & Nadkarni (2018)](../sources/cheng-nadkarni-2018-resuscitation-education.md) provide the strongest evidence base for mastery learning and spaced practice as core design principles in healthcare simulation, demonstrating that simulation-based resuscitation education benefits substantially from deliberate spacing of practice and mastery-based progression criteria.

## Applications

- **Health professions education**: Procedural skills (surgical, resuscitation, examination), clinical reasoning, communication, and empathy training across medicine, nursing, and allied health.
- **Aviation and military**: Flight simulators and tactical decision-making environments where real-world errors are catastrophic.
- **Emergency response and public safety**: Fire, rescue, and hazmat scenarios practiced safely before deployment.
- **Industrial and manufacturing**: Complex machinery operation, maintenance procedures, and quality control processes.
- **Corporate leadership and communication**: Role-play simulations for difficult conversations, negotiation, and team leadership.
