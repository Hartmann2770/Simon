## Definition

**Value creation in networks** refers to the multiple forms of value — tangible and intangible, immediate and long-term — that communities of practice and learning networks generate through their shared activities. The concept was systematically developed by Etienne Wenger, Beverly Trayner, and Maarten De Laat in their [2011 framework](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md), in response to the challenge of evaluating and justifying investment in informal learning communities that do not produce easily quantifiable outcomes.

## Key Principles

**1. Five cycles of value.** [Wenger, Trayner, and De Laat (2011)](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md) identify five distinct but connected cycles of value, each capturing outcomes at different levels of depth and different time horizons:

- *Cycle 1 — Immediate value*: the quality of community activities and interactions as they happen. Includes the direct experience of engagement: feeling heard, inspired, challenged, or supported. This value exists even if nothing is learned or applied.
- *Cycle 2 — Potential value*: knowledge capital accumulated through participation. Includes new knowledge, skills, tools, relationships, and resources that participants acquire and that are available for future use. This is latent value — not yet applied.
- *Cycle 3 — Applied value*: changes in practice resulting from participation. Participants do something differently because of what they learned in the community. Applied value is the bridge between potential and realized outcomes.
- *Cycle 4 — Realized value*: tangible performance improvements or outcomes attributable to changed practice. This is the level most visible to organizational funders: reduced errors, faster processes, better decisions, measurable results.
- *Cycle 5 — Reframing value*: transformation of how value itself is understood. The deepest level: participation changes what participants believe matters, how they define success, and what questions they ask. This is the hardest to measure and the most significant.

**2. The assessment gap.** Most organizational evaluation frameworks focus on Cycles 3 and 4 (applied and realized value) because these are quantifiable. But this misses Cycles 1, 2, and 5, which are often the most important — and it creates pressure to disband communities that are generating substantial potential and reframing value that has not yet translated into measurable outcomes. ([Wenger, Trayner, De Laat 2011](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md))

**3. Bidirectional value.** Value flows in both directions: participants create value for the community (contributing knowledge, effort, perspective) and the community creates value for participants (providing knowledge, relationships, identity, belonging). An evaluation framework that captures only organizational outcomes systematically undervalues the participant side of the exchange.

**4. Social learning spaces as value.** The relational context of the community — the trust, the shared norms, the sense of belonging — is itself a form of value, not merely a precondition for producing value. Communities that feel like spaces where learning and risk-taking are possible have value independent of specific knowledge outputs.

**5. Matched assessment strategies.** The framework proposes different assessment approaches for each cycle:
- Immediate: real-time feedback, participant observation, stories of the meeting/event experience
- Potential: inventories of knowledge capital, network maps showing new relationships, artifact audits
- Applied: before-and-after accounts, job shadowing, practitioner narratives of changed practice
- Realized: performance data, organizational metrics, case studies of innovation or improvement
- Reframing: strategic alignment analysis, shift-in-framing narratives, changes in community goals

## Evidence

The framework draws on empirical work with learning networks in the Dutch educational system and Dutch national police force, conducted by De Laat and colleagues, combined with Wenger and Trayner's practitioner experience with CoP facilitation. The five-cycle model was inductively developed from analysis of what CoPs actually produced that participants found valuable, then formalized into an evaluable framework.

[Wenger (2000)](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md) and [Snyder and Wenger (2010)](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md) provide the broader systemic context within which value creation occurs — reminding that value in one community must be assessed in relation to the health of the wider social learning system.

## Applications

**Community evaluation and justification.** The five-cycle framework provides a principled basis for evaluating CoPs that avoids the trap of premature ROI assessment. Organizations can develop evaluation portfolios that address each cycle with appropriate evidence — giving decision-makers a complete picture rather than a misleading partial one.

**Community design.** Designing for value creation means attending to conditions that support each cycle: welcoming activities (Cycle 1), structured knowledge sharing and artifact production (Cycle 2), connection of community work to job tasks (Cycle 3), measurement and feedback systems (Cycle 4), and strategic dialogue about what matters (Cycle 5).

**Grant and funding applications.** Development organizations, educational institutions, and NGOs using CoP-based approaches can use the framework to communicate multidimensional value to funders who expect quantifiable impact — while documenting the deeper transformations that justify long-term investment.

**Learning analytics.** Digital learning community platforms can be instrumented to capture evidence at multiple value cycles: participation data (Cycle 1), artifact production (Cycle 2), self-reported practice change (Cycle 3), performance outcomes (Cycle 4). Cycle 5 requires narrative and qualitative methods.
