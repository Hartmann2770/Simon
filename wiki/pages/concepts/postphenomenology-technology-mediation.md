## Definition

**Postphenomenology** is an approach in philosophy of technology, developed primarily by Don Ihde and extended by Peter-Paul Verbeek, that studies the specific ways in which technologies mediate human experience. Rather than treating technology as a neutral tool, postphenomenology investigates how technologies shape what we perceive, what we can do, and what we experience — how they co-constitute human existence.

**Technology mediation** is the central concept: technologies do not simply extend human capacities but actively transform the relationship between humans and the world. A thermometer doesn't just extend my temperature-sensing; it transforms temperature from a qualitative experience into a quantitative measurement, changing how I relate to illness, climate, and physical states.

## Key Principles

### Human-Technology Relations (Ihde's Taxonomy)

Ihde identified four types of human-technology relations, extended by Verbeek:

1. **Embodiment relations**: the technology becomes transparent, experienced as an extension of the body rather than as a separate object. Example: a blind person's cane; eyeglasses. The world is experienced *through* the technology.

2. **Interpretive relations**: the technology mediates interpretation of the world — one reads the world through the technology's output. Example: a thermometer, a seismograph, an MRI scanner. The world is not directly experienced but *interpreted* via technological signs.

3. **Alterity relations**: the technology is experienced as a quasi-other — a counterpart in interaction. Example: a robot, a video game character, an ATM. Neither tool nor human, but an interactant.

4. **Background relations**: the technology shapes context without direct interaction — it structures the environment without being attended to. Example: central heating, lighting systems. The world is experienced within the conditions the technology produces.

### Technology Scripts Behavior

Following Latour, Verbeek argues that **design is never value-neutral**: artifacts encode scripts — they invite certain behaviors and discourage or prevent others. A narrow doorway scripts single-person passage; a wide lecture hall scripts passive listening. Educational technologies script specific learning behaviors and, by implication, specific learning relationships.

### Non-Representational Knowledge

Thrift (2005) extends this insight to organizational and economic knowledge: much human competence is non-representational — it is embodied, habitual, and enacted rather than consciously formulated. Technologies that demand explicit representation of tacit knowledge may actually disrupt skilled performance.

### Affordances (Gibson)

Gibson's theory of affordances ([gibson-1977-affordances](../sources/gibson-1977-affordances.md)) provides a complementary ecological approach: environments (including technological environments) offer action possibilities — affordances — that are perceived directly rather than inferred. Technologies afford certain actions and not others; these affordances shape what learners do in technological environments.

## Applications

1. **Design audit**: treat every educational technology as a mediator that scripts behavior. Ask: what human-technology relation does this tool create? What does it make invisible? What behaviors does it invite or prevent?
2. **Examine background technologies**: the most powerful mediators are often the invisible background conditions — the learning management system, the classroom layout, the assessment format. Their mediating effects are hardest to see precisely because they are backgrounded.
3. **Design for disclosure**: technologies in interpretive relations work best when they make their outputs interpretable and their limitations transparent — learners need to understand what the technology is showing them and what it misses.
4. **Match relation type to learning goal**: a simulator that puts the learner *in* the procedure (embodiment relation) teaches differently from a visualization that shows the procedure from outside (interpretive relation). Match the human-technology relation to the intended learning.
