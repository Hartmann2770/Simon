## Definition

Generative learning is the process by which learners actively construct meaning from new information by making connections between it and their existing knowledge. Introduced by M. C. Wittrock (1974), the generative model holds that understanding — and especially the ability to transfer knowledge — arises not from passive information reception but from the learner's active generation of relationships, analogies, images, summaries, and inferences. Generative processing transforms information into personally meaningful and transferable knowledge.

## Key Principles

1. **Generation versus storage.** Wittrock distinguished two aspects of learning: (a) *storage* — encoding information into memory, which can occur passively; and (b) *generation* — constructing meaningful relationships between new and existing knowledge, which requires active effort. Only generation produces the deep understanding that supports transfer.

2. **Generative activities.** Activities that require learners to generate — rather than passively receive — are more effective for deep learning:
   - Self-explanation (explaining the material in one's own words)
   - Summarising (identifying key ideas and their relationships)
   - Elaborative interrogation (asking "why?" about the material)
   - Concept mapping (drawing relationships between ideas)
   - Analogy generation (connecting to prior knowledge)
   - Question generation (creating test questions about the material)

3. **Transfer through generation.** The generative model directly predicts transfer: when learners actively connect new information to prior knowledge structures, the new information is encoded within those structures and becomes available in contexts where the prior knowledge applies. Purely passive encoding produces context-specific, brittle knowledge.

4. **Generation as effortful processing.** Generative activities are cognitively effortful — they require thinking, not just attending. This places generative learning within the [desirable difficulties](desirable-difficulties.md) framework: the effort required for generation is what produces the learning benefit.

5. **Relationship to retrieval practice.** Retrieval practice is itself a generative activity: when a learner attempts to recall material from memory, they must reconstruct it — generating it from internal cues rather than reading it from an external source. The overlap between generative learning and retrieval practice is deep.

## Evidence

[Wittrock (1974)](../sources/wittrock-1974.md) is the foundational theoretical source. The paper presents a cognitive model of learning with understanding and summarises empirical support for the superiority of generative over passive processing.

[Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) evaluated several generative strategies: elaborative interrogation and self-explanation received "moderate" utility ratings — evidence was positive but more variable than for retrieval practice or spacing. Summarisation and concept mapping received lower ratings, partly because the quality of generation is harder to control and train.

[Roediger, Putnam, & Smith (2011)](../sources/roediger-putnam-smith-2011.md) note that retrieval practice — itself a generative activity — promotes transfer, suggesting generative mechanisms are at work when testing benefits are observed.

## Applications

- Replace re-reading with self-explanation: after reading a passage, explain the key ideas to yourself or to another person.
- Use concept mapping or diagramming activities to require learners to generate explicit relational connections.
- Assign question-generation tasks: learners who write their own test questions understand the material more deeply.
- Introduce elaborative interrogation prompts (e.g., "Why would this be true?") in learning materials.
- Design assessments that require application and explanation rather than recognition — these force generative processing.
