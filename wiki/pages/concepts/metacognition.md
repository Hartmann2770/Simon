## Definition

Metacognition is knowledge about and regulation of one's own cognitive processes. The term was introduced by Flavell (1979) and encompasses: (a) *metacognitive knowledge* — stored beliefs about oneself as a learner, about task demands, and about the effectiveness of different strategies; and (b) *metacognitive monitoring and regulation* — real-time tracking of one's own comprehension, memory, and performance, and adjusting strategy accordingly. In learning research, metacognition is critical because it determines whether students select effective strategies, allocate study time appropriately, and accurately judge what they do and do not know.

## Key Principles

1. **Flavell's tripartite framework.** [Flavell (1979)](../sources/flavell-1979.md) distinguished three types of metacognitive knowledge: *person knowledge* (beliefs about oneself and others as cognitive agents, e.g., "I am bad at maths"), *task knowledge* (beliefs about the difficulty and demands of tasks, e.g., "prose is easier to learn than lists"), and *strategy knowledge* (beliefs about which strategies work for which tasks, e.g., "I learn better by testing myself").

2. **Metacognitive monitoring.** The "feeling of knowing" (FOK) and "judgements of learning" (JOL) are metacognitive monitoring signals — subjective assessments of whether material has been learned. Research shows these signals are systematically biased: fluency of processing (how easy information feels to process) inflates JOLs even when retention is poor.

3. **The fluency illusion.** Restudying makes material feel familiar and easy to process, creating an illusion of having learned it. [Roediger & Karpicke (2006b)](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006b.md) showed that students who restudied felt more confident about later recall than students who tested themselves — but their actual retention was substantially worse. This is the "fluency illusion" or "illusion of knowing."

4. **Metacognition is learnable.** Flavell argued that metacognitive skill develops throughout childhood and can be explicitly taught. Students who understand the desirable-difficulties paradox — that difficult, effortful practice is more effective despite feeling harder — are better able to adopt effective strategies.

5. **Metacognition and retrieval practice.** [Roediger, Putnam, & Smith (2011)](../sources/roediger-putnam-smith-2011.md) list improved metacognitive monitoring as one of the ten benefits of testing: testing exposes gaps in knowledge, producing more accurate calibration of what has and has not been learned. In contrast, restudying creates overconfidence.

6. **Developmental trajectory.** Young children are severely limited in metacognitive knowledge; significant development occurs through adolescence and into adulthood. Educational interventions can accelerate development of metacognitive skills.

## Evidence

[Flavell (1979)](../sources/flavell-1979.md) established the conceptual framework and research agenda. [Bjork (1994)](../sources/bjork-1994.md) identified the "current-performance illusion" as a key metacognitive failure in training contexts — instructors and learners alike optimise for surface-level performance signals rather than durable learning. [Roediger & Karpicke (2006b)](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006b.md) provided direct evidence of metacognitive miscalibration: restudiers' confidence outran their actual retention on delayed tests. [Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) noted that students' heavy reliance on ineffective strategies (highlighting, rereading) partly reflects metacognitive failures — students judge fluency as learning.

## Applications

- Teach students explicitly about the fluency illusion so they can distrust feelings of familiarity as indicators of learning.
- Use "pre-mortems" or prediction exercises to expose metacognitive miscalibration before high-stakes tests.
- Promote self-testing habits (flashcards, practice problems, recitation) rather than re-reading.
- In instructional design, build in mechanisms for learners to test their own understanding (not just exposure) at multiple points.
- Train educators to measure retention at realistic delays rather than end-of-session performance to avoid the current-performance illusion.
