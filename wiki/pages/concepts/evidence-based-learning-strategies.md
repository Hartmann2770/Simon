## Definition

Evidence-based learning strategies are study and instructional methods whose effectiveness is supported by rigorous empirical research — typically experimental and meta-analytic studies — distinguishing them from strategies that are merely popular, intuitive, or based on uncontrolled observations. The field distinguishes strategies that produce durable learning (long-term retention and transfer) from those that produce merely fluent performance during practice.

## Key Principles

### The high-utility strategies (Dunlosky et al.)

[Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) rate ten strategies across five criteria. Two receive *high* utility ratings:
- **Practice testing / retrieval practice** — the single most broadly effective strategy, working across ages, subjects, and retention intervals
- **Distributed practice / spaced repetition** — consistently superior to massed practice for long-term retention

Two receive *moderate* utility:
- Elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, interleaved practice

Low-utility strategies despite widespread student use: highlighting, rereading, keyword mnemonics.

### Phase-sensitivity (Hattie & Donoghue)

[Hattie & Donoghue (2016)](../sources/hattie-donoghue-2016-learning-strategies.md) add a crucial qualification: strategy effectiveness depends on learning phase. Strategies suited for *surface learning* (mnemonic devices, practice testing for recall) differ from those suited for *deep learning* (elaboration, self-explanation, concept mapping) and *transfer* (varied practice, strategy awareness). Applying deep-learning strategies before surface knowledge is consolidated produces little gain.

### Evidence-informed L&D design

[Neelen & Kirschner (2020)](../sources/neelen-kirschner-2020-evidence-informed-learning-design.md) operationalise evidence-based principles for the L&D context:
- Begin with a performance analysis, not a content inventory
- Apply retrieval practice, spacing, and interleaving in training design
- Reject popular L&D myths (learning styles, the 70-20-10 rule, the idea that learner preference equals effectiveness)
- Develop evaluative research literacy to assess the quality and transferability of evidence

### Neuroscience perspective

[Oakley, Rogowsky & Sejnowski (2021)](../sources/oakley-rogowsky-sejnowski-2021-uncommon-sense-teaching.md) ground evidence-based strategies in neuroscience: spaced practice and retrieval under conditions of partial forgetting build stronger long-term memory traces by forcing reconsolidation.

## Evidence

The evidence base is substantial and convergent. Retrieval practice and spaced practice are among the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology, supported by decades of laboratory and classroom research ([Dunlosky et al., 2013](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md)). Hattie's synthesis of 228 meta-analyses provides the largest comparative effect-size database for learning strategies.

The evidence also consistently shows that students and teachers are poor judges of which strategies work: the most popular strategies (highlighting, rereading) are among the least effective; the most effective strategies (testing, spacing) are among the least commonly used.

## Applications

- Teach students explicitly about the gap between popular and effective study strategies; metacognitive awareness of this gap improves strategy adoption.
- Build retrieval practice into every instructional design: low-stakes quizzing, flashcards, practice problems before restudy.
- Space learning across sessions rather than concentrating it; build review cycles into course calendars.
- Interleave problem types in practice sets rather than blocking by type.
- Assess after a delay rather than immediately after instruction to measure durable learning rather than fleeting performance.
- Use Hattie's phase model to match strategies to the learner's current level: surface strategies for novices, deep strategies for learners who have built foundational knowledge.
