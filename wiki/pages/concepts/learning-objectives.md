## Definition

Learning objectives (also called learning outcomes, performance objectives, or instructional objectives) are explicit statements that describe what a learner will be able to do as a result of instruction. The field has evolved from Mager's behavioural objectives (1962) through Bloom's Taxonomy (1956, revised 2001) to competency-based frameworks and contemporary critiques that argue for performance-centred, affect-sensitive, or context-specific formulations.

## Key Principles

### Mager's behavioural approach

[Mager's ABCD model](../sources/mager-abcd-learning-objectives.md) defines four required components of a well-formed objective:
- **Audience** — who the learner is
- **Behaviour** — what they will do (observable action verb)
- **Condition** — under what circumstances
- **Degree** — to what standard

The core insight is that vague verbs ("understand," "appreciate") are unobservable and therefore cannot drive design or assessment. Mager's approach underpins *criterion-referenced instruction*: design and assessment are anchored to a specific performance standard.

### Bloom's Taxonomy and its limits

Bloom's Taxonomy (1956, revised by Anderson & Krathwohl in 2001) classifies cognitive objectives in a six-level hierarchy from recall to evaluation/create. It is widely used in curriculum design.

Critiques include:
- The hierarchy does not reliably map onto distinct instructional or assessment methods ([Sugrue, n.d.](../sources/sugrue-blooms-taxonomy-alternative.md))
- The levels describe *types of thinking* but do not prescribe *how to develop* each type
- For workplace learning, performance transfer — not level of thinking — is the primary concern ([eLearning Coach, 2020](../sources/elearningcoach-2020-alternatives-blooms.md))

### Alternatives to Bloom's

[Sugrue (n.d.)](../sources/sugrue-blooms-taxonomy-alternative.md) proposes Merrill's *content-performance matrix* as a design-actionable alternative: content type (fact, concept, procedure, principle) crossed with performance level (recall vs. use) generates different instructional strategies for each cell.

[Forneris et al. (2022)](../sources/forneris-et-al-2022-competency-based-nursing.md) demonstrate a competency-based approach in nursing education: objectives are written as observable performance competencies in real practice contexts, bypassing the Bloom's hierarchy.

[Camara (n.d.)](../sources/camara-holistic-framework-education.md) argues for a holistic framework that includes non-cognitive competencies (self-regulation, interpersonal skills) alongside cognitive outcomes.

### Objectives as retrieval prompts

[Kirschner & Neelen (2021)](../sources/neelen-kirschner-2021-learning-objectives-goal.md) propose using objectives as *pre-test questions* rather than content roadmaps. Posing the objective as a question before instruction engages the [testing effect](testing-effect.md): attempting retrieval before encoding primes subsequent learning.

### Performance-centred critique

[Shackleton-Jones (2019)](../sources/shackleton-jones-2019-how-people-learn.md) argues that objectives-driven design misses the fundamental question of *why* learners would be motivated to pursue the objective. Effective design begins with the performance gap and the affective conditions that would motivate a learner to close it, not with an objectives list.

## Evidence

The evidence base for *behavioural* objectives in terms of improved learning outcomes is mixed; well-specified objectives improve instructional coherence and assessment validity but do not on their own produce learning. The evidence is stronger when objectives are used to generate retrieval practice (pre-testing), consistent with [testing effect](testing-effect.md) and [retrieval practice](retrieval-practice.md) research.

Competency-based frameworks are supported by outcome data in professional education (nursing, medicine, law), where the alignment of objectives to authentic practice contexts improves transfer.

## Applications

- Write objectives with observable verbs (define, calculate, diagnose, compare) rather than unobservable ones (understand, know, appreciate).
- Use Mager's ABCD model for professional/training contexts where performance standards are clear.
- For complex professional outcomes, use competency frameworks that specify performance context rather than cognitive level alone.
- Present objectives as questions at the start of a learning session to activate pre-testing benefits.
- Align assessment tasks to objective verbs: if the objective says "apply," the test cannot ask only for recall.
