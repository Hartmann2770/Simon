## Definition

Direct instruction (also called explicit teaching) refers to a family of teaching approaches in which the teacher or instructional designer takes clear responsibility for selecting, sequencing, and presenting content, providing guided practice with feedback, and moving students toward independent performance in structured, deliberate steps. The term encompasses teacher-led explanation, worked examples, guided practice, and scaffolded support — as opposed to minimally guided discovery or purely constructivist approaches.

## Key Principles

1. **Begin with prior knowledge activation.** Each new lesson begins with a short review of previously learned material, strengthening those schemas and making them available for integrating new content. This is Rosenshine's first principle and is consistent with CLT's account of schema activation reducing intrinsic load.

2. **Present new material in small steps.** New content is introduced in manageable increments, with student practice after each step, rather than front-loading large amounts of information. This directly manages intrinsic load.

3. **Provide models and worked examples.** Before students practise independently, they see complete, correctly worked examples. This is the primary instantiation of CLT's worked-example effect in teaching practice.

4. **Guided practice with checking.** The teacher actively monitors student practice, asking frequent questions and checking understanding before allowing independent work. The target success rate during guided practice is approximately 80% — high enough to confirm learning but not so easy as to confirm mere familiarity.

5. **Scaffold complex tasks.** When tasks exceed students' current capacity, supports (hints, partial solutions, checklists, frameworks) are provided and then gradually withdrawn (fading) as competence grows.

6. **Weekly and monthly review.** Regular cumulative review of prior content is built into the instructional sequence. This directly implements spaced practice and counters forgetting.

7. **High success rate as a signal.** Rosenshine's research on master teachers identified approximately 80% correct responses during guided practice as a signature of effective instruction — it signals that the instructional sequence is well-calibrated.

## Evidence

[Rosenshine (2012)](../sources/rosenshine-2012-principles-of-instruction.md) synthesises three converging bodies of evidence: cognitive science research on memory and working memory, observational studies of master teachers, and research on cognitive supports. The convergence of all three on essentially identical prescriptions is taken as strong corroboration.

[Oakley, Rogowsky, & Sejnowski (2021)](../sources/oakley-rogowsky-sejnowski-2021-uncommon-sense-teaching.md) provide a neuroscientific perspective, arguing that direct instruction is essential for building the foundational knowledge in long-term memory that enables subsequent higher-order thinking. Students with smaller working memory spans — who are disproportionately disadvantaged by unstructured discovery learning — benefit most from direct instruction's scaffolding.

[Sweller (2011)](../sources/sweller-2011-cognitive-load-theory.md) supplies the theoretical mechanism: unguided problem-solving is cognitively expensive for novices because they lack the schemas needed to constrain search. Direct instruction provides those schemas through worked examples and guided practice.

[Neelen & Kirschner (2020)](../sources/neelen-kirschner-2020-evidence-informed-learning-design.md) catalogue the broader evidence base and address L&D myths that impede direct instruction adoption (e.g., the belief that discovery learning is inherently more motivating or more effective).

## Applications

- Build daily review of prior content into lesson openings (5–10 minutes of low-stakes retrieval).
- Sequence new content in steps no larger than working memory can handle; use worked examples before problems.
- Check for understanding continuously rather than only at the end of a lesson; use cold-calling, mini-whiteboards, exit tickets.
- Provide scaffolds (partially completed examples, sentence frames, procedural checklists) for difficult tasks, then systematically fade them.
- Design practice so that success rate during guided phases is approximately 80%; adjust pacing if success is too low (slow down) or too high (accelerate or increase challenge).
