## Definition

In the context of learning environments, **space** refers to the structural, geometric, and technical properties of an environment — its layout, navigation, affordances, and infrastructure. **Place** refers to space that has been invested with social meaning through human practice, history, and community — an environment that people feel they *belong to* rather than merely *inhabit*. The distinction, originally drawn from humanistic geography (Tuan 1977) and applied to collaborative computing (Harrison and Dourish 1996), is fundamental to understanding why some learning environments engage learners deeply while others feel alienating.

The key insight: **designers create spaces; communities make places**.

## Key Principles

### The Space/Place Distinction

Yi-Fu Tuan (1977) established the philosophical foundation: space is open, undifferentiated, and associated with freedom of movement; place is bounded, familiar, and associated with security, belonging, and accumulated meaning. Places are spaces that have been *paused in* — where movement has settled into dwelling, and dwelling has accumulated history.

Harrison and Dourish (1996) translated this to collaborative computing: physical and digital spaces become places through social practice. The same virtual classroom can be experienced as a sterile information delivery system or as a meaningful community, depending on what social life develops within it.

### From E-learning Space to E-learning Place (Wahlstedt et al.)

Wahlstedt et al. (2008) identify three interlocking conditions for the space-to-place transition in e-learning:

1. **Space**: the technical infrastructure must afford rich social interaction
2. **Community**: participants must develop genuine relationships and shared norms
3. **Practice**: activities and routines must develop over time within the environment

All three are necessary: a rich technical environment without community remains a space; a community without adequate affordances cannot develop place; activities without infrastructure cannot be sustained.

### Place is Mutually Constituted

Dourish's (2006) revision complicates the simple space→place sequence: space and place are not sequential stages but are **continuously co-produced** through ongoing social practice. Physical properties and social meanings are not independent — they emerge together through use. Designers cannot control place-making but they can create conditions that enable or constrain it.

### Organizational Dimensions

Whitworth (2008) adds the institutional dimension: the space-to-place transition depends not only on technology and social dynamics but on **organizational structures** — curricula that require genuine collaboration create conditions for place; curricula organized around individual completion do not. Institutional design choices shape what social life is possible.

### Digital and Physical Places

Zhang and Jacob (2012) apply the framework to cyberspace: digital environments can constitute genuine places when they accumulate social history, develop community norms, and become sites of identity formation. The felt sense of "being somewhere" in online environments is real and consequential for learning — it is not simply metaphorical.

### Location-Based Learning (Gazzard)

Gazzard (2011) extends the analysis to mobile media and location-based technologies: physical spaces can be rapidly converted into meaningful learning places through location-aware technologies that layer information, narrative, or game mechanics onto them. Field trips augmented with AR, geocaching activities, and location-triggered problem-solving can create intense, rapid place-formation through the specificity of location-based interaction.

## Applications

1. **Design for place-formation, not just space**: beyond organizing content, create conditions for community, shared history, and repeated practice. This means discussion forums, social presence features, informal spaces, and activities that require genuine collaboration.
2. **Respect the social nature of place-making**: designers set conditions; community members make places. Over-controlling the social environment (prescribing all interactions, eliminating informal spaces) prevents authentic place formation.
3. **Attend to organizational design**: curriculum structure and institutional norms powerfully shape whether genuine place-formation is possible in a learning environment.
4. **Leverage location for physical learning**: location-aware technologies can create powerful learning places from physical environments by layering relevant information, narrative, or tasks onto sites with inherent meaning.
5. **Support social presence in online learning**: features that make other learners genuinely "there" — asynchronous video, social profiles, shared workspaces — support the social dimension of place formation in digital environments.
