## Definition

A **community of practice** (CoP) is a group of people who share a domain of concern, engage in joint activities and mutual help around it, and develop over time a shared repertoire of resources, stories, tools, and ways of doing things. The concept was introduced by Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger in [*Situated Learning* (1991)](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md) and fully theorized in Wenger's [*Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity* (1998)](../sources/wenger-1998-communities-of-practice.md).

Communities of practice are everywhere and have existed throughout human history — guilds, professional bodies, scientific disciplines, and neighborhood networks are all examples. What the CoP concept adds is a systematic account of why and how they work as learning systems.

## Key Principles

**1. Three defining characteristics.** Wenger's [1998 book](../sources/wenger-1998-communities-of-practice.md) and [2006 introduction](../sources/wenger-2006-cop-brief-introduction.md) specify that all three must be present:
- *Domain*: a shared area of knowledge and concern that defines the community's identity and gives its members a common purpose. Without a domain, there is no shared direction.
- *Community*: members engage with each other — they discuss problems, share resources, build relationships. Without community, the domain is just a topic with isolated practitioners.
- *Practice*: members develop over time a shared repertoire of experiences, tools, stories, and ways of addressing recurrent problems. Without practice, the community is just a club.

**2. Participation and reification.** Practice in a CoP consists of both active **participation** (doing things together, relating to others) and **reification** (producing objects, documents, tools, and concepts that embody aspects of the practice). Neither alone is sufficient: pure participation without reification has no memory; pure reification without participation loses meaning. A healthy CoP maintains a productive tension between both poles ([Wenger 1998](../sources/wenger-1998-communities-of-practice.md)).

**3. Mutual engagement, joint enterprise, shared repertoire.** The more specific structural definition of a CoP in the 1998 framework:
- *Mutual engagement*: members do things together and build relationships through regular interaction
- *Joint enterprise*: members develop a shared understanding of what they are collectively about — this is negotiated, not imposed
- *Shared repertoire*: the community accumulates resources (terms, routines, gestures, artifacts, stories) that encode its history

**4. Identity and membership trajectories.** Learning in a CoP is inseparable from identity formation — becoming a certain kind of person, not just acquiring certain skills. Membership has a temporal structure: [*legitimate peripheral participation*](../concepts/legitimate-peripheral-participation.md) describes the path from newcomer to full participant ([Lave and Wenger 1991](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md)).

**5. Boundaries and constellations.** CoPs are not isolated: they are connected to other communities through boundary objects (artifacts that bridge different practices) and brokers (people who span multiple communities). Groups of related CoPs form **constellations of practice**. These connections are as important as internal cohesion for systemic learning ([Wenger 2000](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md), [Snyder and Wenger 2010](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md)).

**6. Cultivation, not command.** CoPs cannot be created by organizational fiat — they emerge from practice. But they can be cultivated by providing conditions: legitimacy, resources, time, and space. The art of CoP stewardship is supporting without over-controlling ([Wenger 2010](../sources/wenger-2010-career-of-a-concept.md)).

## Evidence

The foundational empirical base comes from anthropological studies of apprenticeship across five settings (Yucatec midwives, tailors, Navy quartermasters, meat cutters, AA members) reported in [Lave and Wenger 1991](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md). Wenger's 1998 monograph draws on extended fieldwork with insurance claims processors. [Akther and Georgesen (2011)](../sources/akther-georgesen-2011-telecentres.md) apply the framework to telecentre adoption in rural Bangladesh, showing how CoP dynamics shape digital literacy acquisition in non-Western, low-resource contexts — including how exclusion from legitimate peripheral participation reproduces inequality.

[Wenger, Trayner, and De Laat (2011)](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md) provide empirical grounding for a five-level value creation model, drawing on Dutch educational and police network cases, demonstrating that CoPs produce value at multiple levels with different time horizons.

[Wenger (2010)](../sources/wenger-2010-career-of-a-concept.md) documents how the concept has been received and often misapplied in management and policy contexts over two decades, providing critical perspective on its own history.

## Applications

**Professional development.** CoP theory has been widely applied to teacher professional development, medical education, and management training. The implication is that sustained collegial practice communities — not one-off workshops — are the primary mechanism for professional learning.

**Organizational knowledge management.** Organizations can cultivate CoPs to address knowledge that resists formalization: tacit expertise, cross-functional problem-solving, lessons learned from practice. But mandating CoPs undermines their emergent character.

**Educational design.** If learning is participation in practice communities, then schools must consider what communities students are joining, not only what content they are receiving. Authentic tasks, exposure to practitioners, and opportunities to contribute to genuine practice are more important than curriculum coverage.

**International development.** [Akther and Georgesen's (2011)](../sources/akther-georgesen-2011-telecentres.md) study shows that technology interventions succeed or fail depending on whether they are embedded in existing practice communities. Technology literacy programs must engage existing community networks, not assume a neutral user population.

**Assessment.** [Wenger, Trayner, and De Laat's (2011)](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md) value-creation framework provides tools for evaluating CoPs across five levels, matching assessment strategy to type of value sought.
