## Definition

Transfer of learning is the application of knowledge, skills, or strategies acquired in one context to a new or different context. Transfer is the ultimate goal of most formal instruction: learners are taught in classrooms, laboratories, or training environments, but the measure of that instruction's success is whether they can perform in workplaces, clinical settings, or everyday life. The concept is central to learning science because instruction is always, to some degree, in a different context from application.

Transfer is typically classified along two dimensions:

- **Near versus far transfer**: Near transfer occurs when the new context closely resembles the training context (same task, slightly different conditions). Far transfer occurs when the new context is substantially different — different domain, format, or purpose.
- **Positive versus negative transfer**: Positive transfer means prior learning aids performance in the new context. Negative transfer means prior learning interferes with it (e.g., a trained driving habit that causes errors on the opposite side of the road).

## Key Principles

### 1. Transfer requires abstracted knowledge structures

The cognitivist account of transfer — dominant in the information-processing tradition — holds that transfer occurs when a learner applies a generalised schema or principle to a new instance. In this view, successful transfer depends on the learner having extracted abstract, context-independent representations from their training experience. [Cognitive load theory](cognitive-load-theory.md) operationalises this: schemas built in long-term memory provide the generalised structures that can be recruited across contexts. The richer and more abstract the schema, the wider the range of situations it can be applied to (see [Schema Theory](schema-theory.md)).

### 2. Transfer is enhanced by desirable difficulties

Conditions that make initial learning harder — spacing, interleaving, varied practice — reliably improve transfer even when they impair performance during training. [Bjork (1994)](../sources/bjork-1994.md) explains this through storage strength: desirable difficulties force learners to construct more robust and flexible memory traces that are less dependent on the specific training cues. [Interleaving](interleaving.md), in particular, forces learners to discriminate among different problem types, building the classification skills needed when problem types are not pre-identified — as they are not in real-world contexts.

### 3. Retrieval practice transfers beyond the practised items

A common misconception is that retrieval practice enhances only verbatim recall of studied material. [Roediger & Butler (2011)](../sources/roediger-butler-2011.md) and [Roediger, Putnam, & Smith (2011)](../sources/roediger-putnam-smith-2011.md) review evidence that retrieval practice improves performance on transfer tasks — novel questions, application problems, and tasks in different formats. The mechanism is that retrieval organises and integrates knowledge in ways that support flexible application, not just rote repetition. The [testing effect](testing-effect.md) thus benefits transfer as well as retention.

### 4. Generative processing supports transfer

[Wittrock (1974)](../sources/wittrock-1974.md) argued that transfer depends on the active construction of relationships between new information and existing knowledge (see [Generative Learning](generative-learning.md)). Passive encoding produces brittle, context-specific knowledge; generative processing produces knowledge that is tied to prior structures and can be activated in the contexts where those structures are relevant.

### 5. Situated cognition problematises transfer

The most fundamental challenge to the cognitivist account comes from [situated learning theory](situated-learning.md). [Lave and Wenger (1991)](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md) argue that knowledge is not context-independent information that can be picked up in one setting and applied in another. Rather, knowing is constituted in and by the contexts of practice. What a midwife knows is inseparable from the social, material, and cultural setting of midwifery — it is not an abstract schema that can simply be "transferred" to a new domain. On this view, transfer as traditionally conceived may be a largely fictitious construct: what looks like transfer is often re-learning in a new context, mediated by surface similarities, rather than genuine application of abstract principles.

This critique has practical implications: training designed on cognitivist assumptions (teach abstract principles, expect application) may fail when the target performance context differs substantially from training. Situated learning theory suggests the more effective design is to place learning in the authentic practice context from the outset.

### 6. The simulation transfer problem

[Simulation-based education](simulation-based-education.md) is predicated on transfer: skills practiced in simulation are expected to transfer to real clinical, aviation, or operational settings. The evidence is mixed and context-dependent. [Katz et al. (2020)](../sources/katz-et-al-2020.md) and [Wan & Lam (2019)](../sources/wan-lam-2019.md) document simulation-to-knowledge gains, but transfer of procedural skills from simulation to practice requires careful attention to fidelity matching and debriefing quality. Transfer from simulation is most reliable when (a) the simulation is functionally faithful to the real task, (b) deliberate practice with feedback occurs, and (c) spaced re-exposure is provided after the simulation.

## Evidence

The strongest evidence that deliberate training can produce generalised transfer comes from the [desirable difficulties](desirable-difficulties.md) literature. Bjork (1994) demonstrated systematically that interleaved and varied practice improves performance on final tests with novel items — a proxy for near-to-moderate transfer. [Rohrer (2012)](../sources/rohrer-2012.md) showed that interleaved mathematics practice produced transfer to new problem formats, whereas blocked practice did not.

The [retrieval practice](retrieval-practice.md) literature provides transfer evidence at a different level: Roediger, Putnam, & Smith (2011) review multiple studies showing that practice testing improves performance on transfer questions that were not directly tested — suggesting that retrieval reorganises knowledge in ways that generalise.

Lave and Wenger (1991) provide the counterpoint: their five apprenticeship cases show learners developing highly competent, contextually sophisticated knowledge through situated participation — knowledge that may not be reliably deployable outside that context. The situated view is reinforced by decades of schooling research showing that students often fail to apply mathematical or scientific knowledge outside the school context where it was taught.

## Tensions and Open Questions

The cognitivist and situated accounts of transfer are genuinely in tension, and neither fully explains the empirical record:

- If all knowledge is context-bound (situated view), how do we explain the reliable transfer effects found in laboratory studies with abstract problem-solving?
- If knowledge transfers via abstract schemas (cognitivist view), why do schooling studies so consistently find failure to transfer to real-world contexts?

A resolution may lie in the concept of **bridging**: when training explicitly teaches learners to map abstract principles onto diverse concrete situations — through varied examples, case-based reasoning, and authentic transfer tasks — the gap between context-bound knowing and abstract schema application is smaller. Neither pure decontextualised instruction nor context-only apprenticeship may be optimal.

## Applications

- **Vary training examples widely.** Transfer is maximised when training includes diverse instances of the target knowledge, forcing generalisation rather than pattern-matching to a single example type.
- **Use interleaving.** Mix problem types within practice sessions rather than blocking by category; this prepares learners for real-world situations where problem type is not signposted.
- **Include far-transfer tests in evaluation.** End-of-training performance tests measure near-transfer at best; include delayed tests with novel problem formats to evaluate whether instruction has produced durable, generalisable learning.
- **Situate practice in authentic contexts progressively.** Begin with explicit instruction of principles (cognitivist approach) but migrate toward application in authentic settings as competence grows — bridging the situated/cognitivist divide.
- **Debrief for transfer.** In simulation and training contexts, structured debriefing that explicitly connects simulation experience to real-world application is essential for supporting transfer.
- **Do not rely on spontaneous transfer.** Extensive evidence shows that transfer rarely occurs spontaneously without explicit support. Instruction should include explicit prompts to apply knowledge to new contexts.
