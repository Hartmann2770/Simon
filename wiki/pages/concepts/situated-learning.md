## Definition

**Situated learning** is the thesis that learning is fundamentally inseparable from the context — social, material, and cultural — in which it occurs. Cognition is not a process confined to the individual mind; it is distributed across persons, tools, and the settings of action. Learning, therefore, is not primarily the transfer of abstract knowledge into individual heads, but the process of becoming a participating member of a practice community embedded in a specific world.

The term was introduced by Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger in [*Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation* (1991)](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md), building on Lave's earlier anthropological work on everyday cognition.

## Key Principles

**1. Cognition is distributed, not contained.** Knowing is not a property of individuals; it is distributed across the social and material environment. What a midwife knows is not fully separable from the tools, relationships, locations, and social norms that constitute midwifery practice. Decontextualized knowledge is impoverished knowledge.

**2. Learning is changing participation.** The appropriate unit of analysis for learning is not the acquisition of a mental representation but the transformation of a person's participation in a community of practice. Learning is becoming: becoming a midwife, a sailor, a tailor, an alcoholic-in-recovery. This is the core move of [Lave and Wenger 1991](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md).

**3. Context is not a container; it is constitutive.** Context is not a backdrop against which learning happens — it actively constitutes what learning is and what is learned. The same "fact" carries different meanings, affordances, and uses in different practice settings. Situated learning theory holds that meaning is not transported from context to context; it is renegotiated in each.

**4. The curriculum is the structure of participation.** In situated learning, the sequence of tasks, the access to more competent others, and the organization of work constitute the learning curriculum. There is no need for a separate instructional sequence imposed on top of practice — the practice itself teaches, provided that legitimate peripheral participation is available. ([Lave and Wenger 1991](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md))

**5. Contrast with Bandura's social learning.** [Bandura (1977)](../sources/bandura-1977-social-learning-theory.md) also argued that learning is fundamentally observational and social, but located the mechanism in individual cognitive processes (attention, retention, reproduction, motivation). Situated learning theory rejects this cognitive-individualist framing: the social structure of participation — who has access to what activities, with whom, under what conditions — is not merely the vehicle for individual learning but constitutes the learning itself.

**6. Relationship to activity theory.** Situated learning shares with [activity theory](../concepts/activity-theory.md) the rejection of cognitivism and the emphasis on social practice as the unit of analysis. The difference is that activity theory uses a more explicit structural framework (the activity system, tools, rules, division of labor) and focuses on contradictions as drivers of change, while situated learning focuses on membership, identity, and participation trajectories.

## Evidence

The foundational evidence is the set of five apprenticeship cases in [Lave and Wenger 1991](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md): Yucatec midwives, Vai and Gola tailors, US Navy quartermasters, grocery store meat cutters, and Alcoholics Anonymous members. Across these diverse settings, the pattern of learning-as-changing-participation held, suggesting it is not a culturally specific artifact. The AA case is particularly striking: newcomers learn sobriety not by being instructed in principles but by participating in the meeting community, hearing stories, and gradually taking on speaking and sponsoring roles.

[Wenger's 1998 insurance claims processor fieldwork](../sources/wenger-1998-communities-of-practice.md) extends the evidence base into a formal organizational setting, showing that even in a highly bureaucratized environment, learning occurs primarily through participation in the informal practice community rather than through official training.

[Akther and Georgesen (2011)](../sources/akther-georgesen-2011-telecentres.md) show that digital literacy in rural Bangladesh follows a situated learning pattern: new users learn by watching, then assisting, then independently using telecentres — replicating the LPP dynamic in a technology adoption context.

## Applications

**Apprenticeship design.** Situated learning argues that the most effective learning environments are authentic practice settings with graded access to full participation. Vocational education, clinical training, and professional practice programs should be designed around legitimate engagement in real tasks, not simulation-only environments.

**School reform.** Traditional schooling creates what Lave and Wenger call a "school practice" — students learn to participate in schools, not in the practices schools ostensibly prepare them for. Project-based learning, internships, and community-embedded education attempt to reconnect school practice to genuine communities of practice.

**Digital learning environments.** Online learning designs benefit from creating genuine communities of practice around content domains — not just courses where individuals complete tasks in parallel, but communities where members help each other, share resources, and develop a shared repertoire.

**Transfer.** Situated learning theory problematizes the transfer of learning: knowledge acquired in one context does not automatically transfer to another because its meaning is context-constituted. This has implications for training design — if you want workers to apply knowledge in a specific context, situate the learning in that context or create authentic bridges.
