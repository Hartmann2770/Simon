## Definition

The activity system model is Engeström's triangular diagram representing the structural components of a collective activity and their interrelations. It provides a visual and conceptual tool for analyzing any purposeful human activity as a system — making visible both the structural elements and the tensions (contradictions) that drive change. It is the central analytical unit of third-generation activity theory.

The model expands Vygotsky's original subject–tool–object triangle by adding three additional social elements: community, rules, and division of labour.

## Key Principles

**The six nodes of the activity system:**

1. **Subject** — the individual(s) or group whose perspective is taken in the analysis; the actors engaged in the activity.
2. **Object** — the raw material or problem space that the activity is oriented toward; what is being transformed. The object carries the motive and gives the activity its direction and meaning.
3. **Tools (Instruments)** — the material and symbolic artifacts used to work on the object. These mediate the subject–object relation.
4. **Community** — the collective that shares the same general object; the social context in which the activity takes place.
5. **Rules** — explicit and implicit norms, conventions, and regulations that govern the activity within the community.
6. **Division of Labour** — the distribution of tasks, roles, and responsibilities among members of the community in pursuit of the shared object.

**The triangular structure:**
- The upper sub-triangle (subject–tools–object) represents individual mediated action.
- The lower nodes (community, rules, division of labour) represent the social context.
- Together, they form one system: changes in any node propagate to others.

**The outcome:** The activity system produces outcomes (transformations of the object). The same object can yield different outcomes depending on how the system is configured.

**Multiple interacting systems:** In third-generation AT, the unit of analysis extends to networks of two or more activity systems interacting — sharing an object, or with boundary objects that mediate between them. This enables analysis of inter-organizational learning and boundary crossing.

## Evidence

[Engeström (1999)](../sources/engestrom-1999-individual-social-transformation.md) presents the theoretical justification for the systemic model: individual mediated action is too narrow a unit; the full activity system captures the historically layered social context. [Engeström (2001)](../sources/engestrom-2001-expansive-learning-at-work.md) uses the model to explain expansive learning — the cycle that transforms the activity system itself. [Engeström (2005)](../sources/engestrom-2005-developmental-work-research.md) documents its application in dozens of Change Laboratory interventions.

[Barab et al. (2002)](../sources/barab-et-al-2002-activity-theory-astronomy.md) provide one of the strongest empirical demonstrations: the activity system model reveals four types of contradictions operating simultaneously in a technology-rich university course — contradictions that standard HCI usability analysis or cognitive task analysis would miss entirely.

[Nardi (1996)](../sources/nardi-1996-context-and-consciousness.md) and [Kaptelinin, Nardi, and Macaulay (1999)](../sources/kaptelinin-nardi-macaulay-1999-activity-checklist.md) apply the systemic model in HCI contexts, showing that the six-node analysis prevents design from focusing only on the subject–tool–object sub-triangle while ignoring community, rules, and division of labour.

[Avis (2009)](../sources/avis-2009-transformation-or-transformism.md) critiques the model for not adequately representing power relations and structural inequalities that cannot be captured as "tensions" within a system.

## Applications

- **Educational analysis**: Map a course or learning programme as an activity system to identify misalignments between tools, rules, community expectations, and division of labour. A common pattern: instructors design around the subject–tools–object triangle (content delivery) while ignoring the community norms and rules that determine how students actually engage.
- **Technology design evaluation**: Engeström's model is used as a design evaluation framework to ask not just "does the tool work?" but "how does this tool fit into the activity system, and what secondary contradictions will it introduce?"
- **Organizational learning**: The Change Laboratory uses the activity system diagram as a collective mirror — showing workers a model of their own activity system to prompt reflection and redesign.
- **Research design**: Formulating a study as an analysis of an activity system ensures that individual behaviour is always situated in its social and institutional context, preventing both over-individualization and over-socialization of explanations.
