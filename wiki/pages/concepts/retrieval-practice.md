## Definition

Retrieval practice is the deliberate act of attempting to recall information from memory without immediate access to the source material. It is distinguished from passive re-reading or restudying by the requirement to reconstruct knowledge from internal memory stores. Retrieval practice is the active mechanism that produces the [testing effect](testing-effect.md): the strong, reliable advantage in long-term retention that tests and recall exercises produce relative to passive study.

## Key Principles

1. **Retrieval is a memory modifier, not just a read-out.** Each time a memory is successfully retrieved, it becomes more retrievable in the future. Retrieval is not neutral — it changes the underlying memory trace. This is the core insight of retrieval-based learning theory.

2. **Effortful retrieval produces stronger benefits.** The more difficult it is to retrieve information — the more "work" the retrieval requires — the greater the long-term benefit. This is related to the [desirable difficulties](desirable-difficulties.md) framework. Free recall (generating answers without cues) produces larger effects than cued recall, which produces larger effects than recognition.

3. **Successful and unsuccessful retrieval both matter.** Successful retrieval strengthens the memory trace. Unsuccessful retrieval (followed by corrective feedback) also enhances subsequent retention more than restudying, partly because errors highlight knowledge gaps and the subsequent correction is encoded more deeply.

4. **Formats of retrieval practice** that work well include: free recall, practice testing and quizzing, cued recall (answering questions), generating the material from memory before review, and oral recitation. All outperform passive restudying, though free recall tends to produce the largest effects.

5. **Mechanism — episodic context account.** [Whiffen & Karpicke (2017)](../sources/whiffen-karpicke-2017.md) provide evidence that retrieval works partly by requiring learners to reinstate the original learning context, creating richer episodic memory traces and enhanced temporal organisation of stored knowledge.

6. **Retrieval promotes transfer.** Retrieval practice does not merely enhance verbatim recall — it also improves performance on transfer tasks that require applying knowledge in new contexts. This challenges the characterisation of testing as a rote-memorisation tool.

## Evidence

[Karpicke & Roediger (2007)](../sources/karpicke-roediger-2007.md) demonstrated the key result: repeated retrieval of recalled items more than doubled one-week retention compared to dropping items from further practice, while repeated studying produced no comparable benefit. [Roediger & Karpicke (2006b)](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006b.md) showed the effect with educationally realistic prose passages.

[Nunes & Karpicke (2015)](../sources/nunes-karpicke-2015.md) provide a comprehensive review of retrieval-based learning research, tracing its history and theoretical development. [Roediger & Butler (2011)](../sources/roediger-butler-2011.md) review mechanisms and moderators.

[Bae, Therriault, & Redifer (2019)](../sources/bae-therriault-redifer-2019.md) established that among retrieval formats, free recall and practice quizzing are most effective, and that combining strategies amplifies benefits.

[Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) rated practice testing as "high utility" — the top-rated strategy among ten reviewed.

## Applications

- Self-testing with flashcards (especially spaced-repetition systems like Anki) is the most scalable individual application.
- Practice problems and quizzes in homework replace re-reading as the default study activity.
- "Brain dump" exercises — writing everything you can remember about a topic before reviewing — generate high-effort free recall.
- Interleaved quizzing across topics prevents the blocked practice that confines retrieval to recently-encountered material.
- Pre-tests before instruction expose learners to questions they cannot yet answer; subsequent instruction answers those questions, producing deeper encoding.
