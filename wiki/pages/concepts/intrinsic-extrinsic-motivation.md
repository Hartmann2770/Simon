## Definition

**Intrinsic motivation** is engagement in an activity for its own sake — because it is inherently interesting, enjoyable, or personally meaningful. **Extrinsic motivation** is engagement driven by separable outcomes: rewards, grades, approval, or avoidance of punishment. The distinction matters for learning because intrinsically motivated learners show deeper processing, greater persistence, more creativity, and better long-term retention than extrinsically motivated learners doing the same tasks.

## Key Principles

### The Motivation Continuum (Self-Determination Theory)

Ryan and Deci's Self-Determination Theory reframes the intrinsic/extrinsic binary as a continuum of **autonomy**:

- **External regulation**: doing something to get a reward or avoid punishment (fully controlled)
- **Introjection**: doing something from internal pressure (shame, guilt) — still controlled
- **Identification**: doing something because it is personally valued, even if not inherently enjoyable — partially autonomous
- **Integration**: doing something because it is fully assimilated into identity and values — fully autonomous
- **Intrinsic motivation**: doing something for the pure enjoyment of the activity itself

Higher autonomy on this continuum predicts better learning outcomes, more persistence, and greater well-being.

### Three Basic Psychological Needs

Intrinsic motivation and autonomous extrinsic motivation are supported by three basic psychological needs:

- **Autonomy**: the sense that one's actions are self-chosen and volitional
- **Competence**: the sense of being effective and capable
- **Relatedness**: the sense of meaningful connection to others

Educational environments that satisfy these needs foster intrinsic motivation; environments that thwart them undermine it.

### Expectancy-Value Model (Eccles and Wigfield)

Eccles and Wigfield's model ([eccles-wigfield-2002-motivational-beliefs](../sources/eccles-wigfield-2002-motivational-beliefs.md)) adds a second framework: learners engage with tasks they **expect to succeed at** AND that they **value**. Task value has four components:
- **Intrinsic value**: inherent interest/enjoyment
- **Attainment value**: importance to identity
- **Utility value**: perceived usefulness for future goals
- **Cost**: what is given up or risked by engaging

### The Undermining Effect of Rewards

A major empirical finding: **contingent rewards** (especially salient, expected rewards for intrinsically interesting activities) reliably undermine intrinsic motivation. This is one of the most replicated findings in motivational psychology. The mechanism: rewards shift the perceived locus of causality from internal ("I do this because I want to") to external ("I do this for the reward"), which reduces autonomy.

## Evidence

- [Ryan and Deci (2000)](../sources/ryan-deci-2000-intrinsic-extrinsic.md): Core SDT review — defines the motivation continuum, documents the undermining effect of controlling contexts, identifies autonomy support as key to maintaining intrinsic motivation.
- [Eccles and Wigfield (2002)](../sources/eccles-wigfield-2002-motivational-beliefs.md): Expectancy-value theory — shows that both expectation of success and perceived value independently predict engagement and achievement.
- [Clear (2018)](../sources/clear-atomic-habits.md): Habit formation perspective — identity-based habits as a form of fully internalized motivation; the four laws of behavior change as a practical framework for sustaining learning behaviors.

## Applications

1. **Design for choice**: giving learners meaningful options (topics, formats, pacing) satisfies the autonomy need and supports intrinsic motivation.
2. **Communicate relevance**: explicitly connecting content to learners' identities and future goals increases utility and attainment value.
3. **Calibrate challenge**: tasks that are too easy produce boredom (low competence challenge); tasks that are too hard produce anxiety (competence threat). The zone of proximal difficulty supports competence need satisfaction.
4. **Minimize controlling rewards**: if rewards are used, use informational rewards (recognizing genuine achievement) rather than controlling rewards (contingent on compliance). Unexpected rewards are less damaging than expected, contingent ones.
5. **Build community**: relatedness is supported by collaborative learning, mentoring relationships, and learning communities — connect to [Communities of Practice](communities-of-practice.md).
