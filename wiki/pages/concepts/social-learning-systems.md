## Definition

A **social learning system** is a configuration of interconnected communities of practice that collectively constitute the learning capacity of an organization, field, or society. The concept, developed by Etienne Wenger in his [2000 article](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md) and elaborated with Snyder in [2010](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md), holds that the unit of systemic learning is not the individual, the organization, or even the single community of practice, but the network of communities and the connections between them.

## Key Principles

**1. Learning capacity resides in connected communities.** No single community of practice has access to all the knowledge relevant to complex contemporary challenges. Systemic learning requires the healthy functioning of multiple CoPs and the mechanisms that connect them. Organizational performance depends not on individual or team competence alone, but on the quality of the social learning system as a whole. ([Wenger 2000](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md))

**2. Three modes of belonging.** [Wenger (2000)](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md) identifies three ways individuals relate to communities in a social learning system:
- *Engagement*: direct participation in joint activities — the most common understanding of CoP membership
- *Imagination*: building an image of the community and one's place within it through reflection, stories, and analogy, even without direct engagement; enables relating to distant communities and forming identity as part of a larger system
- *Alignment*: coordinating actions with broader structures, standards, and enterprises extending beyond the immediate community; enables large-scale coordination but risks bureaucratic rigidity if decoupled from engagement

**3. Boundary mechanisms.** The connections between communities in a social learning system depend on two key mechanisms ([Wenger 2000](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md), [Wenger 1998](../sources/wenger-1998-communities-of-practice.md)):
- *Boundary objects*: artifacts, documents, standards, or concepts plastic enough to satisfy the needs of different communities, enabling coordination without full translation (e.g., a patient record that serves both doctors and administrators)
- *Brokers*: people who have partial membership in multiple communities and can translate knowledge, practices, and perspectives across their boundaries; brokering is skilled, risky work — brokers can be suspected of betrayal by all parties

**4. Three levels of systemic learning.** [Snyder and Wenger (2010)](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md) identify conditions required at three levels for social learning systems to address complex challenges:
- *CoP level*: individual practice communities must be healthy, generative, and domain-committed
- *Inter-community level*: boundary crossing, brokering, and knowledge exchange must be enabled
- *Institutional level*: policies, infrastructure, and legitimacy structures must support CoPs without over-formalizing them

**5. Conditions for systemic learning.** Effective social learning systems require: **legitimacy** (CoPs are taken seriously by decision-makers), **connectivity** (communities are linked to others), **resources** (time, facilitation, tools), and **diversity** (multiple perspectives are represented). ([Snyder and Wenger 2010](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md))

**6. Value creation at multiple levels.** [Wenger, Trayner, and De Laat (2011)](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md) demonstrate that social learning systems generate value simultaneously at five levels: immediate experience, accumulated potential, changed practice, realized performance outcomes, and reframed understanding of what matters. Evaluation frameworks must capture all levels to avoid short-termism.

## Evidence

The theoretical base is primarily Wenger's ([Wenger 2000](../sources/wenger-2000-cop-social-learning-systems.md), [2010](../sources/wenger-2010-career-of-a-concept.md)), with empirical grounding from case studies in Dutch educational and police networks ([Wenger, Trayner, De Laat 2011](../sources/wenger-trayner-laat-2011-value-creation.md)) and international development contexts ([Snyder and Wenger 2010](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md)). The framework has been applied in healthcare networks, sustainability initiatives, agricultural extension systems, and inter-organizational professional learning programs.

## Applications

**Organizational learning design.** Rather than targeting learning interventions at individuals or formal teams, social learning systems thinking focuses on cultivating the communities and the inter-community connections that constitute the real learning infrastructure of an organization.

**Knowledge management.** The boundary objects and broker concept provides a practical vocabulary for knowledge management: what artifacts connect which communities? Who are the brokers? Are they resourced and legitimized?

**Educational reform.** National or regional education improvement is a social learning system problem, not a school-by-school problem. Reform efforts must attend to the health of teaching practice communities, the mechanisms connecting schools, and the institutional conditions that support or undermine those communities.

**Global challenges.** [Snyder and Wenger (2010)](../sources/snyder-wenger-2010-world-as-learning-system.md) argue that addressing sustainability, climate, and development challenges requires building functional social learning systems across national and sectoral boundaries — a design challenge at a new scale.
