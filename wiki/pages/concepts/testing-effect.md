## Definition

The testing effect (also called the retrieval practice effect or test-enhanced learning) is the finding that retrieving information from memory — taking a test, doing a recall exercise — produces substantially greater long-term retention than an equivalent amount of time spent restudying the same material. The effect has been documented in experimental research since at least the early twentieth century, but received its major modern treatment from Roediger and Karpicke beginning in the mid-2000s.

## Key Principles

1. **Retrieval strengthens memory.** The very act of trying to retrieve information reinforces and reconstructs the memory trace in ways that passive re-exposure does not. This is not simply about feedback or error correction — testing without feedback still outperforms restudying.

2. **Delayed tests reveal the advantage.** On tests administered shortly after learning, restudying often matches or beats testing. The superiority of testing becomes pronounced at delays of days, weeks, and months — exactly the timescales that matter for durable learning.

3. **Free recall outperforms recognition.** Generating an answer from scratch (free recall) produces larger testing effects than recognition-format tests, because the retrieval effort is greater. The more effortful the retrieval, the stronger the benefit.

4. **Feedback amplifies the effect.** Corrective feedback after testing — knowing whether your response was correct and what the right answer was — potentiates the testing effect, particularly for errors.

5. **Multiple tests outperform single tests.** Repeated retrieval practice (especially spread over time) produces multiplicative benefits rather than diminishing returns.

6. **Metacognitive illusion of restudying.** Students consistently report feeling more confident about material they have restudied — but this feeling is misleading. Restudying produces *fluency*, not *retention*.

## Evidence

The foundational empirical demonstration is [Roediger & Karpicke (2006b)](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006b.md): students who took immediate free-recall tests on prose passages showed far better retention after 1 week than students who restudied the same passages, even though restudiers felt more confident. [Karpicke & Roediger (2007)](../sources/karpicke-roediger-2007.md) showed that repeated retrieval of already-recalled items produced >100% retention advantage over repeated studying.

The [Roediger & Karpicke (2006a)](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006a.md) review synthesised evidence for the phenomenon's breadth, while [Roediger, Putnam, & Smith (2011)](../sources/roediger-putnam-smith-2011.md) catalogued ten distinct pathways through which testing benefits learning — covering not just retention but organisation, transfer, metacognitive calibration, and anxiety reduction.

A meta-analysis by [Sundararajan, Adesope, & Trevisan (2017)](../sources/sundararajan-adesope-trevisan-2017.md) confirmed large, consistent effect sizes for practice testing over restudying and identified moderators (test format, feedback, age). [Polack & Miller (2022)](../sources/polack-miller-2022.md) extended the evidence base to non-human animals, arguing the testing effect is a fundamental property of memory systems.

[Bae, Therriault, & Redifer (2019)](../sources/bae-therriault-redifer-2019.md) showed that free recall and practice quizzing are the most effective retrieval formats, and that combining strategies (e.g., test-generation + free recall) enhances performance. [Glover](../sources/glover-testing-phenomenon.md) documented the effect's historical neglect, and [Binks (2018)](../sources/binks-2018.md) reviews applications in professional nursing education.

[Dunlosky et al. (2013)](../sources/dunlosky-et-al-2013.md) gave practice testing the highest "utility" rating of all ten learning strategies they evaluated.

## Applications

- **Low-stakes quizzing** embedded in courses — weekly or end-of-lesson quizzes improve retention even without grading.
- **Self-testing** with flashcards, practice problems, or free-recall attempts before reviewing notes.
- **Spaced repetition software** (Anki, SuperMemo) automates the scheduling of retrieval practice based on predicted forgetting curves.
- **Formative assessment** in classrooms — frequent, low-stakes tests calibrate both teacher and student understanding.
- **Pre-testing** (testing before instruction) can improve learning by activating relevant knowledge and creating "desirable errors" that prime receptivity to correct information.
