## Definition

Multimedia learning principles are a set of evidence-based design guidelines for creating instructional materials that combine words and pictures (or other media combinations). Developed primarily by Richard Mayer and grounded in his *Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning* (CTML), the principles specify how to design multimedia content so that it works *with* rather than against the architecture of human memory.

## Key Principles

Mayer identifies twelve principles (with overlapping support from Sweller's CLT). The most important are:

1. **Multimedia principle.** People learn better from words and pictures together than from words alone. Images are not decoration — they carry independent cognitive value when paired with relevant verbal content.

2. **Spatial contiguity principle.** Words and their corresponding graphics should be placed close together rather than on separate pages or screens. This eliminates the split-attention cost of integrating physically distant elements.

3. **Temporal contiguity principle.** Corresponding narration and animation should be presented simultaneously rather than successively. Sequential presentation forces the learner to hold one in working memory while waiting for the other.

4. **Coherence principle.** People learn better when extraneous words, pictures, and sounds are excluded. Adding interesting but irrelevant content (background music, extended anecdotes) increases extraneous load and impairs learning.

5. **Signalling principle.** Adding cues that highlight the organisation of essential material (headings, arrows, highlights, outlines) helps learners select and organise relevant information.

6. **Redundancy principle.** People learn better from graphics and narration than from graphics, narration, and on-screen text simultaneously. When narration and text are identical, processing the text wastes capacity without adding information.

7. **Modality principle.** People learn better from narration and graphics than from on-screen text and graphics. Spoken words use the auditory channel; printed words compete with graphics for the visual channel.

8. **Segmenting principle.** People learn better when a complex animation is presented in learner-paced segments rather than as a continuous unit. Segmenting allows time for sub-schemas to form before new material adds to load.

9. **Pre-training principle.** People learn better from a multimedia lesson when they first receive instruction in the names and characteristics of key components. Pre-training builds the vocabulary schema that supports subsequent integration.

10. **Personalisation principle.** People learn better when words are in conversational rather than formal style. First- and second-person language promotes deeper cognitive engagement.

## Evidence

The principles are each supported by a dedicated body of experimental research — primarily Mayer's own lab, published across decades. [Mayer & Moreno](../sources/mayer-moreno-cognitive-load-multimedia.md) provide the most comprehensive single synthesis, linking each design intervention to its experimental support and specifying which source of cognitive overload it addresses.

[Mayer (2010)](../sources/mayer-2010-science-of-learning-medical-education.md) demonstrates that the same principles apply in medical education, with similarly sized effects.

The theoretical grounding in [Sweller (2011)](../sources/sweller-2011-cognitive-load-theory.md) provides the cognitive-architecture explanation for *why* each principle works: every violation of a multimedia principle corresponds to an unnecessary increase in extraneous cognitive load.

## Applications

- **E-learning design.** Replace on-screen text narration with graphics + narration; remove background music; place labels immediately adjacent to diagram elements.
- **Video instruction.** Use segmenting (pause points) in instructional videos; avoid slide designs where the speaker simply reads the text on screen.
- **Slide design.** Minimise text on slides shown alongside narration; use visuals that carry information not in the verbal channel.
- **Medical and professional training.** Apply pre-training before complex case simulations; use modality and contiguity principles for clinical procedure demonstrations.
