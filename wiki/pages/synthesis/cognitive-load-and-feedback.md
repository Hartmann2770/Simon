## Question

How does cognitive load theory interact with feedback? When does feedback help learning, and when does it hinder it from a cognitive load perspective?

## Analysis

The relationship between cognitive load and feedback is more nuanced than either concept suggests alone. They interact at three key points.

### Feedback can increase or decrease cognitive load

[Hattie & Timperley's (2007)](../sources/hattie-timperley-2007-power-of-feedback.md) four feedback levels have different load profiles:

- **Task-level feedback** ("Your answer is incorrect because X") has low cognitive load — it corrects a specific error. But it also produces shallow learning.
- **Process-level feedback** ("Try connecting these two concepts before drawing a conclusion") imposes *germane* cognitive load — the productive kind that builds schemas ([Sweller 2011](../sources/sweller-2011-cognitive-load-theory.md)). This is the highest-impact feedback type.
- **Self-regulation feedback** ("How would you check whether your answer makes sense?") adds metacognitive load but develops the learner's capacity to monitor their own cognitive state ([Flavell 1979](../sources/flavell-1979.md)).

The design challenge: process-level feedback produces better learning but demands more working memory. If the learner is already at capacity (high intrinsic load from complex material), process-level feedback may push them into overload.

### Feedback timing is a desirable difficulty

[Bjork (1994)](../sources/bjork-1994.md) identifies **reducing feedback immediacy** as a canonical [desirable difficulty](../concepts/desirable-difficulties.md). Immediate feedback reduces cognitive load during practice (the learner doesn't have to hold uncertainty in working memory), but delayed feedback forces retrieval and self-monitoring — producing stronger long-term retention.

This creates a direct tension with [CLT](../concepts/cognitive-load-theory.md): from a cognitive load perspective, immediate feedback is better because it prevents error consolidation and reduces extraneous load. From a desirable difficulties perspective, delayed feedback is better because the struggle builds storage strength.

The resolution comes from **phase sensitivity** ([Hattie & Donoghue 2016](../sources/hattie-donoghue-2016-learning-strategies.md)): during early surface learning, when intrinsic load is high, immediate task-level feedback prevents overload. During deeper learning, when schemas are forming, delayed process-level feedback introduces productive difficulty.

### Feedback must match learner expertise

CLT's **expertise reversal effect** applies directly to feedback. Novices benefit from:
- Immediate, task-level corrective feedback (reduces extraneous load)
- Worked examples with embedded feedback

Experts are harmed by the same approach — it becomes redundant load. They benefit from:
- Delayed, process-level feedback
- Self-regulation prompts that develop [metacognitive](../concepts/metacognition.md) monitoring
- Less frequent feedback that forces self-evaluation

### Phase-matched feedback model

| Learner stage | Cognitive load state | Optimal feedback |
|---|---|---|
| Novice (high intrinsic load) | Near capacity | Immediate, task-level, corrective |
| Developing (moderate load) | Room for germane load | Process-level, slightly delayed |
| Advanced (low intrinsic load) | Capacity available | Self-regulation prompts, delayed, infrequent |

## Conclusion

Feedback is not inherently good or bad for learning — it depends on whether the cognitive load it introduces is germane (schema-building) or extraneous (noise). The same feedback given to a novice and an expert can flip from helpful to harmful. The practical resolution is phase-matched feedback: immediate and corrective for novices under high load, delayed and process-oriented for developing learners with cognitive capacity to spare, and self-regulatory for advanced learners who benefit from the desirable difficulty of self-monitoring.
