## Citation

Hirabayashi, K., & Jain, N. (2024). Harvard undergraduate survey on generative AI. Preprint/report. http://arxiv.org/abs/2406.00833

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/DPVBX2UQ/attachment/CYF2W9Z3/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Survey of Harvard undergraduates documenting actual patterns of generative AI use across academic disciplines, study habits, and social contexts.
2. A substantial majority of Harvard undergraduates reported using generative AI tools (primarily ChatGPT) for academic work — the rate was higher than most faculty expected and higher than publicly acknowledged by students.
3. **Use patterns vary by discipline**: AI use is highest in STEM fields for code generation and debugging; in humanities, it is more commonly used for brainstorming, outlining, and editing rather than wholesale text generation.
4. Students report a gap between their private AI use and their understanding of institutional policies: many used AI in contexts they were uncertain about, reflecting the lag between technological adoption and policy development.
5. **Confidence and self-efficacy** effects: students who used AI reported higher confidence in their work but mixed effects on perceived learning — some felt AI use enhanced understanding (through explanation and feedback); others felt it reduced effort and learning depth.
6. Students overwhelmingly favor explicit instruction in AI literacy and guidelines for appropriate use over blanket bans.

## Methodology

Survey study of Harvard undergraduate students. Quantitative and qualitative self-report data. Sample characteristics and methodology described in report. Conducted 2024.

## Relevance

Empirical data on how students actually use AI in higher education, bridging the gap between policy debates and on-the-ground reality. Relevant to AI literacy, assessment design, and institutional policy discussions.
