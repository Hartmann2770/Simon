## Citation

Rohrer, D. (2012). Interleaving helps students distinguish among similar concepts. *Educational Psychology Review, 24*(3), 355–367. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-012-9201-3

## Key Findings

1. When students encounter sets of similar concepts or problems, blocked practice (all exposures to one concept grouped together) reduces discrimination learning — students have no need to distinguish between concepts when only one is present at a time.
2. Interleaved practice (exposures to different concepts mixed together) forces learners to select the appropriate strategy or concept for each item, improving discrimination and final test performance.
3. Multiple experiments comparing interleaving and blocking found consistent advantages for interleaved conditions on delayed tests.
4. The mechanism is "discrimination learning" — interleaving requires students to identify which category a problem belongs to, a skill that blocked practice never demands.
5. Evidence was limited at the time of publication, and ecologically valid classroom studies were still needed — but a prudent reading suggests at least a portion of practice should be interleaved.

## Methodology

Empirical review and theoretical analysis drawing on multiple experiments, including mathematics problem-solving paradigms with middle-school students. The paper identifies the discrimination-learning account as the primary mechanism explaining interleaving benefits.

## Relevance

The most focused treatment of *why* interleaving works — the discrimination account complements more general desirable-difficulties explanations. It is particularly relevant for mathematics and science education, where students must learn to identify which procedure applies to which type of problem, and for any domain where similar categories must be distinguished.
