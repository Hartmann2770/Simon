## Citation

Gergen, K. J. (2001). *Social Construction in Context*. SAGE Publications.

## Key Findings

1. **Social constructionism** holds that knowledge, meaning, and social reality are not discovered but actively constructed through social processes — language, discourse, relational practices, and cultural conventions.
2. What counts as "real," "true," or "rational" varies across cultures and historical periods; these categories are sustained by social agreement rather than correspondence to mind-independent facts.
3. Knowledge is irreducibly relational: it is generated in and through social relationships and practices, not produced by solitary minds encountering an objective world.
4. Constructionism has implications for education: it challenges transmission models (knowledge as objective content to be delivered) in favor of dialogic and participatory models (knowledge as co-constructed in interaction).
5. The book extends constructionist analysis beyond the individual and dyad to institutions, communities, and global processes — showing how social construction operates at multiple scales.
6. Critical awareness of social construction is itself transformative: recognizing that current ways of knowing are historically contingent opens space for new forms of inquiry and practice.

## Methodology

Theoretical monograph. Philosophical and sociological analysis with illustrative examples. No primary empirical data.

## Relevance

Provides the philosophical underpinning for constructivist learning theory and for understanding communities of practice as sites of knowledge construction. Connects to Vygotsky's social constructivism and Lave and Wenger's situated learning theory.
