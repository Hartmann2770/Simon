## Citation

Bandura, Albert. *Social Learning Theory*. Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice-Hall, 1977. ISBN: 978-0-13-816751-6.

## Key Findings

1. Much human learning occurs through **observation** rather than direct experience: people learn behaviors, attitudes, and emotional reactions by watching others and noting the consequences of their actions. This vicarious learning is efficient because it allows individuals to acquire complex behaviors without the risks and costs of trial-and-error.
2. Bandura's model of observational learning identifies four subprocesses: **attention** (the observer must attend to the modeled behavior), **retention** (the behavior must be encoded in memory), **reproduction** (the observer must be able to replicate the behavior), and **motivation** (the observer must have reason to do so). A deficiency in any process breaks the learning chain.
3. **Reciprocal determinism** is the central theoretical claim: behavior, person (cognitive factors, beliefs, self-perceptions), and environment are mutually determining. Learning is not a one-way effect of environment on behavior but a continuous interaction among all three.
4. Social learning is not purely imitative: **cognitive mediation** is essential. Learners do not passively copy what they see; they form internal representations, anticipate outcomes, and regulate their own behavior based on self-evaluation against internalized standards.
5. **Self-efficacy** — a person's belief in their own ability to execute the behaviors required to produce specific outcomes — is a central determinant of whether observed behaviors are adopted and whether learning leads to performance. High self-efficacy increases persistence in the face of difficulty; low self-efficacy leads to avoidance.
6. **Reinforcement** in social learning theory operates primarily through anticipated and vicarious outcomes rather than only through direct reward: seeing a model rewarded increases imitation; seeing a model punished decreases it.
7. Bandura distinguishes **learning** (acquiring a behavior) from **performance** (enacting it): people often know how to do things they do not do, because motivational and situational factors determine performance independently of acquisition.

## Methodology

Theoretical monograph integrating and extending Bandura's experimental program, including the classic Bobo doll studies on aggression modeling. Synthesizes experimental evidence on observational learning with new theoretical apparatus including reciprocal determinism and self-efficacy.

## Relevance

Bandura's social learning theory is the behaviorist-adjacent precursor to situated learning and communities of practice — it shares the insight that learning is deeply social and observational, but situates this within a cognitive-behavioral rather than sociocultural framework. Understanding Bandura is important for understanding what Lave and Wenger were reacting against: they accepted that learning is social and observational but rejected the individualist cognitive framing, arguing that the social structure of participation — not cognitive modeling — is the primary unit of analysis. Self-efficacy theory, however, has had lasting independent influence on educational design: learners need not only opportunity to observe but also experiences that build confidence in their own competence.
