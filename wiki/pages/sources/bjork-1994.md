## Citation

Bjork, R. A. (1994). Memory and metamemory considerations in the training of human beings. In J. Metcalfe & A. P. Shimamura (Eds.), *Metacognition: Knowing About Knowing* (pp. 185–206). MIT Press. https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/4561.003.0011

## Key Findings

1. Conditions that make acquisition more difficult and slow often produce better long-term retention and transfer — Bjork names these "desirable difficulties."
2. Examples of desirable difficulties include: spacing study sessions (versus massing them), interleaving practice (versus blocking), reducing feedback frequency, varying practice conditions, and testing rather than restudying.
3. Learners and trainers are often misled by performance during training: easy, massed practice produces fast short-term gains but poor long-term retention; difficult, spaced practice produces slower apparent progress but far better long-term outcomes.
4. The concept of "current-performance illusion" describes how instructors optimise for surface-level training metrics rather than durable learning.
5. Metamemory (one's knowledge and beliefs about one's own memory) is systematically biased: people misjudge fluency as understanding and overestimate their retention when material feels easy.

## Methodology

Theoretical book chapter drawing on the New Theory of Disuse and a range of experimental evidence to articulate a principled account of why training conditions that seem worse often produce better long-term outcomes.

## Relevance

The chapter that coined and systematically developed the term "desirable difficulties." It remains the primary reference for the concept. Its emphasis on the gap between training performance and actual retention is fundamental for applied learning design, military training, sports coaching, and educational psychology.
