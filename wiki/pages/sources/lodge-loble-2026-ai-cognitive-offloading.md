## Key Findings

1. The report identifies a profound new challenge: widespread AI use in schooling risks students *offloading* cognitive work that is essential for establishing durable knowledge, skills, and thinking capacity.
2. **Cognitive offloading** — delegating cognitive processing to external tools — is not inherently harmful; humans have always used tools (writing, calculators) to reduce working memory load. The problem is when offloading *replaces* the effortful processing that produces learning rather than supplementing it.
3. CLT predicts that reducing extraneous load via AI assistance could, in principle, *free* working memory for germane processing. But if AI completes the very tasks (problem-solving, writing, retrieval) through which learning occurs, schemas do not form in long-term memory.
4. Evidence shows that AI assistance can produce short-circuit effects: students appear to perform better in the moment (assisted by AI) but retain less and transfer less because the cognitive struggle that builds long-term memory has been eliminated.
5. The report argues for an *intentional design* approach: AI tools in education must be designed and deployed to augment effortful learning rather than replace it, preserving the desirable difficulties that build lasting capability.
6. Policy implications include: teacher guidance on when AI assistance is productive vs. counterproductive; assessment design that cannot be trivially completed by AI; and explicit student education about cognitive offloading trade-offs.

## Methodology

Research report produced for University of Technology Sydney. Synthesises CLT, cognitive offloading literature, AI-in-education research, and empirical studies on AI use in learning contexts.

## Relevance

Highly timely analysis connecting CLT and desirable difficulties to the emerging challenge of generative AI in education. Extends the cognitive load framework to a new context and has direct implications for instructional design and learning policy.
