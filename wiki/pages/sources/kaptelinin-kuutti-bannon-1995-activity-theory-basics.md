## Citation

Kaptelinin, Victor, Kari Kuutti, and Liam Bannon. "Activity Theory: Basic Concepts and Applications." In *Human-Computer Interaction*, 189–201. 1995. DOI: 10.1007/3-86541-069-3. URL: http://dx.doi.org/10.1007/3-540-60614-9_14.

## Abstract (from source)

> The objective of the tutorial is to introduce attendees to Activity Theory, a general theoretical framework for the analysis of human and communal action in the world. After an overview of the theory, focus shifts to how this framework can be utilized in practice. Some examples are shown of how this framework can provide a fresh perspective on certain extant problems in the fields of HCI and CSCW. Hopefully, participants become more aware of the nature and complexity of current controversies concerning the role of theory in the design of computer artefacts. By the end of the tutorial, participants should be able to understand the basic principles of the approach, and to describe their work activities in ways illuminated by this framework.

## Key Findings

1. Activity theory provides a unified framework for analyzing human action that bridges individual cognitive processes and collective social practices, making it well suited to both HCI and CSCW research.
2. The hierarchical structure (activity–action–operation) is explained as both a descriptive taxonomy and a dynamic account: items shift levels as skills are acquired or disrupted.
3. The intentionality principle distinguishes activity theory from behaviourism: the same observable behaviour can constitute different actions depending on the goal, and the same goal can be pursued through different operations.
4. The authors identify five key principles of activity theory for HCI: object-orientedness, internalization/externalization, tool mediation, hierarchical structure, and continuous development.
5. For CSCW specifically, activity theory highlights that collaborative work systems must support shared objects of activity, not just individual task flows.

## Methodology

Tutorial chapter / short monograph. Primarily conceptual with illustrative design examples. Written as an accessible entry point for HCI/CSCW practitioners encountering activity theory for the first time.

## Relevance

This is a widely referenced introductory exposition of activity theory for a technical/design audience. The five principles it articulates have become a standard condensed summary of AT in HCI literature. For learning researchers coming from an educational technology background, it provides a concise, practically oriented bridge to the philosophical source texts.
