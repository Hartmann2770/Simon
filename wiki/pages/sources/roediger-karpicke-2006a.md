## Citation

Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). The power of testing memory: Basic research and implications for educational practice. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 1*(3), 181–210. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1745-6916.2006.00012.x

## Key Findings

1. Testing memory for previously studied material is one of the most powerful ways to improve long-term retention.
2. Tests enhance retention even when given without corrective feedback — the act of retrieval itself is what matters.
3. The testing effect generalises beyond simple word lists to educationally relevant prose passages and classroom settings.
4. Frequent testing in the classroom can boost educational achievement at all levels of education.
5. Dynamic testing and formative assessment are related approaches that leverage the same underlying mechanism.
6. Some negative consequences of testing (e.g., intrusion of wrong responses) exist but are typically small and do not cancel the large positive effects.

## Methodology

Selective review of laboratory experiments and classroom studies demonstrating the testing effect. Covers both basic cognitive-science paradigms (word-list recall) and applied educational settings. The authors situate their review within broader discussions of formative assessment and dynamic testing.

## Relevance

This is a landmark review that established the educational relevance of the testing effect for a broad psychological science audience. It synthesises decades of basic research and makes a direct argument for incorporating frequent low-stakes testing into instructional design. Closely complemented by the authors' companion empirical paper (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006b).
