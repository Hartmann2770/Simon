## Key Findings

1. Robert Mager is the originator of the *behavioural objectives* movement in instructional design. His core argument (first made in *Preparing Instructional Objectives*, 1962) is that learning objectives must be specific enough to be observable and measurable.
2. The **ABCD model** provides four components for a well-formed learning objective:
   - **A — Audience**: who the learner is
   - **B — Behaviour**: what the learner will do (using an observable, action verb)
   - **C — Condition**: under what circumstances the behaviour will be performed
   - **D — Degree**: how well (the criterion for acceptable performance)
3. Vague objectives (e.g., "understand," "appreciate," "know") cannot drive instructional design or assessment because they do not specify what the learner will *do*. Mager insists on verbs that describe observable actions.
4. Clear objectives serve dual functions: they guide the *instructor* in selecting appropriate content and assessment, and they guide the *learner* by making expectations explicit.
5. Mager's behavioural approach is foundational to criterion-referenced instruction (CRI) — the idea that instruction should be designed to meet a specific performance standard rather than to rank students against each other.

## Methodology

Design-guidelines document; not a primary research study. Based on Mager's long practice and writing in instructional design.

## Relevance

The foundational reference for behavioural objectives in instructional design. Still widely taught and used, though also critiqued for overemphasising atomistic behaviours at the expense of complex, integrated performance. Essential historical context for any discussion of learning objectives.
