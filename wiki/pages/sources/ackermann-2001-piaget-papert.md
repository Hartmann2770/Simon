## Citation

Ackermann, E. (2001). Piaget's constructivism, Papert's constructionism: What's the difference? *Future of Learning Group Publication*, 5(3), 438. http://learning.media.mit.edu/content/publications/EA.Piaget%20_%20Papert.pdf

## Key Findings

1. Both Piaget and Papert agree that knowledge is actively constructed by the learner rather than passively received — but they emphasize different aspects of this process.
2. **Piaget's constructivism** focuses on internal cognitive development: knowledge construction happens through the interaction of assimilation (fitting new experience into existing schemas) and accommodation (modifying schemas to fit new experience). The primary locus is the individual mind developing through stages.
3. **Papert's constructionism** adds an external dimension: learning is most powerful when it is *situated in the act of constructing shareable artifacts* — physical or digital objects that can be shown, discussed, and shared with others. "The best learning happens when you make something."
4. Ackermann captures the distinction: Piaget studied how children come to internalize the world (internal construction); Papert studied how children extend their minds into the world through making (external construction).
5. Constructionism stresses the importance of objects-to-think-with: concrete, manipulable things that serve as anchors for abstract thinking. This is why the LOGO turtle is so powerful — it makes mathematical concepts touchable.
6. The two theories are complementary rather than contradictory: constructionism is, in Papert's words, "Piaget with the design and social dimensions added."

## Methodology

Comparative conceptual analysis. Theoretical article drawing on primary texts by Piaget and Papert plus Ackermann's own work with children and constructionist environments.

## Relevance

Clarifies the theoretical relationship between two foundational learning theories. Essential for understanding why making and sharing artifacts is not just pedagogically useful but epistemologically important: knowledge becomes more stable, transferable, and social when externalized in artifacts.
