## Citation

Rogers, E. M. (1995). *Diffusion of Innovations* (4th ed.). Free Press.

## Key Findings

1. **Diffusion** is the process by which an innovation is communicated through certain channels over time among members of a social system. Four key elements: the innovation, communication channels, time, and the social system.
2. Adoption of innovations follows an **S-curve** over time: initial slow uptake by innovators and early adopters, rapid acceleration through the early and late majority, and final saturation with laggards.
3. Five categories of adopters: **innovators** (risk-tolerant, first to adopt), **early adopters** (opinion leaders, selective), **early majority** (deliberate, follow social proof), **late majority** (skeptical, adopt from peer pressure), **laggards** (tradition-bound, last to adopt).
4. Five attributes of innovations that predict adoption rate: **relative advantage** (better than what it replaces), **compatibility** (consistent with existing values and practices), **complexity** (ease of use), **trialability** (can be experimented with), and **observability** (results visible to others).
5. **Opinion leaders** — influential members of a social system — are critical change agents: innovations that gain adoption among opinion leaders spread rapidly; rejection by opinion leaders slows or stops diffusion.
6. Communication channels vary in their influence across adoption stages: mass media channels are most effective for creating awareness; interpersonal channels are most effective for persuading adoption decisions.

## Methodology

Synthesis of over 3,000 diffusion studies across disciplines (agriculture, public health, education, technology). Meta-analytic and theoretical. First published 1962; each edition updates with new studies.

## Relevance

Essential framework for understanding how educational innovations and technologies spread (or fail to spread) in organizations and educational systems. Explains why promising innovations stagnate and what characteristics predict successful adoption at scale.
