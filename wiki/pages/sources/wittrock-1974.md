## Citation

Wittrock, M. C. (1974). Learning as a generative process. *Educational Psychologist, 11*(2), 87–95. https://doi.org/10.1080/00461527409529129

## Key Findings

1. Learning with understanding occurs when the learner actively generates meaningful relationships between new information and existing knowledge — not through passive reception.
2. The generative model distinguishes between storage (encoding) and generation (construction of meaning) as separable processes; transfer and understanding require generation, not just storage.
3. Empirical research supports the model: learners who actively generate connections (e.g., by creating their own examples, explanations, or analogies) remember and transfer material better than passive learners.
4. The model integrates research streams from cognitive development, human learning, information processing, and aptitude-treatment interaction research.
5. Generation during learning supports transfer of experience and abilities to novel tasks, making it foundational for deep as opposed to surface learning.

## Methodology

Theoretical paper presenting the generative model of learning with reference to supporting empirical studies. The model is positioned as a unifying framework for diverse cognitive research traditions.

## Relevance

One of the foundational theoretical frameworks for generative learning — a concept with direct implications for instructional design (e.g., why explaining, summarising, question-answering, and map-making work). Wittrock's generative model anticipates later work on elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, and retrieval practice. It is foundational for understanding why active, effortful processing produces deeper learning than passive exposure.
