## Citation

Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. *Psychological Science, 17*(3), 249–255. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x

## Key Findings

1. Testing with educationally relevant prose passages produces substantially greater long-term retention than restudying the same material.
2. At a 5-minute delay, repeated studying outperformed repeated testing — but on tests after 2 days or 1 week, prior testing produced far better retention.
3. Even a single free-recall test without feedback improved later retention more than an additional study opportunity.
4. Repeated studying increased students' confidence that they would remember the material — but this confidence was not matched by actual retention on delayed tests, revealing a metacognitive illusion.
5. Three immediate tests further amplified the testing advantage over one test on delayed assessments.

## Methodology

Two laboratory experiments. Students studied prose passages (texts on topics such as sun and moon) and were assigned to restudying conditions or immediate free-recall testing conditions of varying repetition (1 or 3 repetitions). Final retention tests were given at 5 minutes, 2 days, or 1 week after initial learning. No corrective feedback was given during testing.

## Relevance

This is one of the most-cited empirical demonstrations of the testing effect with realistic educational materials. It directly challenges the widespread assumption that studying more is always beneficial, and highlights a metacognitive failure: students feel more confident after restudying even though their long-term retention is worse. This has direct implications for how students self-regulate their learning.
