## Citation

Mayer, R. E. (2010). Applying the science of learning to medical education. *Medical Education, 44*(6), 543–549. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2923.2010.03624.x

## Key Findings

1. Mayer's *cognitive theory of multimedia learning* (CTML) provides the psychological foundation: people learn through dual channels (auditory/visual), each with limited processing capacity, and learning requires active processing — selecting, organising, and integrating information.
2. The article translates CTML into twelve evidence-based multimedia principles for designing instructional materials in medical education: coherence, signalling, redundancy, spatial contiguity, temporal contiguity, segmenting, pre-training, modality, multimedia, personalisation, voice, and image principles.
3. Each principle is illustrated with an example relevant to medical training (e.g., anatomy diagrams, patient-assessment videos) and linked to effect-size evidence from controlled experiments.
4. The *coherence principle* (remove extraneous content) and *modality principle* (use spoken rather than written words with animations) are highlighted as having the strongest and most consistent effects.
5. Mayer argues explicitly that learning is constrained by cognitive architecture — not all instructional decisions are arbitrary matters of preference — and that evidence-based design can be meaningfully imported from basic research into professional health education.

## Methodology

Invited review article for *Medical Education*. Draws on Mayer's own extensive programme of experimental research and translates it for a health professions education audience. No new empirical data.

## Relevance

Demonstrates how CLT-informed multimedia principles apply beyond the classroom to professional and health-professions training. Important bridge paper for instructional designers working in healthcare, simulation, or continuing professional development.
