## Citation

Engeström, Yrjö. "Activity Theory and Individual and Social Transformation." In *Perspectives on Activity Theory*, edited by Yrjö Engeström, Reijo Miettinen, and Raija-Leena Punamäki-Gitai. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1999. ISBN 0-521-43127-1.

## Key Findings

1. Activity theory bridges the gap between individual psychology and sociology by taking collective activity systems — not isolated acts or social structures — as the unit of analysis.
2. Individual and social transformation are inseparable: changes in an activity system alter the possibilities for individual development, and individual actions can recursively reshape the activity system.
3. Engeström frames the third generation of activity theory as concerned with networks of interacting activity systems, adding dialogue, multiple voices, and boundary crossing to the earlier two-generation framework.
4. Contradictions — systemic tensions within and between activity systems — are positioned not as problems to be eliminated but as the engine of historical development and learning.
5. The chapter argues that transformative learning requires making the object of activity visible and contestable, rather than treating it as a given.

## Methodology

Theoretical/conceptual chapter synthesizing Vygotsky's, Leont'ev's, and Engeström's own prior work. Draws on case material from organizational and educational settings to illustrate theoretical claims.

## Relevance

This chapter is a programmatic statement of what third-generation activity theory is and why it matters. It is essential reading for understanding how Engeström reframes the tradition he inherited. For learning researchers, the key move is the shift from dyadic (individual–tool–world) to systemic analysis, which allows one to study learning as social and historically situated transformation rather than individual cognitive change.
