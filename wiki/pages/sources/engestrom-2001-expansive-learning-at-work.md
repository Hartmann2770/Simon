## Citation

Engeström, Yrjö. "Expansive Learning at Work: Toward an Activity Theoretical Reconceptualization." *Journal of Education and Work* 14, no. 1 (February 2001): 133–156. DOI: 10.1080/13639080020028747. https://doi.org/10.1080/13639080020028747

## Key Findings

1. Expansive learning is learning that produces qualitatively new activity — not just better performance within existing rules, but the collective creation of new forms of practice.
2. The cycle of expansive learning follows a sequence of epistemic actions: questioning, analyzing, modelling, examining, implementing, reflecting, and consolidating the new model. This is not a smooth progression; regression and conflict are normal.
3. The Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD), as originally conceived by Vygotsky for individuals with expert guidance, is reconceptualized at the collective level: the ZPD of an organization is the distance between its current practice and the new form of activity that can be collaboratively constructed.
4. Traditional learning theories presuppose a stable body of knowledge to be transmitted or discovered; expansive learning addresses situations where the object itself is in flux and must be collectively redefined.
5. Work-based learning is not adequately captured by either behaviourist training or constructivist discovery metaphors; it requires attention to the contradictions that motivate workers to challenge and transform their own activity systems.
6. Engeström distinguishes expansive learning from incremental improvement: expansion involves breaking out of the current activity frame, not just optimizing within it.

## Methodology

Theoretical article drawing on case studies from Finnish workplace interventions (healthcare, telecommunications). Develops conceptual argument through illustration rather than formal empirical testing.

## Relevance

This is the most widely cited single article on expansive learning and the best entry point to Engeström's theory. Its reconceptualization of the ZPD as a collective phenomenon is a significant theoretical contribution. For learning researchers and designers, it raises the question of whether curriculum and training ever deliberately target the collective reframing of activity — and what that would look like in practice.
