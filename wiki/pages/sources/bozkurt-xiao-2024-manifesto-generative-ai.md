## Key Findings

1. The manifesto articulates a set of principles for educators navigating the generative AI moment: centering human agency, critical pedagogy, equity, and authentic learning over efficiency and automation.
2. **Teaching is a fundamentally human act**: while AI can assist with many peripheral tasks (content generation, assessment feedback), the relational, ethical, and transformative dimensions of teaching cannot be automated.
3. Assessment must be reimagined for an AI-abundant world: traditional assessments that can be trivially completed by AI test recall of information rather than understanding, judgment, or creativity. New assessment designs must foreground process, reasoning, and unique personal perspective.
4. **Digital equity** is a central concern: AI tools are not equally accessible to all learners, and their widespread adoption in well-resourced contexts may widen existing educational inequalities.
5. Critical AI literacy — understanding how AI systems work, what their limitations and biases are, and how they shape knowledge — should be a core educational competency, not an add-on.
6. The manifesto resists both techno-utopianism (AI will solve education) and moral panic (AI will destroy it), calling for thoughtful, evidence-informed, equity-conscious engagement.

## Methodology

Collaborative manifesto/position paper. Multiple authors from diverse institutional contexts. Not an empirical study; critical synthesis and normative argument.

## Relevance

Provides a critical-pedagogical counterweight to more enthusiastic AI-in-education claims. Essential for understanding the equity, agency, and assessment dimensions of AI's impact on teaching and learning.
