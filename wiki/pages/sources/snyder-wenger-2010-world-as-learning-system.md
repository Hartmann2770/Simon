## Citation

Snyder, William M., and Etienne Wenger. "Our World as a Learning System: A Communities-of-Practice Approach." In *Social Learning Systems and Communities of Practice*, edited by Chris Blackmore. London: Springer, 2010. ISBN: 978-1-84996-132-5.

## Key Findings

1. The chapter argues that the most important challenges facing contemporary societies — sustainability, global poverty, public health, climate change — cannot be addressed by conventional expertise or organizational hierarchies alone; they require **learning across communities, disciplines, and sectors** at a systemic level.
2. Communities of practice provide a model for this systemic learning: they are small enough to be relational and trust-based, yet through their interconnections form **social learning systems** capable of addressing complex, distributed problems.
3. Snyder and Wenger develop the concept of **global learning systems**: loosely coupled configurations of CoPs that share a broad domain concern (e.g., sustainable agriculture) and are connected by overlapping memberships, shared tools, and common reference points, even without formal organizational ties.
4. Effective systemic change requires attention to three levels simultaneously: the **CoP level** (ensuring that key practice communities are healthy and generative), the **inter-community level** (enabling boundary crossing, brokering, and knowledge sharing), and the **institutional level** (creating policies and infrastructure that legitimize and support CoPs without over-formalizing them).
5. The authors describe conditions that promote CoP-based systemic learning: **legitimacy** (CoPs are taken seriously by decision-makers), **connectivity** (communities are linked to others working on related problems), **resources** (CoPs have access to tools, time, and facilitation support), and **diversity** (multiple perspectives are represented in the practice).
6. Case illustrations draw on CoP-based initiatives in international development, healthcare networks, and environmental management, showing that voluntary communities outperform top-down programs when domain ownership and intrinsic motivation are high.

## Methodology

Conceptual chapter combining theoretical synthesis with illustrative case material from Snyder's consulting work with international development organizations and Wenger's broader CoP program.

## Relevance

Extends the CoP framework from organizational learning to societal-scale change, making it relevant to education reform, policy design, and development practice. The three-level model (CoP, inter-community, institutional) provides a practical heuristic for systems-level learning design. Useful for anyone designing professional learning networks that need to scale beyond individual institutions.
