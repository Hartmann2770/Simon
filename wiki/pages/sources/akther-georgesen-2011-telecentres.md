## Citation

Akther, Farzana, and Marianne Georgesen. "Telecentres: One-Stop Learning Hubs in Rural Bangladesh." Unpublished document, 2011. http://vbn.aau.dk/files/47313715/Akther_Georgsen_Telecentres_as_learning_hubs_final.pdf

## Key Findings

1. Telecentres in rural Bangladesh function as community-based learning hubs that aggregate multiple services — government information, agricultural extension, health information, e-commerce — into a single physical access point, attempting to overcome the fragmentation of rural service delivery.
2. The study applies a communities-of-practice lens to telecentre adoption, finding that sustainable uptake depends on whether the telecentre becomes embedded in existing local practice communities (farmers' groups, women's associations, small business networks) rather than operating as a standalone technology delivery point.
3. Telecentres that achieved high adoption were those where local facilitators acted as **brokers** between digital resources and existing village practice communities — translating information into locally actionable form and connecting it to prior knowledge held within the community.
4. **Legitimate peripheral participation** plays a role in digital literacy acquisition: new users observe others, take on small tasks (printing, form-filling), and gradually develop confidence and competence through graded participation — not through formal digital-literacy instruction.
5. Gender and social hierarchy shape access to peripheral participation. Women, lower-caste members, and the elderly were often excluded from the telecentre's public space, limiting their opportunities for informal, observation-based learning.
6. The most durable communities of use that formed around telecentres developed a **shared repertoire** — common vocabulary about services, recognized norms for use, shared success stories — indicating the emergence of a CoP rather than a mere user group.

## Methodology

Qualitative case study drawing on fieldwork in rural Bangladesh. Methods include interviews with telecentre operators, community members, and development workers, supplemented by observation. The study applies CoP theory retrospectively to interpret patterns of adoption and exclusion.

## Relevance

Provides an empirical test of CoP and situated learning concepts in an international development and ICT-for-development context, extending the theory well beyond Western organizational settings. Useful for learning designers working on community-based digital literacy programs, non-formal education, and technology adoption in low-resource contexts. The attention to social exclusion from peripheral participation is a significant practical and ethical contribution.
