# Learning in Virtual Reality: Effects on Performance, Emotion and Engagement

## Citation

Allcoat, Devon, and Adrian von Mühlenen. "Learning in Virtual Reality: Effects on Performance, Emotion and Engagement." *Research in Learning Technology* 26 (2018): 2140. https://doi.org/10.25304/rlt.v26.2140

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/PDFEZH4I/attachment/A3STLR2N/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Participants who learned content in VR showed comparable factual knowledge performance to those who learned via video or textbook — VR did not significantly boost test scores.
2. VR learners reported significantly higher positive emotion and engagement during learning compared to the video and textbook conditions.
3. The emotional and motivational benefits of VR were measurable and consistent, suggesting VR's primary near-term value may be affective rather than purely cognitive.
4. These findings challenge the assumption that increased engagement automatically translates into improved knowledge acquisition on standard outcome measures.
5. The study underscores the importance of measuring multiple dimensions of learning (performance, affect, engagement) rather than test scores alone.

## Methodology

Experimental between-subjects design. Three conditions: VR, video, and textbook, all covering the same factual learning content. Participants (n = 39 university students) completed a knowledge test post-learning and self-report measures of emotion and engagement. The VR condition used a head-mounted display.

## Relevance

One of the more methodologically controlled early studies on VR-based learning. Its finding that VR boosts engagement and positive emotion without necessarily improving test performance has important implications for how we evaluate [immersive learning](../concepts/immersive-learning.md) technologies. It connects to debates about whether [desirable difficulties](../concepts/desirable-difficulties.md) apply in VR contexts, and to [metacognition](../concepts/metacognition.md) research showing that subjective ease of learning can be misleading.
