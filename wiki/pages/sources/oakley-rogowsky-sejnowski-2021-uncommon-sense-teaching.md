## Citation

Oakley, B., Rogowsky, B., & Sejnowski, T. J. (2021). *Uncommon sense teaching: Practical insights in brain science to help students learn*. Penguin.

## Key Findings

1. The book applies neuroscience findings to classroom teaching, arguing that brain science has significant — largely unrealised — implications for pedagogical practice.
2. Two broad modes of cognitive processing are described: **focused mode** (directed, working-memory-intensive) and **diffuse mode** (background, default-network processing). Effective learning alternates between both modes.
3. Working memory capacity varies among learners: students with smaller working memory spans benefit more from direct instruction and structured practice; those with larger working-memory spans can handle more open-ended discovery. One-size-fits-all pedagogy disadvantages one group or the other.
4. The book is critical of the *constructivist* overemphasis on discovery learning and emphasises that direct instruction supports schema formation in long-term memory, making subsequent higher-order thinking possible.
5. **Interleaving** and **spaced practice** are presented as neuroscience-consistent strategies: they force retrieval under conditions of partial forgetting, strengthening the connections that constitute long-term schemas.
6. The book includes a strong defence of direct instruction for building foundational knowledge before inquiry-based exploration — consistent with Rosenshine's principles and Kirschner, Sweller & Clark's work on minimal guidance.

## Methodology

Practitioner/popular science book synthesising neuroscience research (primarily working memory, the default mode network, and memory consolidation) for teachers and educators. Co-authored by neuroscientist Terrence Sejnowski, giving it stronger neurobiological grounding than most teaching-practice books.

## Relevance

Useful bridge between neuroscience and classroom practice. Provides accessible explanations of working memory and long-term memory that support CLT's prescriptions. Reinforces the evidence base for direct instruction and challenges prevalent constructivist assumptions.
