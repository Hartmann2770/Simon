## Citation

Lave, Jean, and Etienne Wenger. *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1991. DOI: 10.1017/CBO9780511815355. ISBN: 978-0-521-41308-4.

## Key Findings

1. Learning is fundamentally a social process, not a process occurring solely inside the learner's head. Cognition and knowledge are distributed across persons, activities, and the settings in which they occur.
2. The concept of **legitimate peripheral participation** (LPP) describes how newcomers gain membership in a community of practice: they begin at the periphery, observing and participating in low-stakes, peripheral tasks, and gradually move toward fuller participation as competence develops.
3. "Peripheral" does not mean marginal or unimportant — it signals a starting position that is structurally connected to the whole practice. Being peripheral grants access to the practice, to its masters, and to a legitimate identity as a practitioner-in-formation.
4. "Legitimate" refers to the social recognition that the newcomer belongs, even in a limited way. Without legitimacy, participation is denied and learning fails to take hold.
5. Learning is better understood as **changing participation** in communities of practice than as the acquisition of an internalized body of knowledge. What changes is the learner's identity, relationships, and position within the community.
6. The authors draw on five empirical cases of apprenticeship: Yucatec midwives, Vai and Gola tailors, US Navy quartermasters, meat cutters, and recovering alcoholics in AA — demonstrating LPP across radically different settings and cultures.
7. The curriculum in situated learning is not a planned syllabus but the **structure of the practice itself**: the sequence of tasks, the access to masters, and the organization of work create the conditions for learning.
8. Motivation is intrinsic to participation: newcomers are drawn forward by the prospect of becoming full practitioners, not by external reward structures.

## Methodology

Theoretical synthesis informed by ethnographic case studies. Lave and Wenger draw on anthropological fieldwork across five apprenticeship settings to develop and illustrate the theoretical framework. This is not a study that tests hypotheses; it builds conceptual vocabulary from close observation of how learning actually unfolds in practice communities.

## Relevance

This is the foundational text for both situated learning theory and communities of practice. It established the vocabulary (LPP, CoP, legitimate participation) that would define an entire research tradition. For learning designers, it challenges the assumption that learning can be adequately organized through explicit instruction detached from authentic practice. It argues that the social structure of participation — who can do what, with whom, under what conditions — is the primary learning environment, not the content of lessons.
