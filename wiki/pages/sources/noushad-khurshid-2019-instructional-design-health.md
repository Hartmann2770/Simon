## Citation

Noushad, B., & Khurshid, F. (2019). Facilitating student learning: An instructional design perspective for health professions educators. *Research and Development in Medical Education, 8*(2), 65–70. https://doi.org/10.15171/rdme.2019.014

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/BNZP9MRV/attachment/EI3DURDN/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Learning in any context involves acquisition, storage, and utilisation of information by the human memory system; in health professions, this is especially demanding because novices must integrate large volumes of novel concepts to make high-stakes clinical decisions.
2. Effective instructional design for health education must account for the characteristics and limits of all three memory components: sensory memory (brief, gating function), working memory (limited capacity, ~7 ± 2 elements for familiar chunks), and long-term memory (unlimited, organised in schemas).
3. The paper applies CLT to health professions education, recommending: chunking content, sequencing from simple to complex, using worked examples early, and providing explicit connections to prior knowledge.
4. Contextualised learning — embedding content in realistic clinical scenarios — is proposed to support meaningful encoding and schema activation.
5. Formative assessment with feedback is presented as essential for monitoring learning and redirecting cognitive effort before the high-stakes summative assessment.

## Methodology

Review and application paper. Synthesises cognitive load theory and instructional design literature for a health education readership. No new primary empirical data.

## Relevance

Illustrates the practical application of CLT principles in professional education. Relevant for understanding how CLT scales from classroom to clinical/professional training contexts.
