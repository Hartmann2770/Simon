## Citation

Wenger, Etienne, Beverly Trayner, and Maarten De Laat. *Promoting and Assessing Value Creation in Communities and Networks: A Conceptual Framework*. Heerlen: Ruud de Moor Centrum, Open Universiteit, 2011. ISBN: 978-90-358-1808-8.

## Key Findings

1. Communities of practice and learning networks create value at **multiple levels** simultaneously. Wenger, Trayner, and De Laat identify five cycles of value creation, each capturing a different kind of outcome over different time horizons.
2. The **five cycles of value** are:
   - *Immediate value*: the quality and experience of activities and interactions as they happen (engagement, inspiration)
   - *Potential value*: knowledge capital accumulated — what participants learn, relationships built, resources produced
   - *Applied value*: changes in practice that result from participation — what participants do differently
   - *Realized value*: tangible outcomes and performance improvements resulting from changed practice
   - *Reframing value*: redefining success and how value itself is understood; the deepest level of transformation
3. The framework critiques overly narrow approaches to assessment that focus only on quantifiable, short-term outcomes (immediate or realized value) and miss the deeper transformations (potential, applied, reframing value) that constitute the real contribution of communities to organizational and social learning.
4. **Social learning spaces** — the relational contexts in which CoPs and networks operate — are themselves a form of value, not merely a means to an end. Ignoring this leads to communities being disbanded when they cannot demonstrate immediate ROI.
5. Value creation is **bidirectional**: participants create value for the community, and the community creates value for participants. Assessment frameworks must capture both directions.
6. The authors propose a set of **assessment strategies** matched to each cycle: stories and narratives (for immediate and potential value), before-and-after comparison (for applied value), performance metrics (for realized value), and strategic alignment analysis (for reframing value).
7. The framework is explicitly designed for community stewards and organizational sponsors who need tools to justify investment in informal learning communities — translating CoP theory into accountability language without betraying it.

## Methodology

Conceptual/practical framework report. Synthesizes Wenger's theoretical program with Laat's empirical research on learning networks (particularly Dutch police and educational contexts) and Trayner's practitioner experience with CoP facilitation.

## Relevance

Critical for anyone who needs to evaluate CoPs in organizational or educational settings. The five-cycle framework solves a persistent practical problem: how to demonstrate the value of social learning communities to funders and managers who expect quantifiable returns. It also clarifies what to look for when designing communities — the goal is not just immediate activity but accumulated potential value and changed practice. Connects CoP theory to questions of accountability, assessment, and learning design.
