## Citation

Whiffen, J. W., & Karpicke, J. D. (2017). The role of episodic context in retrieval practice effects. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition, 43*(7), 1036–1046. https://doi.org/10.1037/xlm0000379

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/N75KYZBA/attachment/DA39PRZE/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Making list-discrimination judgements — requiring participants to retrieve the original episodic context of each studied word — consistently enhanced subsequent free recall relative to restudying.
2. Both the episodic retrieval condition and a semantic encoding condition improved retention over restudying, but they did so through different routes: episodic retrieval promoted temporal organisation; semantic encoding promoted semantically based retrieval strategies.
3. The pattern of results supports the episodic context account of retrieval practice: the requirement to reinstate the original learning context is a key driver of the testing effect.
4. Recall organisation during the final test showed that retrieval practice led to temporally organised output, distinguishable from the semantic clustering produced by elaborative encoding.

## Methodology

Three laboratory experiments with word-list paradigms. Participants studied multi-list sequences and were assigned to restudy, list-discrimination (episodic retrieval), or semantic encoding conditions. Final free recall was coded for output organisation (temporal clustering, semantic clustering) as well as raw accuracy.

## Relevance

Provides strong theoretical support for the episodic context account of retrieval practice — one of the leading mechanistic explanations. By separating the routes through which retrieval and encoding conditions produce their effects, the study enriches understanding of *why* testing works and has implications for designing retrieval tasks that maximise benefit.
