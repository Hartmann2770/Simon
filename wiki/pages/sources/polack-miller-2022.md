## Citation

Polack, C. W., & Miller, R. R. (2022). Testing improves performance as well as assesses learning: A review of the testing effect with implications for models of learning. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Animal Learning and Cognition, 48*(3), 222–241. https://doi.org/10.1037/xan0000323

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/T377SUMX/attachment/BREHXIZY/reader)

## Key Findings

1. The testing effect — performance improvements from prior testing — has been demonstrated in a wide variety of well-controlled experiments with both human and non-human subjects.
2. The phenomenon has been largely unacknowledged in basic associative-learning theory, which traditionally treats testing as a neutral assessment tool, not a learning event.
3. Learning is still widely conceived as occurring only during acquisition (before testing); this framing ignores the substantial processing that occurs at the time of testing.
4. The testing effect is sufficiently robust across tasks, parameters, and species to represent a fundamental challenge for theories of learning that focus exclusively on acquisition.
5. The review calls for new research with non-human subjects to identify the basic neural and associative mechanisms engaged during retrieval.

## Methodology

Narrative review of the testing-effect literature in both human and animal learning. The review situates the phenomenon within associative learning theory and identifies the theoretical gaps created by the testing effect's existence.

## Relevance

Broadens the testing-effect literature beyond cognitive psychology and education to basic learning science and animal cognition. Its theoretical argument — that any comprehensive learning theory must account for retrieval as a learning event — is important for foundational understanding. The human and animal evidence combined strengthens the case that the testing effect is a fundamental property of memory, not a human-specific artefact.
