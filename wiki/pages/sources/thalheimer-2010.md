## Citation

Thalheimer, W. (2010). How much do people forget? (31 pp.). Work-Learning Research.

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/SK58GZKP/attachment/Y3LWQD3H/reader)

## Key Findings

1. The classic Ebbinghaus "forgetting curve" has been widely misrepresented in training and HR contexts — the specific numbers often cited (e.g., "people forget 70% within 24 hours") are not directly supported by rigorous experimental evidence.
2. Forgetting rates depend heavily on conditions: type of material, encoding strength, meaningfulness, retrieval cues available, number of prior retrievals, and time intervals.
3. Well-designed learning interventions with spaced review can dramatically reduce forgetting compared to single-session training.
4. The practical implication for training design is to use spaced practice, retrieval practice, and contextual variety rather than relying on single learning events.

## Methodology

Applied research review synthesising memory and forgetting research with emphasis on practical implications for workplace training and learning design. The paper critiques oversimplified forgetting-curve statistics and contextualises research findings.

## Relevance

Important for critically evaluating the popular invocations of "the forgetting curve" in instructional design and training discourse. Thalheimer's work helps practitioners distinguish genuine empirical findings about forgetting from oversimplified myths, and reinforces the evidence base for spaced repetition and retrieval practice as countermeasures against forgetting.
