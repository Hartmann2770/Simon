## Key Findings

1. A number of common myths in healthcare simulation reduce its efficiency and effectiveness. Among the most prevalent: that **high fidelity always equals better learning** (fidelity should be matched to learning objectives, not maximized by default), and that **more simulation equals more learning** (quality, structure, and debriefing matter more than quantity).
2. The **debriefing** is the learning event, not the simulation itself. Simulation without structured debriefing is largely ineffective; the post-scenario reflection and discussion produces the durable learning, not the scenario itself.
3. **Psychological safety** is a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have: learners who fear embarrassment or judgment in simulation cannot engage in the risk-taking and error-making that is the whole point of simulation as a learning environment.
4. Simulation should be used for what simulation is good at — practicing skills, developing judgment under uncertainty, rehearsing rare high-stakes events — not as a substitute for other forms of learning that work better for knowledge acquisition.

## Relevance

Useful corrective to naive assumptions about simulation-based learning. Reinforces the evidence that fidelity matching, debriefing quality, and psychological safety are the critical variables in effective simulation design.
