## Citation

Wenger, Etienne. "Communities of Practice: A Brief Introduction." 2006. Reproduced for use in "South Australia Teaching for Effective Learning Resource."

## Key Findings

1. A community of practice is defined by three fundamental characteristics that must all be present: a **domain** (a shared area of knowledge and concern that gives members a common identity), a **community** (members who engage in joint activities and discussions, help each other, and share information), and a **practice** (the shared repertoire of resources, experiences, tools, and stories that members develop together).
2. Communities of practice are **everywhere** and are not new: humans have always organized themselves into groups oriented around shared practices. What is new is the explicit attention paid to them as sites of learning and knowledge management.
3. CoPs are **different from other social groupings**: they are not simply teams (which are defined by organizational structure), networks (which are defined by informal relationships), or interest groups (which share interests but not practice). The practice dimension — the joint work — is what distinguishes a CoP.
4. The three characteristics of domain, community, and practice are interdependent. A domain without a community is just a topic; a community without a practice is just a social group; practice without domain commitment lacks shared purpose.
5. CoPs can exist within organizations, across organizations, or spanning public and private institutions. Their boundaries are defined by their practice, not by organizational charts.
6. Value is created in communities of practice through multiple mechanisms: problem solving, knowledge sharing, coordination, reuse of community assets, and the creation of a shared identity that enables collaboration.

## Methodology

Short conceptual overview written for a practitioner and educational audience. Synthesizes the key elements of the 1998 framework in accessible form.

## Relevance

A useful entry point for practitioners and educators encountering the CoP concept for the first time. The domain-community-practice triad has become the most widely used three-part definition of CoPs in educational and organizational literature. While less theoretically deep than the 1998 book or 2000 article, it captures the essentials and is widely circulated in teacher professional development and knowledge management contexts.
