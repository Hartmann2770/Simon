## Citation

Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013). Improving students' learning with effective learning techniques: Promising directions from cognitive and educational psychology. *Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 14*(1), 4–58. https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100612453266

## Key Findings

1. Practice testing (retrieval practice) received the highest utility rating — "high" — of all ten techniques reviewed, supported by consistent evidence across diverse materials, populations, and retention intervals.
2. Distributed practice (spacing) also received a "high" utility rating, with strong evidence that spreading study sessions over time yields substantially better long-term retention than massed practice.
3. Elaborative interrogation and self-explanation received "moderate" ratings; highlighting, rereading, keyword mnemonic, and imagery-based text learning received "low" utility ratings.
4. Interleaved practice was rated as having "moderate" utility; evidence was promising but more ecologically valid classroom studies were needed at the time of publication.
5. Widely used strategies (highlighting, rereading) have minimal utility despite students' heavy reliance on them.
6. The effectiveness of high-utility techniques generalises across many educationally important conditions (age groups, academic subjects, materials, criterion tasks).

## Methodology

A comprehensive monograph-style meta-review commissioned for *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*. Ten learning techniques were evaluated across five criteria: learning conditions, student characteristics, materials, criterion tasks, and educational contexts. Each technique received a utility rating (low/moderate/high) based on the breadth and consistency of the evidence.

## Relevance

The most influential and widely cited summary of evidence-based learning strategies for educational practice. Its clear rating framework and comprehensive coverage make it the standard reference for anyone comparing study strategies. The "high" endorsement of practice testing and distributed practice is foundational for instructional design and learning-science communication.
