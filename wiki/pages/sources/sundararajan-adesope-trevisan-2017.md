## Citation

Sundararajan, N., Adesope, O., & Trevisan, D. (2017). Rethinking the use of tests: A meta-analysis of practice testing. *Review of Educational Research, 87*. https://doi.org/10.3102/0034654316689306

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/Q36387NB/attachment/UTTKJPWZ/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Practice tests are more beneficial for learning than restudying and all other comparison conditions (filler activities, no-presentation controls).
2. Mean effect sizes were substantial and statistically significant across the full sample of studies.
3. Effect sizes were moderated by: features of the practice test (format, feedback, number of tests), participant characteristics (age, educational level), outcome constructs (retention, transfer), and methodological features of individual studies.
4. Free-recall practice tests produced larger effects than recognition-format tests.
5. Feedback after practice testing enhanced the benefit compared to testing without feedback.
6. Results have direct implications for instructional design: practice testing should be considered a primary strategy rather than a mere assessment tool.

## Methodology

Systematic meta-analysis of studies comparing practice testing against nontesting conditions. Evidence-based criteria were used to select studies; effect sizes were synthesised using meta-analytic methods. Moderator analyses examined conditions that amplify or attenuate the testing effect.

## Relevance

Provides quantitative synthesis of the testing-effect literature with moderator analysis. Moves beyond narrative reviews to establish effect-size estimates and boundary conditions, helping educators understand not just *whether* practice testing works but *when* and *how strongly* it works under different conditions.
