## Citation

Hattie, J., & Timperley, H. (2007). The power of feedback. *Review of Educational Research, 77*(1), 81–112. https://doi.org/10.3102/003465430298487

## Key Findings

1. Feedback is one of the most powerful influences on learning and achievement, but its effects are highly variable — both strongly positive and strongly negative outcomes are documented in the literature, depending on the type and level of feedback.
2. A conceptual model of feedback is proposed around three questions a learner needs answered: **Where am I going?** (feed-up: goal clarity), **How am I going?** (feed-back: current performance relative to goal), and **Where to next?** (feed-forward: next steps).
3. Feedback operates at four levels: **task level** (correctness of specific work), **process level** (strategies used to produce the work), **self-regulation level** (learner's metacognitive monitoring), and **self level** (praise/blame directed at the person). Process and self-regulation feedback are most effective for deep learning; self-level feedback (praise) has minimal effect on achievement.
4. Feedback is most powerful when goals are clear and challenging, the gap between current performance and goal is neither too large nor too small, and the learner has sufficient knowledge to act on the feedback.
5. Praise and reward — the most common types of teacher feedback — have very little effect on learning outcomes; corrective process-level feedback has the largest effects.
6. The review analyses over 500 meta-analyses, making it one of the most comprehensive surveys of educational interventions ever published.

## Methodology

Conceptual review synthesising meta-analytic and empirical literature. The authors construct a theoretical model and test it against the evidence base rather than conducting new experiments. Part of Hattie's broader *Visible Learning* research programme.

## Relevance

Foundational reference for the study of feedback in learning. The four-level model (task, process, self-regulation, self) is widely used in teacher professional development and instructional design. Essential reading alongside Hattie's *Visible Learning* for evidence-based teaching.
