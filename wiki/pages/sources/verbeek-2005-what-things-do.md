## Citation

Verbeek, P.-P. (2005). *What Things Do: Philosophical Reflections on Technology, Agency, and Design*. Pennsylvania State University Press.

## Key Findings

1. Technology is not a neutral instrument but an active mediator that shapes the relationship between humans and the world — technological artifacts co-constitute human experience and action.
2. Drawing on Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, Verbeek argues that things "withdraw" from attention when they work well (embodiment relations) but structure perception and action invisibly as they do so.
3. Ihde's taxonomy of human-technology relations is refined and extended: **embodiment relations** (technology becomes extension of body), **interpretive relations** (technology mediates interpretation of world), **alterity relations** (technology encountered as quasi-Other), and **background relations** (technology shapes context without direct interaction).
4. Design is never value-neutral: designers inevitably "script" behaviors and moral stances into artifacts, as demonstrated by Latour's concept of the non-human moral agent.
5. The proper ethical response is a **postphenomenological design ethics**: designers should critically reflect on the mediating roles their artifacts will play, rather than treating function and ethics as separate concerns.
6. Technological mediation applies directly to learning contexts: educational tools (simulations, digital environments) are not transparent delivery mechanisms but active shapers of what learners perceive, attend to, and experience.

## Methodology

Philosophical analysis integrating empirical turn in philosophy of technology (Ihde, Latour) with phenomenological tradition (Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty). No empirical data — conceptual analysis and case studies of design.

## Relevance

Foundational text for postphenomenological approach to understanding how technologies mediate human experience. Directly relevant to understanding how educational technologies shape learning, perception, and the learner-world relationship beyond simple tool use.
