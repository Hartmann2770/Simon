## Citation

Wenger, Etienne. *Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1998. ISBN: 978-0-521-66363-2.

## Key Findings

1. A **community of practice** is defined by three dimensions that must all be present: **mutual engagement** (members do things together and build relationships), **joint enterprise** (a shared understanding of what the community is about), and **shared repertoire** (accumulated routines, tools, stories, and concepts the community has produced over time).
2. Learning is primarily a matter of **identity formation** — becoming a certain kind of person — not merely acquiring skills or information. The social context of practice constitutes the learner as a practitioner.
3. Wenger introduces the duality of **participation** and **reification**: practice consists both of active participation in the community and of the production of objects, documents, tools, and concepts that "reify" (make into a thing) aspects of that practice. Healthy communities require a productive balance between the two.
4. **Meaning** is negotiated within practice: neither fully subjective nor objectively given by the world, meaning arises at the intersection of participation and reification. What things mean to practitioners is inseparable from what they do.
5. **Boundaries** between communities of practice are significant sites of learning. "Boundary objects" (shared artifacts that are differently interpreted on each side) and "brokers" (people who span communities) are key mechanisms by which knowledge travels across practice boundaries.
6. Identity is constituted through trajectories: **peripheral trajectories** (permanent membership at the margins), **inbound trajectories** (newcomers moving toward full membership), **insider trajectories** (established members redefining the practice), **boundary trajectories** (spanning multiple communities), and **outbound trajectories** (moving toward departure). Each trajectory involves different learning dynamics.
7. Institutional and formal educational settings often fail because they create forms of participation in school practice that have no meaningful connection to the communities of practice for which they ostensibly prepare students.
8. Wenger elaborates the concept of **constellations of practice** — loosely connected groups of communities that share certain elements but do not fully overlap — as the larger context in which individual CoPs are embedded.

## Methodology

Theoretical monograph. Wenger develops his framework through close analysis of insurance claims processors observed over several years (his own fieldwork), combined with theoretical synthesis. The book moves between conceptual argument and illustration from this empirical case.

## Relevance

This is the definitive theoretical elaboration of communities of practice, expanding far beyond the 1991 apprenticeship study into a comprehensive social theory of learning. It provides the conceptual vocabulary — mutual engagement, joint enterprise, shared repertoire, participation/reification, boundary objects, brokers — that practitioners and researchers use when designing or analysing CoPs. Its emphasis on identity and meaning as the substance of learning makes it essential reading for anyone moving beyond narrow instructional design toward questions of professional development, organizational learning, and educational culture.
