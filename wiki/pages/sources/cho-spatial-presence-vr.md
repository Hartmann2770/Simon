# How Spatial Presence in VR Affects Memory Retention and Motivation on Second Language Learning

## Citation

Cho, Yeonhee. "How Spatial Presence in VR Affects Memory Retention and Motivation on Second Language Learning: A Comparison of Desktop and Immersive VR-Based Learning." *(Journal and year details unavailable in metadata.)*

## Key Findings

1. Learners in an immersive VR condition reported significantly higher spatial presence than those in a desktop VR condition when studying the same second-language vocabulary content.
2. Higher spatial presence was positively associated with both vocabulary retention (as measured by a post-test) and self-reported motivation to continue learning.
3. Immersive VR produced better vocabulary recall at delayed test compared to desktop VR, suggesting a retention advantage that extends beyond immediate post-learning.
4. The results support a model in which spatial presence mediates the relationship between immersion level and learning outcomes — the technology matters because of the sense of "being there" it produces, not merely because it is novel.
5. The study adds language learning as a domain where VR shows measurable benefits beyond engagement.

## Methodology

Comparative experiment. Two conditions: head-mounted display (immersive VR) and desktop VR. Both conditions presented identical vocabulary learning content in a virtual environment. Outcome measures: vocabulary test (immediate and delayed), spatial presence questionnaire, motivation scale.

## Relevance

This study directly investigates [spatial presence](../concepts/spatial-presence.md) as a mediating mechanism, not just as a side-effect of immersion. It links to [Schöne et al. 2019](schone-wessels-gruber-2019.md) on VR and autobiographical memory, and to [Krokos et al. 2019](krokos-plaisant-varshney-2019.md) on the immersion–recall relationship. Also touches on motivation as a learning variable — relevant to [Allcoat & von Mühlenen 2018](allcoat-muhlenen-2018.md).
