## Citation

Hattie, J. A. C., & Donoghue, G. M. (2016). Learning strategies: A synthesis and conceptual model. *npj Science of Learning, 1*, 16013. https://doi.org/10.1038/npjscilearn.2016.13

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/DWYQJNH9/attachment/8DSG8GGL/reader)

## Key Findings

1. A conceptual model of learning is proposed with three inputs/outcomes — **skill** (knowledge, strategies), **will** (motivation, dispositions), and **thrill** (engagement) — and three phases of learning: **surface acquisition**, **surface consolidation**, **deep acquisition**, **deep consolidation**, and **transfer**.
2. A synthesis of 228 meta-analyses identifies the most effective learning strategies, but crucially finds that strategy effectiveness depends on the phase of learning: strategies that work for surface learning are not the same as those that work for deep learning or transfer.
3. Surface-learning strategies (e.g., mnemonic devices, practice testing for recall, spaced practice) are powerful for building initial knowledge bases but do not on their own produce deep understanding or transfer.
4. Deep-learning strategies (e.g., elaboration, self-explanation, concept mapping, interteaching) become effective once a foundation of surface knowledge exists; applying them prematurely produces little gain.
5. Transfer is promoted by varied practice, helping students understand *why* strategies work, and by making the conditions of application explicit.
6. The model challenges the common assumption that any single strategy (retrieval practice, spacing) is universally best; what matters is matching strategy to phase and goal.

## Methodology

Theoretical synthesis drawing on 228 meta-analyses. A conceptual model is developed and used to organise the evidence base. No new experimental data are generated.

## Relevance

Important corrective to oversimplified advocacy for "learning science" strategies. Argues for a phase-sensitive view of instruction rather than universal prescriptions. Complements and complicates Dunlosky et al.'s utility ratings by adding developmental phase as a key variable.
