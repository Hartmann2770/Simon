# Utilization of a Voice-Based Virtual Reality Advanced Cardiac Life Support Team Leader Refresher

## Citation

Katz, Daniel, Ronak Shah, Elizabeth Kim, Chang Park, Anjan Shah, Adam Levine, and Garrett Burnett. "Utilization of a Voice-Based Virtual Reality Advanced Cardiac Life Support Team Leader Refresher: Prospective Observational Study." *Journal of Medical Internet Research* 22, no. 3 (2020): e17425. https://doi.org/10.2196/17425

## Key Findings

1. A voice-driven VR simulation of ACLS team leader scenarios was feasible and acceptable for use as a refresher training tool; participants completed scenarios without significant technical barriers.
2. Knowledge retention scores post-VR training were statistically significant compared to pre-training baselines, indicating genuine learning gains.
3. Participants reported high satisfaction and perceived realism, with most rating the VR experience as a useful complement to traditional ACLS simulation.
4. VR training was completed independently and asynchronously by participants — a major logistical advantage over high-fidelity simulation requiring instructors and mannequins.
5. The voice-based interaction model (giving verbal commands) engaged the team leader communication skills that are critical in real cardiac arrests, making the simulation ecologically valid.
6. The study did not include a control comparison group, so the absolute benefit over standard methods cannot be quantified from this data alone.

## Methodology

Prospective observational study (no control group). N = 57 anesthesia residents. Participants completed a VR ACLS team leader scenario using a head-mounted display with voice input. Outcome measures: pre/post knowledge quiz, satisfaction survey, perceived realism rating.

## Relevance

This study demonstrates a practical VR implementation in high-stakes clinical training, extending the evidence base for [simulation-based education](../concepts/simulation-based-education.md). The asynchronous, self-directed completion model connects to [spaced repetition](../concepts/spaced-repetition.md) arguments — learners can refresh skills on demand. The voice interaction distinguishes it from passive VR and raises the complexity of the simulation. Complements [Wan & Lam 2019](wan-lam-2019.md) on VR in health professions education.
