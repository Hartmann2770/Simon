# Experiences in Virtual Reality: A Window to Autobiographical Memory

## Citation

Schöne, Benjamin, Marlene Wessels, and Thomas Gruber. "Experiences in Virtual Reality: A Window to Autobiographical Memory." *Current Psychology* 38 (2019): 715–719. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12144-017-9648-y

## Key Findings

1. VR experiences were encoded as genuine autobiographical memories, not merely as memories of watching a screen — participants recalled VR events using the same phenomenological features as real-world memories.
2. Spatial presence (the sense of "being there") in VR was positively correlated with the vividness and detail of later autobiographical recall.
3. VR experiences that generated high levels of presence showed memory characteristics similar to those of real episodic events, rather than resembling memory for film or media.
4. This suggests that VR may be a uniquely powerful medium for creating durable learning memories, provided the environment generates high presence.
5. The findings support the use of VR in contexts where memorable, context-rich encoding of information is educationally important.

## Methodology

Empirical study using self-report measures of autobiographical memory phenomenology (e.g., the Memory Characteristics Questionnaire) and presence scales following VR experiences. Compared VR memory reports to memories of real and media events.

## Relevance

This paper provides a cognitive science rationale for why immersive VR may produce stronger long-term retention than flat-screen media. The link between [spatial presence](../concepts/spatial-presence.md) and memory quality underpins the argument for VR's educational advantages found in [Krokos et al. 2019](krokos-plaisant-varshney-2019.md) and [Cho](cho-spatial-presence-vr.md). Directly relevant to [immersive learning](../concepts/immersive-learning.md) and [retrieval practice](../concepts/retrieval-practice.md) discussions.
