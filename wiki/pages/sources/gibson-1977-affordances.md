## Citation

## Key Findings

1. An **affordance** is a property of the environment taken relative to an animal — it is what the environment *offers* or *provides* to an organism for action. Affordances are relational: the same object has different affordances for different organisms depending on their size, capabilities, and purposes.
2. Affordances are directly perceived, not inferred from sense data: ecological perception is direct pickup of information about action possibilities. A surface that affords support for walking is perceived as walkable, not first perceived as a surface and then judged as potentially walkable.
3. The concept dissolves the subject/object dichotomy: affordances are neither purely objective (in the environment) nor purely subjective (in the mind) but relational — they exist at the interface of organism and environment.
4. Affordances are **persistent** rather than momentary — they remain available whether or not the organism is currently exploiting them.
5. Social affordances extend the framework: environments afford not just individual actions but coordinated social behaviors — places for gathering, for conversation, for competition.

## Methodology

Theoretical chapter in an edited volume. Conceptual analysis within ecological psychology framework.

## Relevance

Foundational concept for understanding how environments (including learning environments, digital tools, and simulations) offer or constrain certain actions. The concept of affordance is central to educational technology design: digital tools should be designed to *afford* the cognitive and social actions that support learning.
