## Citation

Bjork, R. A., & Bjork, E. L. (1992). A new theory of disuse and an old theory of stimulus fluctuation. In A. F. Healy, S. M. Kosslyn, & R. M. Shiffrin (Eds.), *Essays in Honor of William K. Estes, Vol. 1: From Learning Theory to Connectionist Theory* (pp. 1935–1967). Erlbaum.

## Key Findings

1. The New Theory of Disuse (NTD) proposes two independent dimensions of memory strength: **storage strength** (how well-learned a memory is, which only increases and never decreases) and **retrieval strength** (how accessible a memory currently is, which fluctuates with use and time).
2. Forgetting reflects a decline in retrieval strength, not erasure of storage strength — memories that seem forgotten are not lost but merely inaccessible.
3. Retrieval strength is most potently increased when retrieval strength is low (i.e., when the memory has been "forgotten"). This is the mechanistic basis for the spacing effect and the testing effect.
4. High current retrieval strength (achieved by massed practice) paradoxically impedes future retention gains because there is less "work" done by retrieval when the item is already highly accessible.
5. These properties explain a range of counter-intuitive phenomena: why spacing is better than massing, why desirable difficulties improve long-term retention, and why forgetting is a precondition for strong relearning.

## Methodology

Theoretical chapter presenting a formal account of memory dynamics. The NTD is presented alongside a review of the older stimulus-fluctuation theory (McGeoch; Estes) to show how the two frameworks address related phenomena.

## Relevance

One of the most influential theoretical frameworks in the study of memory and learning. The storage-strength / retrieval-strength distinction provides the mechanistic basis for understanding why spacing, retrieval practice, and interleaving improve long-term retention despite (or because of) making learning harder in the short term. It is foundational for understanding "desirable difficulties."
