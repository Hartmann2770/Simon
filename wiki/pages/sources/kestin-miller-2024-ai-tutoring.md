## Citation

Kestin, G., & Miller, K. (2024). AI tutoring outperforms active learning. Preprint. https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.16852

## Key Findings

1. In a randomized controlled experiment, students learning physics with an AI tutor (GPT-4 based, designed to follow active learning principles) achieved significantly higher learning gains than students in a traditional active learning classroom — a remarkable finding given that active learning is among the most evidence-supported pedagogical approaches.
2. AI tutoring produced approximately **twice** the learning gains of traditional active learning on post-tests, while also increasing student engagement and confidence.
3. The AI tutor was specifically designed to avoid giving direct answers; instead, it used Socratic questioning, provided hints, and adapted explanations to student responses — implementing the principles of active learning but at an individualized, one-on-one scale.
4. The key advantage of AI tutoring appears to be **immediate, personalized scaffolding**: every student received tailored feedback and guidance at exactly the right moment, rather than the intermittent and group-level attention available in a classroom.
5. The authors caution that not all AI tutors would achieve these results — the system was carefully designed with evidence-based pedagogical principles. Simple Q&A-style AI would not replicate the effects.
6. Results raise important questions about the future role of human instructors and the scalability of high-quality one-on-one tutoring.

## Methodology

Randomized controlled trial with undergraduate physics students. One group used AI tutoring; control group received standard active learning instruction. Pre/post-tests on physics content. Between-subjects design.

## Relevance

Highly significant experimental evidence for AI tutoring as a learning intervention. Connects to Bloom's 2-sigma problem (one-on-one tutoring vastly outperforms classroom instruction) and suggests AI may offer a scalable solution. Essential reference for AI in education discussions.
