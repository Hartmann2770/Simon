## Citation

Leont'ev, A. N. *Activity, Consciousness, and Personality*. Englewood Cliffs: Prentice-Hall, 1978.

## Key Findings

1. Human consciousness is not a passive reflection of the world but is constituted through practical, object-oriented activity — what you do and why shapes what you can think and perceive.
2. Leont'ev formalizes the three-level hierarchical structure of activity: **Activity** (motive-driven, collective), **Action** (goal-directed, individual), and **Operation** (condition-driven, automatic/habitual). The level at which something is performed is not fixed: actions can become operations through routinization; operations can rise to the level of conscious action when disrupted.
3. The "object" (predmet) of an activity is the thing that gives it its direction and meaning — not the physical target but the culturally shaped purpose that motivates the collective.
4. Personality is not an inner property but is constituted through the hierarchy of motives that organize a person's activities; who you are is what you habitually do and why you do it.
5. Alienated labour (in the Marxist sense) is analyzed through the activity framework: when workers' actions are systematically disconnected from meaningful motives, consciousness becomes fragmented.

## Methodology

Theoretical monograph drawing on Soviet psychology, Marxist philosophy of practice, and empirical developmental research. No single empirical study; synthesizes decades of Soviet psychological research.

## Relevance

This is the foundational text of activity theory's second generation. The three-level model (activity/action/operation) is the backbone of virtually all subsequent work in the tradition, including Engeström's activity system model. For learning theory, Leont'ev explains why the same behaviour can count as learning or as mere performance depending on what motive structures it: memorizing words for a test is a different activity than acquiring vocabulary to communicate.
