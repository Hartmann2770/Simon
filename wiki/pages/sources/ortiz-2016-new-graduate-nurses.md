## Citation

Ortiz, J. (2016). New graduate nurses' experiences about lack of professional confidence. *Nurse Education in Practice*, 19, 19–24. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nepr.2016.04.001

## Key Findings

1. Professional confidence is an essential trait for new graduate nurses to provide quality patient care — yet new graduates consistently report a lack of confidence as a major barrier to effective practice during the transition period.
2. Sources of low confidence include: the theory-practice gap (what was learned in education vs. what is encountered clinically), insufficient clinical exposure during training, lack of mentorship and support in the workplace, and inadequate preparation for the emotional demands of nursing.
3. The **reality shock** phenomenon — the jarring discrepancy between idealized expectations from education and the demanding reality of clinical practice — is a primary driver of low confidence and early attrition in nursing.
4. New graduates report that **simulated clinical experiences** in education helped build confidence but could not fully substitute for the complexity of real patient interactions under time pressure.
5. Interprofessional communication (with physicians, allied health, support staff) was an especially underprepared competency: new graduates felt unprepared for the social and political dynamics of healthcare teams.
6. Recommendations include: longer transition programs, designated mentors, reduced patient loads for new graduates, and simulation training that specifically targets confidence-building scenarios.

## Methodology

Qualitative study using semi-structured interviews with new graduate nurses. Thematic analysis of interview data. Interpretive phenomenological approach.

## Relevance

Documents the transition-to-practice challenge in nursing education from the new graduate perspective. Relevant to simulation-based education as a bridge between academic learning and clinical practice, and to understanding what training designs most effectively build professional confidence.
