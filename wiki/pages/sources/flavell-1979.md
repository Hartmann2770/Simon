## Citation

Flavell, J. H. (1979). Metacognition and cognitive monitoring: A new area of cognitive-developmental inquiry. *American Psychologist, 34*(10), 906–911. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.34.10.906

## Key Findings

1. Flavell introduces and defines the term "metacognition" — one's knowledge and beliefs about one's own cognitive processes, including memory, comprehension, and problem-solving.
2. Metacognitive knowledge encompasses knowledge about: (a) persons (including oneself as a cognitive agent), (b) tasks (their difficulty, demands), and (c) strategies (which approaches work for which tasks).
3. Metacognitive experiences are real-time conscious episodes — feelings of knowing, sensing that something is not understood — that accompany cognitive activity.
4. Young children are substantially limited in metacognitive knowledge compared to older children and adults; development involves acquiring increasingly accurate monitoring of one's own cognition.
5. Metacognitive monitoring and self-regulation are learnable skills that can be explicitly taught.

## Methodology

Conceptual/theoretical paper defining the domain of metacognition, synthesising prior developmental research, and setting out a research agenda. Published as a presidential address to the American Psychological Association's Division on Developmental Psychology.

## Relevance

The founding paper of the metacognition research programme. Flavell's tripartite framework (metacognitive knowledge about persons, tasks, and strategies) remains the standard conceptual foundation. Directly relevant to learning research because metacognitive accuracy determines whether students use effective learning strategies, whether they know when they have and have not learned, and whether they can regulate study time effectively.
