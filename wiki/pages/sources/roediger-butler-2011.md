## Citation

Roediger, H. L., & Butler, A. C. (2011). The critical role of retrieval practice in long-term retention. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 15*(1), 20–27. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2010.09.003

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/5UFAV73J/attachment/MLKTRDMZ/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Retrieval practice is the most powerful single learning strategy for enhancing long-term memory — more effective than re-reading, concept mapping, or other widely used study strategies.
2. Retrieval enhances retention not just for the practised material itself but can facilitate learning of related, non-practised information (transfer-appropriate processing).
3. Corrective feedback after retrieval practice potentiates the benefit: testing with feedback outperforms testing without feedback.
4. Retrieval practice effects have been demonstrated across many materials (facts, concepts, prose) and populations.
5. The mechanism likely involves elaborative retrieval: students must reconstruct information rather than merely recognise it, creating richer memory traces.

## Methodology

Narrative review in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, synthesising two decades of experimental research on retrieval practice. The review covers foundational studies, mechanisms, moderators (feedback, type of test, spacing), and classroom applications.

## Relevance

A concise but authoritative review aimed at cognitive scientists and educators. Establishes retrieval practice as a "cornerstone" learning strategy and motivates the integration of low-stakes testing into instruction. Its accessibility and journal prestige made it widely influential in bridging the science-practice gap.
