## Citation

Oermann, M. H., Krusmark, M. A., Kardong-Edgren, S., Jastrzembski, T. S., & Gluck, K. A. (2021). Personalized training schedules for retention and sustainment of cardiopulmonary resuscitation skills. *Simulation in Healthcare*. https://doi.org/10.1097/SIH.0000000000000559

## Key Findings

1. At the end of the acquisition period, shorter training intervals (1 or 7 days) produced better immediate performance than longer intervals (30 or 90 days).
2. At 3–6 months after acquisition, performance was better for participants trained with longer initial intervals — a classic spacing reversal demonstrating the long-term benefit of distributed practice.
3. A Predictive Performance Optimizer (PPO) — an algorithm prescribing personalised training intervals based on predicted learning and forgetting curves — outperformed fixed-interval refreshers for compression performance variability at 1 year.
4. Six-month fixed-interval refreshers were worse than either 3-month or PPO-prescribed intervals on key CPR metrics.
5. Personalised scheduling can improve performance, minimise variability, and reduce overall training time — a finding with substantial practical implications for healthcare training programmes.

## Methodology

Multiphased longitudinal study with nursing students randomly assigned to two initial-acquisition conditions (short versus long spacing intervals) and additionally randomised to three sustainment conditions (3-month, 6-month, or PPO-prescribed refreshers). Skills were assessed immediately after acquisition and at multiple follow-up intervals up to one year.

## Relevance

A strong applied demonstration of spaced repetition principles in a high-stakes real-world context (CPR certification maintenance). The study goes beyond standard spacing-effect research to test algorithmically personalised scheduling — directly relevant for adaptive learning systems and spaced repetition software design. It also illustrates the classic paradox: short-spaced practice feels more effective initially but leads to faster forgetting.
