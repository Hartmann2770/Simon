## Citation

Dehaene, S. (2020). *How We Learn: Why Brains Learn Better Than Any Machine — and How to Harness That Gift*. Viking.

## Key Findings

1. The brain is not a blank slate or passive information receiver but an **active hypothesis-testing machine**: learning consists of forming, testing, and revising internal models of the world. Effective education works with this process, not against it.
2. Dehaene identifies **four pillars of learning**: **attention** (focusing on what is relevant), **active engagement** (generating and testing predictions), **error feedback** (the brain's most powerful learning signal comes from prediction errors), and **consolidation** (sleep and rest are essential for memory consolidation and not merely recovery time).
3. **Sleep is not optional for learning**: during sleep, the brain replays, consolidates, and reorganizes newly acquired information. Chronic sleep deprivation is one of the most powerful destroyers of learning capacity.
4. **Curiosity** is not a personality trait but a neurological state: when the brain detects an information gap (something unknown that is relevant to its models), dopamine is released and attention is recruited. Effective teaching creates and resolves productive information gaps.
5. **Prediction errors** drive learning more powerfully than correct responses: when a prediction is confirmed, the brain learns little; when a prediction is violated (you expected one thing, and another happened), maximum synaptic change occurs. This is why retrieval practice works — it creates prediction opportunities and errors.
6. The book is broadly critical of passive educational methods (watching, listening, reading without engagement) in favor of active, prediction-generating, error-correcting approaches.

## Methodology

Synthesis of cognitive neuroscience, with references to brain imaging studies, animal learning research, and educational experiments. Written for a general audience by a leading neuroscientist.

## Relevance

Provides neuroscientific grounding for evidence-based learning strategies: retrieval practice, spaced repetition, active learning, and the critical importance of sleep. Connects directly to the testing effect, desirable difficulties, and feedback literatures.
