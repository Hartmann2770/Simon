## Citation

Wenger, Etienne. "Communities of Practice and Social Learning Systems: the Career of a Concept." In *Social Learning Systems and Communities of Practice*, edited by Chris Blackmore. London: Springer, 2010. ISBN: 978-1-84996-132-5.

## Key Findings

1. This chapter traces the intellectual evolution of the CoP concept from its origins in the 1991 *Situated Learning* book through two decades of uptake across research, management, policy, and professional development — showing how the concept was stretched, simplified, and sometimes distorted in use.
2. Wenger distinguishes between **communities of practice as a specific social structure** (groups meeting the criteria of mutual engagement, joint enterprise, shared repertoire) and CoP **as a perspective** (a lens for analyzing any social learning system). Much confusion in the literature arises from collapsing these two senses.
3. The "career" of a concept includes not just academic refinement but **institutionalization** — adoption into managerial toolkits, government policy, and educational reform agendas. This popularization changes the concept itself: managers began "creating" CoPs by decree, which misses the point that CoPs are not designed top-down but emerge from practice.
4. Wenger revisits and extends the earlier framework by emphasizing **social learning systems**: configurations of overlapping and interacting communities, connected through boundary objects and brokers, that collectively constitute the learning capacity of a domain or organization.
5. The key tension in CoP theory is between **cultivation and self-organization**: CoPs cannot be commanded into existence, but they can be cultivated — provided with conditions, resources, and legitimacy. The art of CoP stewardship is supporting without controlling.
6. Wenger reflects critically on the tendency in organizational practice to reduce CoPs to knowledge management tools, losing the identity, meaning, and political dimensions that make them educationally significant.

## Methodology

Conceptual and reflexive chapter. Wenger reviews the history of his own concept's reception and application, synthesizing insights from two decades of empirical work and practitioner feedback. No new primary data are presented.

## Relevance

Important for understanding what the CoP concept is and is not, and why its adoption in management and education sometimes fails. The distinction between CoP-as-structure and CoP-as-perspective is analytically valuable for researchers designing studies. For practitioners, the cultivation-versus-control tension is the practical core: support the conditions for shared practice to emerge rather than mandating participation.
