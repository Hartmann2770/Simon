## Citation

Eccles, J. S., & Wigfield, A. (2002). Motivational beliefs, values, and goals. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 53, 109–132.

## Key Findings

1. The **Expectancy-Value Model** (Eccles et al.) predicts achievement choices and performance: students are most likely to engage in, persist at, and perform well on tasks they expect to succeed at *and* that they value.
2. **Task value** has four components: **intrinsic value** (inherent interest/enjoyment), **attainment value** (importance to self-concept and identity), **utility value** (usefulness for future goals), and **cost** (what is given up or risked by engaging). All four components independently predict engagement and persistence.
3. **Expectancy beliefs** (what students expect to achieve) are distinct from **ability self-concepts** (what students think they are capable of), though the two are related. Both predict achievement, but through different pathways.
4. The motivational system is deeply developmental: young children tend toward unrealistic optimism about their abilities; this declines through middle school as social comparison and grades provide more differentiated feedback. The decline is culturally mediated, not biologically inevitable.
5. Achievement goals (mastery vs. performance goals) interact with ability beliefs and values: mastery orientation (focused on learning) sustains motivation better than performance orientation (focused on demonstrating ability) especially when difficulty is encountered.
6. Socialization influences — parents, teachers, culture — shape children's beliefs and values through modeling, direct feedback, and the interpretations they provide for children's performances.

## Methodology

Comprehensive theoretical review of developmental and social-cognitive research on motivation. Integrates decades of field and laboratory research on expectancy-value, goal theory, and ability self-concept. Draws on Eccles et al.'s own longitudinal studies as well as broader literature.

## Relevance

Establishes the theoretical foundations of expectancy-value theory as a framework for understanding what motivates learners to choose, persist at, and achieve in educational tasks. Useful for designing learning experiences that increase perceived value (utility, interest, relevance to identity) and support positive expectations of success.
