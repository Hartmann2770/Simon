## Citation

Bahrami, B., Olsen, K., Latham, P. E., Roepstorff, A., Rees, G., & Frith, C. D. (2010). Optimally interacting minds. *Science*, 329(5995), 1081–1085. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1185718

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/KX72NEFZ/attachment/DGXHIKVH/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Two people can achieve better-than-individual perceptual decisions when they are allowed to exchange confidence information — the "two heads are better than one" effect is empirically confirmed under specific conditions.
2. The key condition for dyadic performance to exceed individual performance is that both individuals must share their **subjective confidence** in their perceptions, not merely their categorical judgments. Without confidence-sharing, dyadic performance is no better than (or even worse than) the better individual.
3. A mathematical model (optimal Bayesian integration) correctly predicts when dyads outperform individuals and when they do not — suggesting that the mechanism is rational combination of independent evidence sources.
4. When individuals have unequal sensitivity (one person is reliably better than the other), the better strategy for the dyad is to weight the more sensitive person's judgment more heavily. Communication protocols that enable this re-weighting are essential for collective intelligence.
5. The findings generalize beyond perception: any domain where individuals have independent evidence and can communicate uncertainty (medicine, finance, group decision-making) should see collective benefits when confidence-sharing is enabled.

## Methodology

Experimental psychophysics with computational modeling. Participants performed visual perception tasks individually and in dyads, with and without verbal communication. Signal detection theory and Bayesian modeling used to derive predictions.

## Relevance

Provides cognitive-scientific grounding for collaborative learning: explains *why* collaborative work can exceed individual performance, under what conditions it does so, and what communication structures are necessary. Supports instructional design of peer learning activities that require genuine sharing of uncertainty and reasoning.
