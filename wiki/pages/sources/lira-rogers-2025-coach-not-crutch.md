## Citation

Lira, R., & Rogers, T. (2025). Coach not crutch: AI assistance can enhance rather than hinder skill development. Preprint. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2502.02880

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/643CCN4D/attachment/QGQPY7H5/reader)

## Key Findings

1. The study directly tests the "crutch hypothesis" — that using AI for writing practice will reduce learning and skill development compared to unassisted practice. Results show that, contrary to predictions, AI-assisted practice *enhanced* rather than hindered writing skill development.
2. Participants randomly assigned to AI-assisted writing practice showed greater gains on subsequent unassisted writing tasks than those who practiced without AI support — a transfer effect.
3. The explanation: well-designed AI assistance functions as a **coach** rather than a replacement. It provides immediate, specific, actionable feedback that learners can apply, improving their own skills over time.
4. The crutch effect (where AI use substitutes for skill development) is predicted by lay intuition but not confirmed experimentally — suggesting that the folk theory about AI making people intellectually dependent is not universally supported by evidence.
5. The distinction between **AI as crutch** (doing the task for the learner) and **AI as coach** (scaffolding the learner's own performance) is critical for design: the former replaces learning; the latter accelerates it.
6. These findings connect to Lodge and Loble's cognitive offloading analysis: the key variable is not whether AI is used but *how* — whether it replaces or supplements the cognitive processes that build skill.

## Methodology

Randomized experiment. Participants assigned to AI-assisted or unassisted writing practice conditions. Outcomes measured on subsequent unassisted writing tests. Pre/post design with transfer test.

## Relevance

Strong experimental evidence against the blanket claim that AI use undermines learning. Provides nuanced framework (coach vs. crutch) for thinking about when AI assistance is beneficial and when harmful. Complements Lodge/Loble on cognitive offloading and Kestin/Miller on AI tutoring.
