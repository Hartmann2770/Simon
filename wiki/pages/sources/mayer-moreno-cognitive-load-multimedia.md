## Citation

Mayer, R. E., & Moreno, R. (2003). Nine ways to reduce cognitive load in multimedia learning. *Educational Psychologist, 38*(1), 43–52.

## Key Findings

1. Multimedia learning involves three memory systems: sensory memory (brief, large-capacity), working memory (brief, limited-capacity — separately for auditory and visual channels), and long-term memory (durable, large-capacity). Overloading either channel undermines learning.
2. Three sources of cognitive overload in multimedia are identified: **essential processing** (required by the material's complexity), **incidental processing** (caused by extraneous design), and **representational holding** (holding one representation while processing another).
3. Nine specific design interventions are proposed, each targeting one source of overload:
   - *Offloading* (shift words from visual to auditory channel)
   - *Segmenting* (learner-paced chunks instead of continuous presentation)
   - *Pre-training* (teach component names/characteristics before the main lesson)
   - *Weeding* (remove interesting-but-irrelevant material)
   - *Signalling* (highlight essential material to guide attention)
   - *Aligning* (place printed words adjacent to corresponding graphics)
   - *Eliminating redundancy* (avoid simultaneous on-screen text and identical narration)
   - *Synchronising* (present corresponding narration and animation at the same time)
   - *Individualising* (provide explanatory detail to novices; reduce it for experts)
4. Each intervention is linked to experimental evidence; the modality principle (offloading) and the split-attention principle (aligning) have the strongest empirical support.
5. The framework emphasises that *less is often more* in multimedia design — adding more content frequently hurts learning by increasing extraneous load.

## Methodology

Theoretical review and framework paper. Synthesises experimental studies by Mayer's group and related researchers to build a taxonomy of overload sources and corresponding design solutions.

## Relevance

The most practically actionable paper in the multimedia learning literature. Bridges Sweller's cognitive load theory and Mayer's cognitive theory of multimedia learning into concrete design rules. Widely cited in e-learning and instructional design practice.
