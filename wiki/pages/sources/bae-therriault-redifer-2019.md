## Citation

Bae, C. L., Therriault, D. J., & Redifer, J. L. (2019). Investigating the testing effect: Retrieval as a characteristic of effective study strategies. *Learning and Instruction, 60*, 206–214. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2017.12.008

## Key Findings

1. Free recall and practice quizzing were the most effective types of retrieval practice for long-term retention after a 7-day delay.
2. Test-generation (generating one's own test questions) and keyword strategies were less effective alone but produced significant benefits when combined with free recall.
3. Repeated retrieval (study-test learning condition) outperformed single retrieval and rehearsal (study-study-study) conditions, confirming the advantage of multiple retrieval attempts.
4. There was a significant interaction between learning condition and type of retrieval strategy — the combination of retrieval type and frequency matters, not just one or the other.
5. The study extends the testing-effect literature by comparing multiple retrieval formats within the same experiment, showing differential effectiveness across strategies.

## Methodology

Between-subjects experiment with 338 undergraduates randomly assigned to one of nine conditions crossing retrieval strategy type (free recall, practice quizzing, test-generation, keyword) with learning condition (repeated retrieval, single retrieval, or rehearsal). Retention was tested after a 7-day delay.

## Relevance

Provides nuanced evidence about which specific retrieval formats work best, moving beyond the binary contrast of "test vs. no test." The finding that free recall and quizzing are most effective, and that combining strategies amplifies benefits, informs the design of self-testing and practice quizzing tools.
