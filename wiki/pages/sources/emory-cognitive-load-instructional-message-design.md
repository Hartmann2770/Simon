## Key Findings

The chapter applies cognitive load theory to the specific sub-domain of *instructional message design* — the craft of presenting content through text, graphics, audio, and layout in ways that optimise learning. Key points (based on available metadata):

1. CLT provides an explicit rationale for message-design decisions: every design choice either adds to or subtracts from the learner's available working memory.
2. The chapter likely covers the principal CLT effects as they apply to message design: split-attention, redundancy, modality, and coherence effects.
3. Instructional message design must attend to both the *format* of information (visual vs. auditory, text vs. diagram) and its *sequence* (what the learner sees first shapes what schemas are available to integrate new content).

Useful bridge between CLT as an abstract cognitive theory and the practical craft of designing instructional materials (slides, e-learning screens, printed guides). Complements Mayer's work on multimedia specifically.
