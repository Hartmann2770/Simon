## Citation

Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000). Intrinsic and extrinsic motivations: Classic definitions and new directions. *Contemporary Educational Psychology*, 25(1), 54–67.

## Key Findings

1. **Intrinsic motivation** is motivation to engage in an activity for its inherent satisfaction — the activity is done because it is interesting, enjoyable, or personally meaningful, not for any separable consequence.
2. **Extrinsic motivation** covers a spectrum of regulatory styles (Self-Determination Theory's continuum), from fully controlled external regulation (doing something to get a reward or avoid punishment) through introjection (internal pressure, e.g., shame) and identification (personally valued but not inherently interesting) to integrated regulation (fully assimilated into identity).
3. The conventional binary opposition of intrinsic vs. extrinsic is too crude: SDT shows that extrinsic motivation can be more or less autonomous (self-determined) depending on the degree of internalization.
4. Three basic psychological needs support internalization and intrinsic motivation: **autonomy** (sense of volition), **competence** (sense of effectiveness), and **relatedness** (sense of connection to others). Contexts that satisfy these needs promote autonomous motivation; contexts that thwart them undermine it.
5. Controlling contexts (surveillance, deadlines, contingent rewards, threats, imposed goals) reliably undermine intrinsic motivation and promote controlled extrinsic regulation — even when the initial activity was intrinsically interesting.
6. Educational environments with autonomy-supportive teachers, rationale-giving, meaningful choices, and acknowledgment of feelings show better outcomes on intrinsic motivation, learning quality, and well-being than controlling environments.

## Methodology

Theoretical review article. Integrates 30+ years of experimental and field research on intrinsic and extrinsic motivation and Self-Determination Theory. Reviews key studies including cognitive evaluation theory experiments (effects of reward on intrinsic motivation) and organismic integration theory research.

## Relevance

Core reference for motivation in learning design. Explains why reward-based systems can undermine engagement, why learner autonomy matters, and how to design learning environments that support self-determination. Essential for understanding the motivational foundations of instructional design choices.
