## Citation

Dourish, P. (2006). Re-space-ing place: "Place" and "space" ten years on. In *CSCW '06: Proceedings of the 2006 ACM Conference on Computer Supported Cooperative Work* (pp. 299–308). ACM Press. https://doi.org/10.1145/1180875.1180921

## Key Findings

1. Revisiting the influential Harrison and Dourish (1996) paper ten years later, Dourish reconsiders and refines the space/place distinction. He argues the earlier framing was too clean: space and place are not simply two stages (inert structure vs. social meaning) but are mutually constitutive.
2. Physical and digital spaces are not produced prior to social life and then filled with meaning — rather, they are continuously produced and reproduced through ongoing social practice. Space is always already social.
3. **Material and social are inseparable**: the physical properties of environments and the social meanings attributed to them co-constitute each other continuously. Dourish draws on phenomenological and post-structural theory to make this case.
4. For collaborative systems design, the implication is that neither "build the space and communities will form" nor "document the community and build to support it" captures the dynamic: systems need to support the ongoing, mutual production of space-as-place through use.
5. The paper introduces **embodied interaction** as a richer framework than the space/place binary for understanding how people inhabit and make meaning in hybrid physical-digital environments.

## Methodology

Theoretical paper. Conceptual revision and extension of prior work, drawing on social theory and CSCW literature.

## Relevance

Important theoretical refinement of the space/place framework. Relevant to understanding how digital learning environments are produced through use and how design should support rather than pre-determine social meaning.
