## Citation

Abrahams, M. (2023). *Think Faster, Talk Smarter: How to Speak Successfully When You're Put on the Spot*. Simon & Schuster.

## Key Findings

1. Most communication anxiety and failure arises in **spontaneous** situations — job interviews, Q&A after presentations, unexpected questions — not in planned speeches. Yet most communication training focuses on prepared speaking.
2. The core skill for spontaneous communication is **structured improvisation**: having flexible frameworks and habits that allow rapid, coherent, appropriate responses without full preparation.
3. A key framework offered: **What? So What? Now What?** — briefly state what happened or what the issue is, explain why it matters, and indicate what should come next. This three-part structure works across almost any spontaneous communication situation.
4. **Anxiety reframing** is more effective than anxiety suppression: treating communication anxiety as excitement rather than threat improves performance, because the physiological states are similar but the cognitive framing changes behavior.
5. Active listening — truly attending to what the other person is saying rather than formulating a response — is the foundation of good spontaneous communication and is a learnable skill.
6. Improvisation techniques (from theatrical improv) translate directly to conversational competence: saying "yes, and" (accepting and building on what the other person offers) rather than "yes, but" (accepting but redirecting) improves collaborative dialogue.

## Methodology

Practitioner book. Drawing on Abrahams' experience as a Stanford communications professor and coach. Synthesis of research on communication, anxiety, and improvisational performance.

## Relevance

Tangentially relevant to learning — specifically to understanding communication as a learnable skill and to the design of training for interpersonal communication competencies. Less central to core learning science than most wiki sources.
