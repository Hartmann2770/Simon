## Citation

## Key Findings

1. Despite the long-promised disruption of education technology (e.g., MOOCs), education has remained remarkably resistant to transformation — largely because prior edtech mimicked traditional instruction rather than reconceiving it.
2. Generative AI represents a qualitatively different technological moment: unlike previous tools, LLMs can adapt to individual learners, provide natural-language scaffolding, and generate bespoke content on demand. This removes the prior constraint that made scalable personalization impossible.
3. However, the AI education revolution will only materialize if AI tools are designed around evidence-based learning science rather than around replicating the existing classroom or textbook model at scale.
4. The most promising application is **AI as intelligent tutor**: providing personalized, adaptive, Socratic dialogue that mirrors the proven benefits of one-on-one human tutoring (Bloom's 2-sigma effect).
5. Risk: AI could equally be used to make existing bad practices faster — generating more quizzes, more content dumps, more rote assessment — without improving learning quality. Hardman argues for learning-science-first design principles.

## Methodology

TEDx talk. Expert synthesis and argument from practitioner-researcher perspective. Not a primary study.

## Relevance

Accessible framing of the AI-in-education opportunity and its risks. Useful for situating Hardman's DOMS work and other AI education research in a broader context of edtech history and potential.
