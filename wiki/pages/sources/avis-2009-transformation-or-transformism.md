## Citation

Avis, James. "Transformation or Transformism: Engeström's Version of Activity Theory?" *Educational Review* 61, no. 2 (May 2009): 151–165. DOI: 10.1080/00131910902844754. https://doi.org/10.1080/00131910902844754

## Key Findings

1. Avis argues that Engeström's expansive learning framework, despite its radical rhetoric of transformation, often functions as "transformism" — incremental accommodation to existing capitalist work relations rather than genuine structural change.
2. The Change Laboratory method can be co-opted by management to extract worker knowledge and increase productivity without addressing underlying power asymmetries or fundamental contradictions.
3. Engeström's concept of contradiction is criticized for focusing too narrowly on operational tensions within an activity system while underplaying the structural contradictions arising from the capitalist mode of production.
4. The paper draws on critical theory (especially Gramsci) to argue that truly transformative learning must involve political consciousness and challenge structural inequalities, not merely redesign work processes.
5. Despite these criticisms, Avis acknowledges the framework's value for analyzing the complexity of workplace activity and its genuine attention to collective rather than purely individual learning.

## Methodology

Critical theoretical review. Draws on Engeström's published work, vocational education and training (VET) literature, and Gramscian Marxist theory. No primary empirical data collected.

## Relevance

This is the most prominent critical assessment of Engeström's project. It is useful for understanding the political limits of activity theory as a tool for workplace learning research, and as a corrective to uncritical adoption of the framework. For learning theory, it raises a general question: can educational interventions transform activity systems, or do they merely optimize them? The answer matters for how one evaluates any learning design that claims to promote deep or transformative learning.
