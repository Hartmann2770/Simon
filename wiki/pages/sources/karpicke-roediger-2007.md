## Citation

Karpicke, J. D., & Roediger, H. L. (2007). Repeated retrieval during learning is the key to long-term retention. *Journal of Memory and Language, 57*(2), 151–162. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jml.2006.09.004

[Read PDF in Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/esaitch/items/ES45PIGG/attachment/UWXYR8L7/reader)

## Key Findings

1. Repeated retrieval of previously recalled items enhanced one-week retention by more than 100% compared to dropping those items from further testing.
2. Repeated studying of previously recalled items did not benefit retention relative to dropping those items from further study.
3. Alternating study and test trials produced the best overall retention.
4. The results directly demonstrate that it is the retrieval process — not mere exposure to the correct answer — that drives long-term retention.
5. These findings replicate and extend Tulving's (1967) foundational work on multitrial free recall.

## Methodology

Two laboratory experiments with word-list learning. Subjects studied lists and then took multiple study/test trials. In Experiment 2, recalled items were either dropped from further study/testing or continued to be studied/tested, allowing isolation of the specific contribution of repeated retrieval versus repeated exposure.

## Relevance

A landmark empirical study that cleanly isolates the causal role of retrieval versus restudying. The 100%+ retention advantage for repeated retrieval is one of the most striking quantitative results in the testing-effect literature and is frequently cited in both research and popular-science treatments of learning. Directly informs spaced repetition system design.
