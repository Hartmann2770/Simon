## Citation

Engeström, Yrjö. *Developmental Work Research: Expanding Activity Theory in Practice*. Berlin: Lehmanns Media, 2005. 486 pp. ISBN 3-86541-069-3. (ICHS, Bd. 12.)

## Key Findings

1. Developmental Work Research (DWR) is a methodology for studying and promoting transformation in work organizations by applying expansive learning theory within real workplaces.
2. The Change Laboratory is the central DWR intervention method: a structured setting in which workers collectively analyze contradictions in their activity system and design new models of practice.
3. Learning at work is driven by tensions and contradictions that accumulate until they destabilize current practice, creating a need for qualitatively new ways of working.
4. The object of activity — what the collective work is fundamentally about — is treated as expansive and changeable, not fixed; redefining the object is a key step in organizational learning.
5. DWR is simultaneously a research methodology and a practical intervention, positioning researchers as co-designers of change rather than detached observers.
6. The book documents multiple long-term DWR projects in Finnish hospitals, schools, and service organizations, showing how the Change Laboratory method was refined over two decades.

## Methodology

Longitudinal intervention research in Finnish organizational settings. Each study involves formative interventions: video analysis of work activity, mirroring data back to workers, collaborative modeling sessions, and follow-up observations. The approach is explicitly not experimental; it is designed to generate and study expansive learning in the wild.

## Relevance

This is the definitive methodological handbook for applying Engeström's expansive learning framework in practice. It shows how abstract concepts (activity system, contradiction, zone of proximal development for collectives) translate into concrete research design. For learning theory, the key contribution is documenting what it looks like when entire organizations learn by transforming their own activity systems — a scale of learning rarely addressed by cognitive approaches.
