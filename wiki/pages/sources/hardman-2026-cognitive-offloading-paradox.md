## Citation

Hardman, P. (2026, April 16). The "cognitive offloading" paradox. *Dr Phil's Newsletter*. https://drphilippahardman.substack.com/p/the-cognitive-offloading-paradox

## Key Findings

1. **Synthesises the offloading debate**: Reviews three studies documenting negative effects of cognitive offloading (Gerlich 2025: negative correlation with critical thinking; Kosmyna et al. 2025: weakest neural engagement via EEG; Favero et al. 2025: offloading undermines learning unless freed effort is redirected) before presenting Wang & Zhang (2026) as a counterpoint.
2. **The U-shaped curve**: Hardman distils Wang & Zhang's three-zone model into a key design insight — partial AI use is worse than no AI at all, while committed strategic offloading of entire task categories dramatically improves learning. The "messy middle" of scattered AI assistance creates coordination overhead with no freed capacity.
3. **Six design principles**: (1) Substantial or nothing — delegate entire categories or avoid AI; (2) Partnership framing — position AI as collaborator to activate both vigilance and delegation; (3) Verification built into workflow, not warnings; (4) Learner-first sequencing — require learner generation before AI input; (5) Error identification over correction — AI should flag, not fix; (6) Unassisted assessment — measure independent capability (g=0.751 assisted vs g=0.369 independent across 35 studies).
4. **Offload/protect taxonomy**: Offload summarisation, literature review first passes, data organisation, fact verification. Protect problem formulation, framework critique, assumption questioning, original argument construction, error correction, and final assessment.
5. **AI as design variable**: The article reframes AI from inherently good or bad to a variable whose impact depends entirely on instructional design — specifically whether freed cognitive resources are deliberately redirected toward higher-order thinking.

## Methodology

Practitioner synthesis / newsletter essay. Draws on Wang & Zhang (2026), Lodge & Loble (2026), Gerlich (2025), Kosmyna et al. (2025), Favero et al. (2025), Xu et al. (2026), Bjork (1994), Slamecka & Graf (1978), Karpicke & Blunt (2011).

## Relevance

Translates the Wang & Zhang (2026) academic findings into actionable instructional design principles. The six-principle framework and offload/protect taxonomy are directly usable for learning designers working with AI tools. Updates and extends Hardman's earlier work on AI in learning design.
