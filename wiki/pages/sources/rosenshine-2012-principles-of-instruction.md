## Citation

Rosenshine, B. (2012). Principles of instruction: Research-based strategies that all teachers should know. *American Educator, 36*(1), 12–19, 39. https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ971753

## Key Findings

1. Ten research-based principles of instruction are presented, derived from three converging sources: cognitive science research on memory, observation studies of master teachers, and research on cognitive supports (scaffolding).
2. The ten principles are:
   - Begin each lesson with a short review of previous learning
   - Present new material in small steps, with student practice after each step
   - Ask a large number of questions and check for student understanding
   - Provide models and worked examples
   - Guide student practice
   - Check for student understanding before independent practice
   - Obtain a high success rate (80% or above) during guided practice
   - Provide scaffolds during difficult tasks
   - Require and monitor independent practice
   - Engage students in weekly and monthly review
3. The convergence of cognitive science, master-teacher research, and scaffolding research on essentially the same set of procedures is presented as strong validation — three independent lines of evidence pointing in the same direction.
4. **Daily review** serves to strengthen previous learning and make retrieval easier; **guided practice with checking** ensures students do not practice errors before achieving independence.
5. The principle of **high success rate** (around 80% correct during guided practice) reflects a balance: too easy means insufficient challenge; too hard overwhelms working memory and discourages learners.

## Methodology

Synthetic review article. Rosenshine synthesises decades of process-product classroom research, cognitive science, and observational studies of expert teachers. Published in *American Educator* for a practitioner audience, then reprinted by the International Academy of Education and UNESCO.

## Relevance

One of the most influential evidence-based teaching frameworks globally, widely used in teacher professional development. Bridges cognitive science (especially working memory limits and retrieval practice) with practical classroom instruction. The principles are highly compatible with spaced-repetition and retrieval-practice research.
