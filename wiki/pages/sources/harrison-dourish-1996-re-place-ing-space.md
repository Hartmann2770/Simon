## Citation

Harrison, S., & Dourish, P. (1996). Re-place-ing space: The roles of place and space in collaborative systems. In *Proceedings of the 1996 ACM Conference on Computer Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW '96)* (pp. 67–76). ACM Press.

## Key Findings

1. A foundational distinction is drawn between **space** (the physical or geometrical structure — arrangement, dimensions, locations) and **place** (space invested with meaning through social practice and cultural convention). Space is given; place is made.
2. In collaborative computing systems, much design effort is spent on creating spaces (virtual rooms, shared environments) but the actual collaboration depends on place — the social understandings and behavioral norms that participants bring to and create within those spaces.
3. **Behaviorally appropriate places** emerge from social conventions, shared history, and repeated practice rather than from technical design. The same space can function as different places for different communities.
4. Existing collaborative systems often fail because they attempt to enforce place-like constraints through spatial structure (e.g., "meeting rooms") without enabling the social processes through which places actually form.
5. The lesson for system design: rather than encoding specific place meanings into architecture, designers should create flexible spaces that afford rich social interaction, allowing communities to construct their own places within them.

## Methodology

Theoretical paper. Conceptual analysis drawing on geography (notably Tuan), sociology, and empirical observation of collaborative work environments.

## Relevance

Provides the conceptual foundation for distinguishing space from place in digital/collaborative environments. Widely cited in e-learning and CSCW literature. Directly relevant to understanding how online learning environments are experienced differently by different communities of learners.
