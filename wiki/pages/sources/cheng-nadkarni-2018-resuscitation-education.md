## Citation

Cheng, A., Nadkarni, V., et al. (2018). Resuscitation education science: Educational strategies to improve outcomes from cardiac arrest. *Circulation*, 138(6), e82–e122.

## Key Findings

1. **Mastery learning** — practice to a pre-defined performance standard rather than for a fixed time period — is the most effective educational strategy for resuscitation skills. Learners must demonstrate competence, not merely exposure.
2. **Deliberate practice** with immediate, specific feedback is essential: resuscitation training without corrective feedback produces substantially worse skill acquisition and retention than training with real-time performance data (e.g., CPR feedback devices).
3. **Spaced learning** significantly outperforms massed practice for resuscitation skills: brief, frequent retraining (e.g., brief monthly refreshers) produces better long-term skill retention than annual full-day courses.
4. Simulation fidelity should be matched to learning objectives: low-fidelity mannequins are adequate for skill acquisition of motor components; higher fidelity adds value primarily for team training and complex decision-making scenarios.
5. **Team training** in simulated resuscitation scenarios improves both individual skill and team coordination — particularly communication patterns, role clarity, and leadership behaviors that are difficult to develop in individual training.
6. The evidence base clearly supports a transition from time-based to competency-based training models in resuscitation education.

## Methodology

Comprehensive evidence review commissioned for the American Heart Association. Systematic literature review of resuscitation education research, expert consensus process, evidence grading.

## Relevance

Highly relevant to simulation-based education in healthcare. Provides strong evidence for mastery learning, spaced practice, and deliberate practice as the foundations of effective clinical skills training. Directly applicable to nursing and medical education design.
