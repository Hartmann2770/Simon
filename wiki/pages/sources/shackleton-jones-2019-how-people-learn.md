## Citation

Shackleton-Jones, N., & Schank, R. (2019). *How people learn: Designing education and training that works to improve performance*. Kogan Page.

## Key Findings

1. The book proposes a provocative thesis: most of what organisations call "learning" does not actually improve performance, because it is designed around content delivery rather than around the psychological conditions that cause learning to occur.
2. Shackleton-Jones argues that learning is fundamentally *affective*: people learn what they care about, what is emotionally relevant to their situation. Motivation and affect are not add-ons but preconditions of learning.
3. A distinction is drawn between two types of learning interventions: **resources** (content people can search and use when they need it) and **references** (material designed to support a specific performance context). Most L&D conflates these, producing neither effective learning nor useful resources.
4. The marketing analogy is central: just as marketing must understand what customers care about before attempting to persuade them, learning design must understand what learners care about before designing instruction.
5. The book advocates for *performance-centred design*: begin with the performance you want to improve, work backwards to identify what knowledge/skill/attitude is blocking that performance, then design the minimum necessary intervention.
6. Contemporary AI and neuroscience findings are invoked to support the affect-centred account of learning.

## Methodology

Practitioner/trade book presenting a design philosophy. Draws on Schank's case-based learning research, neuroscience, and Shackleton-Jones's L&D practice experience. Not a systematic academic review.

## Relevance

Challenges mainstream instructional design frameworks (particularly content-centric and objectives-driven approaches). Provides an alternative frame — performance-centred and affect-driven — that complements CLT's cognitive focus by foregrounding motivation and context.
