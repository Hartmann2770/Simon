## Key Findings

1. Retrieval-based learning — the enhancement of long-term retention and meaningful understanding through active retrieval practice — has a documented history stretching back to the earliest experimental research on learning and memory.
2. Despite its age, the topic received only sporadic interest for most of the twentieth century; renewed intense focus came in the early 2000s as part of the science-to-practice translation movement.
3. Foundational studies established that retrieval practice outperforms restudying even when testing does not add time to study.
4. Contemporary work shows that retrieval practice promotes meaningful, transferable learning — not just rote recall of surface facts.
5. Retrieval practice benefits generalise across diverse educational settings, materials, and populations.
6. Theoretical accounts include the elaborative retrieval hypothesis, the contextual reinstatement account, and memory reconsolidation.

## Methodology

Selective narrative review covering foundational and contemporary research, with emphasis on the theoretical development of retrieval-based learning as a distinct research programme within cognitive science and its translation into educational practice.

## Relevance

Provides the most accessible overview of the full arc of retrieval-practice research — from historical origins to modern applications. The chapter situates retrieval-based learning within the broader science-education interface and is an ideal entry point for new readers of the literature.
