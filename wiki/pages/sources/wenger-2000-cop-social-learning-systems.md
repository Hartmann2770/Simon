## Citation

Wenger, Etienne. "Communities of Practice and Social Learning Systems." *Organization* 7, no. 2 (2000): 225–246. DOI: 10.1177/135050840072002.

## Key Findings

1. Organizations and broader social systems can be understood as **social learning systems** — configurations of communities of practice connected through boundary processes. The capacity of a system to learn depends on the health and interconnection of its constituent communities.
2. Wenger proposes a **social definition of learning**: learning is not primarily the acquisition of content but the transformation of identity and the development of competence within communities of practice.
3. Three **modes of belonging** structure how individuals relate to communities:
   - *Engagement*: direct participation in joint activities with other members
   - *Imagination*: constructing an image of community through reflection and perspective-taking (even without direct engagement)
   - *Alignment*: coordinating one's actions to fit broader structures, standards, and enterprises that extend beyond one's immediate community
4. **Competence** within a community is defined by three criteria: members recognize each other as competent; competence involves accountability to joint enterprise; and competence is grounded in a shared repertoire. These criteria define what it means to "know" in a given domain.
5. **Identity** is the intersection of membership across multiple communities. Most people belong to several communities simultaneously; their identity is constituted by their ability to reconcile these multiple memberships into a coherent, workable self.
6. Social learning systems have two critical boundary mechanisms: **boundary objects** (artifacts, documents, or concepts that are sufficiently plastic to satisfy the interests of different communities) and **brokers** (people who translate across community boundaries and introduce elements of one practice into another).
7. The essay argues for a shift in the unit of organizational learning analysis: from individuals (who learn) and organizations (as containers) to **communities of practice** (as the sites where learning actually occurs) and the connections between them.

## Methodology

Theoretical essay. Develops conceptual arguments from Wenger's broader program of research, drawing on illustrations from organizational settings. This is the journal article form of the conceptual framework most fully elaborated in the 1998 book.

## Relevance

This is the most frequently cited journal article in the CoP literature. It condenses the core framework into a format accessible to organizational learning researchers and managers. The three modes of belonging (engagement, imagination, alignment) are particularly useful for diagnosing why communities fail — a community reliant only on alignment without engagement becomes bureaucratic; one reliant only on imagination without alignment becomes disconnected from reality. For learning designers, this framework suggests a richer set of participation modes to cultivate than simple direct engagement.
