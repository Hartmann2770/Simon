## Citation

Tuan, Y.-F. (1977). *Space and Place: The Perspective of Experience*. University of Minnesota Press.

## Key Findings

1. Tuan's central thesis: **space** and **place** are basic categories of human experience. Space is open, free, threatening; place is secure, familiar, bounded. "If we think of space as that which allows movement, then place is pause; each pause in movement makes it possible for location to be transformed into place."
2. Place is created through experience over time — through dwelling, habituation, and personal or communal history. A location becomes a place when it acquires meaning: a home, a hometown, a sacred site.
3. **Topophilia** — the affective bond between people and their environment — is central to understanding how places become psychologically significant. This bond can be based on familiarity, beauty, memory, or cultural significance.
4. Children develop sense of place through gradually expanding spatial experience: from the intimate proximate environment (home, body) outward to neighborhood, town, and world. Each scale of place has different affective and cognitive character.
5. The contrast between "inside" (familiar, safe, meaningful) and "outside" (unknown, threatening, undifferentiated) is a universal organizing principle of spatial experience.
6. Modern technology and mobility can erode the depth of place-experience, creating a **placeless** world of standardized, interchangeable environments. This has psychological costs.

## Methodology

Humanistic geography. Synthesis of phenomenological philosophy (Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty), environmental psychology, anthropology, and literary examples. No systematic empirical data.

## Relevance

Foundational text for the geographic and phenomenological concept of place. Essential background for understanding Harrison and Dourish's CSCW work, Wahlstedt et al.'s e-learning places, and the broader literature on how digital learning environments need to become "places" rather than merely spaces.
