## Citation

Zhang, J., & Jacob, C. (2012). Reconceptualizing cyberspace: "Real" places in digital space. *The International Journal of Science in Society*, 3(3), 141–154.

## Key Findings

1. The nature of cyberspace is explored through the lens of space and place theory: the article argues that digital environments are not merely simulations of physical spaces but can constitute genuine **places** — environments saturated with social meaning and community.
2. Drawing on Tuan (1977) and Harrison and Dourish (1996), the authors distinguish the **geometrical** conception of cyberspace (network topology, server locations) from the **experiential** conception (the felt sense of being somewhere).
3. Digital environments like virtual worlds can achieve place-ness when they accumulate social history, develop community norms, and become sites of identity formation and belonging — the same processes that make physical environments into places.
4. The article challenges the assumption that cyberspace is inherently less real or less meaningful than physical space: users report deep place attachments to online environments.
5. For education, this reconceptualization matters: an online learning environment that achieves place-ness will support different (and potentially richer) forms of learning than one that remains merely a geometric space for information delivery.

## Methodology

Theoretical and conceptual analysis. Draws on geography, STS, and online community research. No primary empirical data — conceptual argument with illustrative examples.

## Relevance

Bridges spatial theory and digital learning environments. Supports arguments for designing online and e-learning environments as meaningful places rather than information repositories.
