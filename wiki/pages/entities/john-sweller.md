## Overview

John Sweller is an Australian educational psychologist at the University of New South Wales, best known as the originator of **cognitive load theory** (CLT). First articulated in the late 1980s, CLT has become one of the most influential frameworks in instructional design and educational psychology. Sweller's contribution is to have grounded instructional design in a well-specified cognitive architecture — working memory capacity limits and long-term memory schema structure — and to have derived precise, empirically testable design prescriptions from that architecture.

## Key Contributions

1. **Cognitive Load Theory.** Sweller's central contribution is the theoretical framework described in [Sweller (2011)](../sources/sweller-2011-cognitive-load-theory.md): the three-load taxonomy (intrinsic, extraneous, germane), the worked-example effect, the split-attention effect, the modality effect, the redundancy effect, and the expertise reversal effect.

2. **Evolutionary grounding.** A distinctive feature of Sweller's CLT is its grounding in evolutionary psychology: he distinguishes between *biologically primary* knowledge (acquired without instruction through evolution-shaped mechanisms) and *biologically secondary* knowledge (cultural knowledge requiring explicit instruction). This gives CLT a principled account of *why* instruction is needed and *what kind* of cognitive architecture it must accommodate.

3. **The worked-example effect.** One of CLT's most practically important findings: novices learn more from studying worked examples than from solving equivalent problems, because problem-solving consumes working memory on search rather than on schema construction. This has been replicated extensively and is a foundation of direct instruction advocacy.

4. **Instructional design as applied cognitive psychology.** Sweller's programme transformed instructional design from an art guided by intuition into an applied science accountable to cognitive principles. His work created a direct line from laboratory cognitive science to classroom and e-learning design.
