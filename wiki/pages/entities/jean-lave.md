## Overview

Jean Lave (born 1939) is an American social anthropologist and educational theorist, Professor Emerita at the University of California, Berkeley. She is best known for her anthropological research on everyday cognition, situated learning, and the concept of legitimate peripheral participation, which she developed with Etienne Wenger in the landmark 1991 book *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*.

Lave's approach is fundamentally anthropological: she studies learning where it actually occurs — in workplaces, kitchens, markets, and community settings — rather than in laboratory or classroom conditions. Her work has consistently argued that the cognitive psychology tradition studied an impoverished, decontextualized form of cognition, missing the social and material embeddedness that characterizes real human thinking and learning.

Lave's earlier work, particularly *Cognition in Practice* (1988), documented through fieldwork how Liberian tailors and American grocery shoppers used mathematical reasoning in practice settings in ways that diverged systematically from their performance on formal tests — demonstrating that cognition is context-constituted, not context-independent. This work laid the empirical and theoretical groundwork for the *Situated Learning* collaboration with Wenger.

## Key Contributions

**Situated cognition and everyday mathematics.** Through fieldwork with Liberian tailors (with Brent Scribner) and supermarket shoppers, Lave demonstrated that mathematical reasoning in practice does not transfer straightforwardly from one context to another and is much richer and more adaptive in context than formal tests reveal. This was a direct challenge to the transfer assumptions underlying conventional educational psychology.

**Legitimate peripheral participation (with Etienne Wenger, 1991).** The concept of LPP, introduced in [*Situated Learning*](../sources/lave-wenger-1991.md), describes how newcomers learn by being granted legitimate but peripheral membership in a community of practice and progressively moving toward full participation. The five apprenticeship case studies — Yucatec midwives, Vai and Gola tailors, US Navy quartermasters, meat cutters, and AA members — provide the empirical foundation for the concept.

**Communities of practice (with Etienne Wenger, 1991).** Though Wenger developed the CoP framework most extensively in subsequent work, the concept originated in the joint 1991 book, which was the first systematic articulation of learning as participation in communities of practice rather than acquisition of mental content.

**Critique of schooling and transfer.** Lave has been a persistent critic of the decontextualized knowledge practices of formal schooling and the assumption that knowledge learned in school transfers automatically to practice settings. Her work argues that what schools produce are students skilled in school practice — a specific form of participation — which may or may not equip them for the practices they encounter outside school.
