## Overview

**Richard M. Ryan** and **Edward L. Deci** are psychologists at the University of Rochester who developed **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** — one of the most empirically supported and practically influential theories of human motivation. Over four decades of collaborative research, they have produced the theoretical foundations and most of the empirical evidence for SDT, along with a large research community that has extended it across education, healthcare, sport, work, and clinical psychology.

Their central argument: human beings have three basic psychological needs — **autonomy** (the experience of volition), **competence** (the experience of effectiveness), and **relatedness** (the experience of meaningful connection) — and the satisfaction or frustration of these needs by social environments determines the quality and sustainability of motivation.

## Key Contributions

### Self-Determination Theory

SDT is not a single theory but a **family of mini-theories** addressing different aspects of motivation:

- **Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET)**: explains how external events (rewards, feedback, deadlines) affect intrinsic motivation through their effects on perceived autonomy and competence.
- **Organismic Integration Theory (OIT)**: explains the internalization of extrinsic motivation — how behaviors initially done for external reasons become progressively self-determined.
- **Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT)**: specifies the three needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) and their roles in motivation and well-being.
- **Causality Orientations Theory (COT)**: describes individual differences in the tendency to orient toward autonomous, controlled, or impersonal causality.
- **Relationships Motivation Theory (RMT)**: extends the needs framework to close relationships.

### The Motivation Continuum

Ryan and Deci's most influential contribution for practice: replacing the binary intrinsic/extrinsic distinction with a **continuum of relative autonomy**. This shows that extrinsic motivation is not monolithic — behaviors done for external reasons can be more or less autonomous (self-determined) depending on the degree of internalization.

### The Undermining Effect

A major empirical contribution: **contingent rewards for intrinsically interesting activities undermine intrinsic motivation**. This counterintuitive finding — confirmed in hundreds of experiments — has major implications for educational incentive design. The mechanism: rewards shift the perceived locus of causality from internal to external, reducing the sense of autonomy.

### Educational Applications

Ryan and Deci have specifically addressed education: autonomy-supportive teachers (those who provide choice, acknowledge feelings, offer rationale, and minimize pressure) produce students with better learning quality, persistence, and well-being than controlling teachers, even when controlling teachers achieve higher short-term compliance.
