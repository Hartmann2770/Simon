## Overview

Barak Rosenshine (1930–2017) was an educational psychologist at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. He is best known for synthesising decades of classroom research, cognitive science, and observational studies of master teachers into **ten Principles of Instruction** — one of the most widely adopted evidence-based teaching frameworks globally. His work is remarkable for identifying convergence across three independent research traditions: cognitive science, process-product classroom research, and cognitive supports/scaffolding research.

## Key Contributions

1. **Ten Principles of Instruction.** Rosenshine's most influential contribution: a set of practical, evidence-based teaching behaviours documented in [Rosenshine (2012)](../sources/rosenshine-2012-principles-of-instruction.md) and the [original UNESCO pamphlet](../sources/rosenshine-principles-of-instruction-original.md). The principles address pacing, checking for understanding, worked examples, guided and independent practice, scaffolding, and review. Their power comes partly from the convergence of three independent evidence bases on essentially the same prescriptions.

2. **Process-product research.** Rosenshine was a pioneer in studying what effective teachers *do* in classrooms — observing master teachers and correlating their behaviours with student achievement gains. This empirical observation of expertise complements the theoretical prescriptions of cognitive science.

3. **The 80% success-rate heuristic.** A specific and practically actionable finding from Rosenshine's master-teacher research: effective guided practice produces approximately 80% correct student responses — high enough to confirm learning, low enough to confirm the material is genuinely challenging. This benchmark has been widely adopted in teacher development.

4. **Bridging cognitive science and classroom practice.** Rosenshine's synthesis makes the connection between laboratory cognitive science (especially working memory limits and retrieval practice) and everyday teaching decisions explicit and practical. His principles are essentially CLT and retrieval-practice research translated into teacher actions.
