## Overview

Robert A. Bjork is a Distinguished Research Professor of Psychology at UCLA and one of the most influential theorists in the science of learning and memory. He is best known for two contributions: (1) the New Theory of Disuse (with Elizabeth Bjork), which provides the mechanistic explanation for why spacing, retrieval practice, and other "difficult" conditions enhance long-term retention; and (2) the concept of "desirable difficulties," which he coined and developed to describe learning conditions that appear to impede short-term performance but demonstrably improve long-term retention and transfer. Bjork has been a persistent advocate for applying cognitive science to education and training.

## Key Contributions

1. **New Theory of Disuse (1992).** With Elizabeth Bjork, he proposed that memory has two independent dimensions: *storage strength* (depth of encoding, which only increases) and *retrieval strength* (current accessibility, which fluctuates). Forgetting reflects retrieval strength decline, not storage loss. Retrieval benefits learning most when retrieval strength is low — explaining why spacing and testing work ([bjork-bjork-1992](../sources/bjork-bjork-1992.md)).

2. **Desirable Difficulties (1994).** In a landmark chapter ([bjork-1994](../sources/bjork-1994.md)), Bjork coined the term "desirable difficulties" and catalogued conditions — spacing, interleaving, reduced feedback, testing — that make learning harder in the short term but more durable in the long term. He identified the systematic metacognitive failure that leads learners and trainers to prefer ineffective, easy conditions.

3. **Memory and metamemory in training.** The 1994 chapter explicitly addressed the gap between training performance and post-training retention, arguing that training systems optimised for performance during acquisition are systematically miscalibrated. This critique is foundational for applied learning design.

4. **Transfer-appropriate processing and encoding variability.** Bjork's theoretical work also encompasses the conditions under which knowledge transfers to new contexts — a complement to his work on retention.

5. **Advocacy for evidence-based learning.** Bjork has been one of the most prominent voices arguing that the science of learning must be translated into educational practice, and that practitioners systematically undervalue the strategies (spacing, retrieval, interleaving) that research consistently endorses.
