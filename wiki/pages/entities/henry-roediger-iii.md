## Overview

Henry L. Roediger III is an American cognitive psychologist and the James S. McDonnell Distinguished University Professor at Washington University in St. Louis. He is one of the foremost researchers on human memory, with particular expertise in the testing effect, retrieval practice, collective memory, and false memory (the DRM paradigm). Together with Jeffrey Karpicke, he produced the most influential modern empirical and review work on how testing enhances long-term retention — work that is widely credited with bringing retrieval practice into mainstream educational discourse.

## Key Contributions

1. **Test-enhanced learning (2006).** Roediger and Karpicke's 2006 *Psychological Science* paper ([roediger-karpicke-2006b](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006b.md)) provided a clean, elegant empirical demonstration that taking free-recall tests on prose passages produced far better one-week retention than restudying, even without feedback. This paper is among the most-cited in the testing-effect literature and is widely credited with igniting the modern applied wave of research.

2. **The Power of Testing Memory review (2006).** The companion *Perspectives on Psychological Science* review ([roediger-karpicke-2006a](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006a.md)) synthesised decades of basic research and made the case for frequent testing in educational settings, reaching a broad psychological audience.

3. **Critical role of retrieval practice (2011).** With Andrew Butler, Roediger published an influential *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* review ([roediger-butler-2011](../sources/roediger-butler-2011.md)) that positioned retrieval practice as the single most powerful learning strategy and articulated mechanisms including transfer-appropriate processing and elaborative retrieval.

4. **Ten benefits of testing (2011).** With Putnam and Smith, Roediger catalogued ten distinct mechanisms by which testing benefits learning ([roediger-putnam-smith-2011](../sources/roediger-putnam-smith-2011.md)) — moving the field beyond a single retention effect to a comprehensive account of testing's educational value.

5. **Repeated retrieval research (2007).** The Karpicke & Roediger (2007) *Journal of Memory and Language* study ([karpicke-roediger-2007](../sources/karpicke-roediger-2007.md)) quantified the extraordinary advantage (>100%) of repeated retrieval over repeated studying for long-term retention.
