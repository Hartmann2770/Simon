## Overview

**Dr. Philippa Hardman** is a UK-based instructional designer, researcher, and entrepreneur working at the intersection of learning science and artificial intelligence. She developed the **DOMS (Design of Meaningful and Systematic)** learning design engine, an AI-powered platform that attempts to operationalize evidence-based learning science in the AI-assisted creation of learning content. She writes the "Dr Phil's Newsletter, Powered by DOMS AI" and has given TEDx talks on AI and education.

Her central argument, carried across multiple works: the AI revolution in education will only materialize if AI tools are designed around evidence-based learning science. AI tools that replicate existing bad practices faster (more content dumps, more rote assessment) will not improve learning; AI tools designed with sound pedagogical foundations can transform learning at scale.

## Key Contributions

### Learning-Science-First AI Design

Hardman consistently argues that the question "how do we use AI for education?" must be preceded by the question "what does good learning design look like?" Her DOMS platform represents an attempt to encode evidence-based learning design decisions into AI tool architecture, so that AI assistance produces pedagogically sound outputs rather than merely plausible-sounding content.

### AI as Accelerator and Risk

Hardman's research into AI's impact on instructional design (2024) shows a nuanced picture: AI can accelerate the design workflow significantly, but acceleration does not automatically equal quality improvement. The critical variable is whether the AI system is designed with learning science foundations. Without them, AI produces faster but weaker learning experiences.

### Structured Prompting for Educators

Hardman has developed and advocated for structured prompting frameworks (notably CRAFT: Context, Role, Action, Format, Target audience) specifically for educational purposes. She argues that **prompt engineering is a pedagogical skill**, not a technical one: effective prompting of AI for educational content requires deep knowledge of learning science and learner context.

### The AI Education Revolution

In her TEDx talk (2023), Hardman provides a historical framing: previous edtech "revolutions" (MOOCs, interactive whiteboards) failed to transform education because they replicated existing models rather than reconceiving them. Generative AI is qualitatively different — it enables genuine personalization at scale — but only if deployed in service of evidence-based learning design.
