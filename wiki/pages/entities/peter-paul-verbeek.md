## Overview

**Peter-Paul Verbeek** is a Dutch philosopher of technology at the University of Twente (Netherlands), known for his work in **postphenomenology** — the philosophical study of how technologies mediate human experience. His primary contribution is the development and application of Don Ihde's postphenomenological framework to design ethics, arguing that technological artifacts are never neutral but always active mediators that shape what humans perceive, do, and experience.

Verbeek has held academic and policy roles including membership of the Dutch Scientific Council for Government Policy. His work bridges academic philosophy and practical technology design.

## Key Contributions

### What Things Do

Verbeek's major theoretical work, *What Things Do* (2005), systematically develops the postphenomenological analysis of human-technology relations. He refines and extends Ihde's taxonomy of human-technology relations (embodiment, interpretation, alterity, background) and argues for a **design ethics** based on this analysis: designers must take responsibility for the mediating roles their artifacts will play, since design choices inevitably script human behavior and experience.

### Technology as Moral Agent

Following Latour, Verbeek argues that technologies function as **moral agents** — not in the sense that they have intentions, but in the sense that they inscribe values, shape choices, and produce moral consequences. A speed bump is a moral agent: it enforces compliance without requiring anyone's will. Educational technologies similarly enforce certain pedagogical values whether or not designers intend this.

### Postphenomenological Design Ethics

Verbeek's most distinctive contribution to practice: a **postphenomenological approach to design ethics** that asks designers to:
1. Analyze what human-technology relation their artifact will create
2. Identify what this relation makes visible and what it conceals
3. Take responsibility for the values inscribed in the design
4. Design reflexively, with awareness of the mediating effects being created
