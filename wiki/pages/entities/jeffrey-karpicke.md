## Overview

Jeffrey D. Karpicke is a cognitive psychologist and Professor of Psychology at Purdue University, where he directs the Memory and Cognition Laboratory. His research focuses on retrieval-based learning — how the act of retrieving information from memory enhances long-term retention and meaningful learning. Karpicke is widely regarded as the leading contemporary researcher on retrieval practice and testing effects, with contributions spanning empirical studies, theoretical accounts (particularly the episodic context account), and translation of science to educational practice.

## Key Contributions

1. **Test-enhanced learning (2006).** As Roediger's PhD student, Karpicke co-authored the landmark 2006 *Psychological Science* paper ([roediger-karpicke-2006b](../sources/roediger-karpicke-2006b.md)) demonstrating the testing effect with prose passages. The study's clean design and educationally relevant materials helped propel testing-effect research into mainstream awareness.

2. **Repeated retrieval as the key to retention (2007).** The *Journal of Memory and Language* study ([karpicke-roediger-2007](../sources/karpicke-roediger-2007.md)) isolated the contribution of repeated retrieval by comparing continued testing versus continued studying of already-recalled items. The >100% retention advantage for repeated retrieval over continued study remains one of the most striking quantitative results in memory research.

3. **Retrieval-based learning review (2015).** With Nunes, Karpicke provided a comprehensive review of retrieval-based learning research ([nunes-karpicke-2015](../sources/nunes-karpicke-2015.md)), covering historical origins, foundational studies, and contemporary theoretical accounts.

4. **Episodic context account (2017).** Work with Whiffen ([whiffen-karpicke-2017](../sources/whiffen-karpicke-2017.md)) provided direct empirical support for the episodic context theory of retrieval practice: the requirement to reinstate the original learning context drives retrieval's beneficial effects, producing temporal organisation in subsequent recall.

5. **Meaningful learning through retrieval.** A distinctive thread in Karpicke's later work is demonstrating that retrieval practice promotes not just verbatim retention but meaningful, transferable understanding — challenging the caricature of testing as a rote-memorisation tool.
