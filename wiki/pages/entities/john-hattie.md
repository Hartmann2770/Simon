## Overview

John Hattie is a New Zealand educational researcher, primarily associated with the University of Melbourne, best known for his *Visible Learning* programme — one of the largest ever syntheses of educational research. Through meta-analyses of over 1,600 studies, Hattie ranks hundreds of educational interventions by effect size, providing practitioners with an evidence-based map of what works in education. His work on feedback and learning strategies has become foundational in teacher professional development globally.

## Key Contributions

1. **Visible Learning.** Hattie's monumental synthesis (published as a book in 2009 and extended since) aggregates hundreds of meta-analyses to produce effect-size estimates for educational interventions ranging from class size to feedback to study strategies. The average effect size across interventions is d = 0.40, which Hattie uses as a benchmark: interventions below 0.40 are below average; those above are substantially effective.

2. **The Power of Feedback.** [Hattie & Timperley (2007)](../sources/hattie-timperley-2007-power-of-feedback.md) provide the most influential conceptual framework for feedback in education. The three-question model (Where am I going? How am I going? Where to next?) and the four-level taxonomy (task, process, self-regulation, self) are now standard references in teacher education. Feedback emerges as one of the highest-effect influences on learning — but only when delivered at the right level.

3. **Learning Strategies Synthesis.** [Hattie & Donoghue (2016)](../sources/hattie-donoghue-2016-learning-strategies.md) synthesise 228 meta-analyses on learning strategies and propose a phase-sensitive model: strategies effective at the surface-learning phase differ from those effective at deep learning and transfer. This qualifies simpler claims that certain strategies (e.g., retrieval practice) are universally best.

4. **Surface, Deep, and Transfer Learning.** Hattie's learning model distinguishes surface learning (acquiring initial knowledge base) from deep learning (integrating and connecting concepts) and transfer (applying learning to new contexts). This tripartite model has been widely adopted in curriculum design.
