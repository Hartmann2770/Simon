## Overview

Aleksei Nikolaevich Leont'ev (1903–1979) was a Soviet psychologist and one of Vygotsky's closest students and collaborators. After Vygotsky's death in 1934, Leont'ev became the central figure in developing and systematizing the cultural-historical tradition, producing what is now called the second generation of activity theory. He directed the Psychology Department at Moscow State University for decades and trained generations of Soviet psychologists.

Where Vygotsky focused primarily on semiotic mediation, consciousness, and the zone of proximal development, Leont'ev shifted the fundamental unit of analysis from mediated action to activity — the full, motive-driven, socially embedded process of human engagement with the world. This move was decisive for the subsequent development of the field.

## Key Contributions

**The three-level hierarchical structure of activity.** Leont'ev's most influential theoretical contribution is the distinction between:
- *Activity* — the most comprehensive level; driven by a motive; typically collective and enduring (e.g., the activity of education, of medicine, of play).
- *Action* — an individual, goal-directed component of an activity. The same goal can be motivated by different activities, and the same activity can be served by different actions.
- *Operation* — a habitualized, automatic sub-component of an action; executed without conscious deliberation in response to conditions. Operations form through the routinization of previously conscious actions.

The key insight is that these levels are dynamic: through practice, actions become operations (skill acquisition); through disruption, operations surface back to conscious actions (breakdown). This explains how expertise develops and why errors occur.

**Activity, motive, and consciousness.** Leont'ev argued that consciousness is constituted through activity: what you are conscious of, and how richly, depends on what you are engaged in doing and what motives structure that doing. Alienation — the Marxist concept — is reinterpreted psychologically: when actions are structurally disconnected from meaningful motives, consciousness becomes impoverished.

**The object of activity.** Leont'ev clarified the concept of the "object" (predmet): not a physical thing but the culturally defined purpose that gives an activity its direction. The same physical object (a book) is a different "object of activity" for a scholar (knowledge), a collector (aesthetic possession), and a child doing homework (demonstrating compliance). Identifying the true object of an activity — what it is really about — is the key analytical task.

**Personality as activity structure.** For Leont'ev, personality is not an inner property but is constituted by the hierarchy of motives organizing a person's activities over time. Studying personality means studying which activities a person is engaged in and how their motives are organized relative to one another.

**Development and leading activity.** In *Problems of the Development of the Mind*, Leont'ev introduced the concept of "leading activity" (vedushchaya deyatelnost): the activity that most fully drives development at a given life stage. The leading activity for a developmental period is the one that reorganizes all other activities around it — not necessarily the most frequent activity. This grounds developmental stage theory in the social organization of activity rather than in biological maturation.
