## Overview

Richard E. Mayer is a cognitive psychologist at the University of California, Santa Barbara, and one of the most influential researchers in educational psychology. He is best known for developing the **Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning** (CTML) — a framework that explains how people learn from words and pictures and provides evidence-based principles for designing multimedia instructional materials. Mayer's work builds on and extends Sweller's cognitive load theory into the specific context of media-based instruction.

## Key Contributions

1. **Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML).** Mayer's central theoretical contribution. CTML posits three assumptions: dual channels (auditory and visual processing systems operate semi-independently), limited capacity (each channel has limited working memory), and active processing (learning requires selecting, organising, and integrating information). These assumptions generate specific, testable predictions about multimedia design.

2. **Twelve multimedia design principles.** Mayer's most practically impactful contribution is his evidence-based set of design principles (coherence, signalling, redundancy, spatial contiguity, temporal contiguity, segmenting, pre-training, modality, multimedia, personalisation, voice, and image). Each principle is backed by systematic experimental evidence and directly actionable by designers. See [Mayer & Moreno](../sources/mayer-moreno-cognitive-load-multimedia.md) for the nine-way synthesis and Mayer's book *Multimedia Learning* for the full twelve.

3. **Translating cognitive science to practice.** A sustained feature of Mayer's work is translating laboratory findings into design guidance accessible to practitioners. [Mayer (2010)](../sources/mayer-2010-science-of-learning-medical-education.md) demonstrates this for medical education, showing how CTML applies in professional training contexts.

4. **Science of learning applied to e-learning.** Mayer's framework is the dominant evidence base for e-learning design, providing designers with explicit reasons for design decisions (not merely style preferences) rooted in cognitive psychology.
